Abstract 

Large language models improve final-answer accuracy through extended chain-of-thought reasoning, but often spend tokens inefficiently and offer little inference-time control. Existing efficient reasoning methods control thinking length by shortening, early-stopping, or com-pressing traces, leaving how the model thinks implicit. In this paper, we propose Agentic Chain-of-Thought Steering (ACTS), which for-mulates reasoning steering as a Markov deci-sion process where a controller agent adaptively steers a frozen reasoner during inference. At each step, the controller observes the reasoning trace and remaining thinking budget, then is-sues a steering action consisting of a reasoning strategy and a steering phrase that initiates the next reasoner step. This enables budget-aware strategy control for efficient reasoning while preserving the reasoner’s generation continu-ity. We initialize the controller agent from our constructed synthetic steering trajectories with multi-budget augmentation, and further opti-mize it via reinforcement learning with budget-conditioned reward shaping. Experiments across multiple benchmarks show that ACTS matches full-thinking performance with sub-stantial token savings, and enables controllable accuracy-efficiency trade-offs across different reasoners and tasks. 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) improve final an-swer accuracy by spending test-time computation on extended chain-of-thought reasoning (Wei et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2025). However, long reasoning traces often contain redundant derivations, repeated self-checks, and delayed termination, making rea-soning expensive and difficult to control (Feng et al., 2025; Sui et al., 2025a). Recent efficient rea-soning methods reduce this cost by shortening (Ag-garwal and Welleck, 2025; Hou et al., 2026), com-pressing (Xia et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025a; Xu et al., 2026), early-stopping (Yang et al., 2026; Liu and Wang, 2025), or budget-forcing (Muennighoff et al., 2025; Wen et al., 2025) the generated chain-of-thought thinking trace. While effective, these methods mainly control how long the model thinks, leaving how it thinks largely implicit. A reasoning trace is not a uni-form token sequence: it consists of functional steps such as setting up the problem, carrying out deriva-tions, switching to alternatives, verifying results, and deciding when to stop. Recent studies identify structured transitions between such behaviors, e.g., planning, execution, reflection, and conclusion, in long reasoning traces (Li et al., 2025b; Xiong et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026). This motivates a different control surface for efficient reasoning: steering the stepwise reasoning behavior itself, rather than im-posing only a global length constraint. In this paper, we propose Agentic Chain-of-Thought Steering (ACTS), a framework for effi-cient and controllable LLM reasoning. We formu-late reasoning steering under budget as a Markov decision process in which a controller agent guides a frozen reasoner step by step. At each step, the controller observes the steering history, current rea-soning step, and remaining thinking budget, then se-lects a steering action consisting of a high-level rea-soning strategy and a short natural-language steer-ing phrase that initiates the next reasoner step. This design enables reasoner-agnostic in-flight strategy control while preserving the continuity of the rea-soner’s thinking process. To initialize the controller, we need expert steer-ing trajectories that specify the desired strategy and steering phrase at each reasoning step. Existing expert traces, however, contain only the reasoner’s chain of thought, without explicit controller actions. We therefore synthesize steering trajectories by seg-menting each trace, annotating the implied strategy, and extracting the step-opening phrase as the steer-ing phrase. 2 Related Work 

2.1 Efficient and Budget-Aware Reasoning 

A growing body of work seeks to reduce the cost of long chain-of-thought reasoning. Prompt-level approaches encourage concise traces via brevity instructions, token-budget prompts, or minimal in-formative drafts (Nayab et al., 2024; Han et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2025). Tuning-based methods learn length-controllable variants of CoT through supervised compression of reasoning traces (Xia et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2025b; Zhang et al., 2025a), while RL-based methods train reasoners to satisfy explicit length constraints with length-aware re-wards (Luo et al., 2025; Hou et al., 2026; Aggar-wal and Welleck, 2025; He et al., 2025). Comple-mentary inference-time methods directly truncate or guide generation via budget forcing or budget-aware control signals (Muennighoff et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025a; Wen et al., 2025), or via early-exit once the model is confident in a trial answer (Yang et al., 2026; Liu and Wang, 2025; Jiang et al., 2025). While effective, these methods primarily control how long the model thinks, leaving the underlying reasoning strategy implicit. ACTS is complemen-tary: it steers how the model thinks at each step through explicit reasoning strategies and steering phrases, with budget compliance arising from the learned completion rather than enforced length. 

2.2 Reasoning Steering and Thinking Control 

Beyond length, a separate line of work intervenes directly in the reasoning process to control its be-havior. Prompt-level steering inserts strategic in-structions before the thinking trace to elicit specific reasoning behaviors (Li et al., 2026). Activation steering extracts intervention vectors in the model’s hidden representations to amplify or suppress cog-nitive behaviors such as reflection and backtracking at inference time (Chen et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025b; Venhoff et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2026). A 2third thread trains explicit controller models that select reasoning structures or strategies at inference time via reinforcement learning (Hao et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025a). Closely related, meta-cognitive frameworks place a higher-level reasoner above the generator to adapt strategies during inference (Xi-ang et al., 2025; Sui et al., 2025b). Analyses also identify recurring functional structure in long rea-soning traces (Li et al., 2025b; Xiong et al., 2025), motivating control surfaces aligned with these ob-served behaviors. ACTS unifies these threads un-der a budgeted setting: it learns a reasoner-agnostic controller agent that emits an explicit strategy and a free-form steering phrase at each step, optimized via reinforcement learning with budget-conditioned reward shaping that jointly captures answer correct-ness and budget utilization. 

3 Agentic Chain-of-Thought Steering 

In this section, we introduce our ACTS framework. We first formalize the reasoning steering task in Section 3.1, then describe the two-stage controller agent training in Sections 3.2 and 3.3. 3.1 Reasoning Steering as an MDP 

We formulate reasoning steering under budget as a Markov decision process (MDP). Given a question 

x ∈ X and a thinking-token budget B ∈ N+, the goal is to produce a final answer ˆy via controller-steered reasoner generation. We denote the con-troller agent policy by πθ and the frozen rea-soner by ρ, and initialize the steering history as 

H0 = ( x, b 0), where bt is the fraction of the bud-get remaining at step t and b0 = 100% . Following prior work on chain-of-thought segmentation (Yang et al., 2025; Ling et al., 2026), we define a reason-ing step as a self-contained semantic unit such as calculations and verifications delimited by para-graph boundaries, e.g., ".\n\n" and "?\n\n" . We write the t-th reasoner step as zt and the thinking trace through step t as z≤t = z1 ◦ · · · ◦ zt.

State and Action. At step t, the controller ob-serves the full steering history Ht−1 as the state, which records previous controller actions, reasoner steps, and remaining budgets. It samples a steering action at = ( ut, p t) ∼ πθ(· | Ht−1), where ut ∈ U 

is a high-level reasoning strategy from a prede-fined set and pt is a free-form natural-language steering phrase that primes the reasoner’s next step toward ut. We follow taxonomies of reasoning be-havior (Baron, 1986; Li et al., 2025b, 2026) and instantiate the strategy set as U = {UNDERSTAND ,

“Alternatively, suppose” for EXPLORE . This decou-ples what strategy to take from how to enter it linguistically, preserving continuity with the rea-soner’s natural generation style. A more detailed instantiation is provided in Appendix A. 

Transition. Conditioned on the question x, the current thinking trace z<t , and the controller-emitted steering phrase pt that enacts the selected strategy ut, the reasoner generates a continuation 

st ∼ ρ(· | x, z <t , p t), yielding the next reasoner step zt = pt ◦ st. We then update the remaining-budget fraction bt = bt−1 − ℓ(zt)/B and steering history Ht = ( Ht−1, a t, z t, b t), where ℓ(·) counts thinking tokens. The episode terminates when the controller selects CONCLUDE , the reasoner emits an end-of-thinking token (e.g., </think> ), or a maximum step count is reached. 

Terminal Reward. After the thinking episode terminates at step T , we run an answer-generation step with the reasoner conditioned on the com-pleted thinking trace z≤T and extract the final answer ˆy. 3.3 Online Reinforcement Learning 

To optimize for answer accuracy and budget com-pliance, we further train the controller agent with reinforcement learning. However, simply apply-ing an over-budget penalty to the correctness re-ward creates a perverse incentive: the controller can avoid the penalty by terminating early on questions it has not yet solved, trading correctness for bud-get compliance. Therefore, we instantiate f (c, b T )

with budget-conditioned reward shaping that penal-izes both overthinking and premature termination. 

Budget-Conditioned Reward Shaping. We de-sign a reward that targets two scenarios: (i) pro-4ducing an answer while overspending the budget, signaling overthinking, and (ii) terminating with an incorrect answer while leaving budget underuti-lized, signaling premature termination on an un-solved question. As shown in Figure 3, the reward for a correct answer decays from 1 to 1 − α as the over-budget excess grows, while the reward for an incorrect answer decays from 0 to −α as either the over-budget excess or the underused budget grows. In practice, we additionally apply a 10% grace margin around bT = 0 so that minor over- or under-shoots near the budget boundary do not trigger the penalty.

3.4 Asynchronous Inference-Time Steering 

While ACTS enables adaptive reasoning steer-ing, the controller-reasoner architecture adds serv-ing overhead at inference-time deployment of our trained controller agent. Specifically, each sam-ple alternates calls between the controller agent πθ

and the reasoner ρ over many turns, and serializing these calls naively would inflate per-sample latency and degrade rollout throughput. We address this with an asynchronous two-server pipeline, where the controller agent and the reasoner are hosted as separate SGLang (Zheng et al., 2024) servers, and the per-sample MDP loop is driven by an orchestra-tor over asynchronous HTTP. Since the two servers operate independently, all in-flight samples ad-vance concurrently at request level, amortizing per-call latency while maintaining aggregate through-put. We empirically show in Section 5.5 that our async pipeline achieves inference latency compara-ble to, or even better than, direct reasoner batched generation, despite the additional controller calls. 

4 Experimental Setup 

4.1 Datasets and Evaluation 

As detailed in Section 3.2, we construct synthetic steering trajectories for controller agent SFT from OpenR1-Math corpus (Hugging Face, 2025), from which we sample 7,500 questions whose expert thinking traces are between 512 and 8,192 tokens long. The same question set is reused for online RL in Section 3.3. We evaluate our ACTS frame-work on four math reasoning benchmarks MATH-500 (Hendrycks et al., 2021), AIME24 (Mathe-matical Association of America, 2024), AMC (LI et al., 2024) including both AMC12 2022 and 2023, and the math subset of OlympiadBench (He et al., 2024), and also a science QA benchmark GPQA Diamond (Rein et al., 2024) to test whether our trained controller agent can generalize to non-math domains. We report Accuracy by answer correct-ness and total #Tokens including both thinking and answer generation counted by the reasoner tokenizer as our main metrics. For our ACTS, #Tokens includes both controller and reasoner to-kens. We run multiple seeded repeats and report per-sample averages on small-size benchmarks fol-lowing (Yang et al., 2026): 5 repeats on AIME24 and AMC, and 3 repeats on GPQA Diamond. We use three reasoning models DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-{1.5B,7B } (Guo et al., 2025), and Qwen3-8B (Team, 2025) as the reasoners in our evaluation.

4.3 Implementation Details 

We instantiate the controller agent on Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 (Team, 2025). We train the controller via supervised finetuning on the constructed syn-thetic steering trajectories with learning rate 1e −5,global batch size 64 . We further refine the con-troller via GRPO with learning rate 1e −6, group size G=8 , rollout batch size 32 , train batch size 

64 , penalty coefficient α=0 .5. During RL training, we pair the controller with DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B (Guo et al., 2025) as the frozen reasoner. The controller is sampled with temperature 1.0 and top-p 0.9, while the reasoner uses greedy decod-ing with temperature 0.0 so that reward variance is attributable to controller decisions. During eval-uation, we set the controller temperature 0.7 and top-p 0.8, and reasoner temperature 0.6, top-p 0.95 ,and max new tokens 32 ,768 following Yang et al. (2026) for all compared baselines. Controller SFT training uses OpenRLHF (Hu et al., 2024) and RL training is implemented with SLIME (Zhu et al., 2025). At inference, the controller and reasoner are served as separate SGLang (Zheng et al., 2024) servers communicating over asynchronous HTTP as detailed in Section 3.4. The system prompts used by the controller and the reasoner are provided in Appendix C. All experiments run on 8×A100 80GB GPUs.

5.2 ACTS enables controllable accuracy vs. efficiency trade-offs 

As shown in Figure 4, we sweep the ACTS think-ing budget from tight to near-Vanilla scale and plot accuracy against total tokens for all three reasoners and five benchmarks. The DeepSeek-R1-Distill-1.5B curves on AIME and GPQA also show a mild accuracy reversal at very large budgets. Af-ter analyzing the logged steering trajectories, we attribute this to the controller invoking an increas-ing number of EXPLORE actions (e.g., from 25% to 

42% of all actions on GPQA as the budget triples) that the weak reasoner cannot sustain coherently, indicating that the budget signal itself plays a useful regularizing role for limited-capacity reasoners. 

5.3 Our trained controller agent generalizes to different reasoners and tasks 

Our controller agent is trained only on synthetic steering trajectories derived from DeepSeek-R1 math reasoning, yet it transfers cleanly to an out-of-model-family reasoner, e.g., Qwen3-8B, and to out-of-domain science QA task as shown in Table 1 and Figure 4. Notably, ACTS achieves its largest gains on GPQA Diamond with the DeepSeek rea-soners, improving over Vanilla by up to +11 .9pp at substantially lower token cost despite never hav-ing seen science problems during training. By comparing Vanilla and controller-steered reason-ing traces on GPQA, we attribute this transfer to a domain-agnostic failure mode of the unguided reasoners: their wrong attempts tend to run substan-tially longer than their correct ones, indicating that the reasoners frequently spiral into long, confused traces. Our controller agent’s structured steering, drawing on the domain-agnostic strategy set U de-fined in Section 3.1, directly counteracts this over-thinking by guiding the reasoner through discrete reasoning phases regardless of task content.

5.5 Async controller-reasoner inference incurs negligible latency overhead 

One practical deployment concern of our ACTS framework is inference latency, since the controller-reasoner architecture introduces additional con-troller calls on top of reasoner generation. To mea-sure how our asynchronous two-server pipeline in Section 3.4 addresses this concern, we bench-mark end-to-end token throughput (#Tokens per second) on MATH-500 against the Vanilla and DEER baselines. All three compared methods are implemented with SGLang inference engine for a fair comparison, and share the same 8-GPU com-pute budget: Vanilla and DEER use all 8 GPUs for the reasoner, while ACTS splits them into 4+4 be-tween the controller and reasoner. As shown in Fig-ure 6, ACTS matches Vanilla’s throughput within 

1% on Qwen3-8B and within 11% on DeepSeek-R1-Distill-7B, indicating that asynchronous serv-ing effectively amortizes the controller-reasoner round trips. In contrast, DEER reaches only about half of Vanilla’s throughput on both reasoners, as its iterative probe-and-resume cycles add overhead that batched serving cannot hide. 

6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we present Agentic Chain-of-Thought Steering (ACTS), a framework that formulates rea-soning steering as a controller-reasoner Markov decision process under a thinking-token budget. At each step, the controller observes the steering his-tory and remaining budget, and emits a reasoning strategy and a steering phrase that initiates the next reasoner step. We train it in two stages: behavior initialization from our constructed synthetic steer-ing trajectories with multi-budget augmentation, followed by reinforcement learning with budget-conditioned reward shaping that asymmetrically penalizes overthinking and premature termination. Experiments show that ACTS matches or outper-8forms full-thinking baselines with substantial token savings, enables controllable accuracy-efficiency trade-offs, and generalizes to different reasoners and task domains. Our results on strategy-level steering offer a complementary control surface for efficient reasoning beyond global length control. 

Limitations 

While ACTS demonstrates effective and control-lable reasoning steering, our study has several limi-tations. First, our experiments cover three reason-ers ranging up to 8B parameters from two model families. Whether the same controller can steer substantially larger reasoners, such as 70 B-scale open-weight models or frontier proprietary reason-ers, is beyond our compute budget for evaluation. While our analysis indicates that the controller ex-ploits relatively domain- and model-agnostic fail-ure modes such as overthinking, we leave direct validation at larger scales for future investigation. Second, ACTS assumes that the thinking-token budget is supplied at inference time by the user or the service provider. This matches deployment settings where budgets are externally constrained by latency targets or cost ceilings, while a fully au-tonomous variant would infer a problem-difficulty-appropriate budget on its own. We leave the design of such an adaptive budget mechanism, e.g., via a difficulty estimator or a learned early-confidence signal, for future work.

B Steering Trajectory Construction 

We construct synthetic steering trajectories from OpenR1-Math (Hugging Face, 2025) with reason-ing traces produced by DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025). To cover a broad range of trace lengths, we length-balance the source by sampling 2,000 traces uniformly across 512-token bands over [512 , 8192] 

trace tokens. Each trace is segmented into rea-soning steps at paragraph boundaries that end in sentence-final punctuation ( .\n\n or ?\n\n ). 

Action annotation and extraction. For each step, we use Qwen3-32B as an LLM annotator that jointly (i) classifies the step into one of seven strategies {UNDERSTAND , PLAN , EXECUTE , EX -

> PLORE

, CHECK , SUMMARIZE , CONCLUDE } and (ii) extracts the step’s opening as steering phrase. The annotator prompt is provided in Figure 7. We use temperature 0.7, top-p 0.8, and top-k 20 and the annotator returns a JSON object which we parse directly into the strategy ut and steering phrase pt.

Trajectory assembling. Each annotated trace is assembled into a multi-turn dialog matching the controller’s chat format at inference time, with the controller system prompt (Figure 9) as the system message and the annotator’s Action / Starter pair as the assistant target at each turn. Figure 8 shows a worked example from our SFT corpus. During SFT, the loss is computed only on assistant tokens. 

Trajectory filtering. After assembling steering trajectories, we apply two filters to suppress degen-erate looping behavior. First, consecutive same-strategy runs in each trajectory are capped at 5

to discourage repetitive sequences. Second, tra-jectories that contain 5 or more identical opening steering phrases or exhibit more than 5 alternating-strategy cycles are dropped from the corpus.
