## VistaHop: Benchmarking Multi-hop Visual Reasoning for Visual DeepSearch 

Hang He 1,2,3, Chuhuai Yue 2, Chengqi Dong 2, Chengcheng Wan 1,3,†, Ting Su 1, Haiying Sun 1,Jiajun Chai 2, Xiaohan Wang 2, Guojun Yin 2,†

1East China Normal University 2Meituan 3Shanghai Innovation Institute 

Abstract 

Visual DeepSearch tasks require multimodal large reasoning model (MLRM) agents to an-swer complex visual queries by repeatedly in-specting image regions, grounding reasoning in visual evidence, and connecting fine-grained clues across multiple steps. However, exist-ing benchmarks mainly evaluate single-step vi-sual understanding or isolated image-question answering. They have limited task difficulty, shallow reasoning hops, and one-shot image inspection, and thus fail to evaluate the ability to iteratively revisit visual evidence and reason across multiple steps. In this work, we introduce V ISTA HOP , a com-prehensive benchmark for Visual DeepSearch ,designed to evaluate vision-centric search and multi-hop visual reasoning that requires mod-els to inspect images multiple times and con-nect evidence across different visual regions. VISTA HOP comprises 300 images, 25 visual search scenarios, and 350 multi-hop QA tasks. We also propose V ISTA ARENA , a unified eval-uation environment that supports tool-based interactions, including visual retrieval, im-age inspection, and evidence-grounded rea-soning. Experiments show that even state-of-the-art MLRMs remain far from solving VISTA HOP , with the best model SenseNova-MARS-32B achieving only 24.31% Pass@1. These findings highlight the importance of specialized benchmarks and training meth-ods for Visual DeepSearch . Our code and dataset are available at https://github.com/ ahang0712/vistahop .

Unlike text-dominant agentic search, Visual DeepSearch places repeated visual evidence seeking and region-level grounding at the center of the reasoning process. However, existing visual benchmarks are insuffi-cient for evaluating Visual DeepSearch . Visual perception is often front-loaded, used only initially while reasoning proceeds via language without revisiting visual evidence. Their tasks tend to have limited search depth and dif-ficulty, allowing end-to-end answers rather than multi-step evidence chains. Moreover, some datasets suffer from shortcut reasoning or knowl-edge leakage, where answers can be inferred from textual cues, prior knowledge, or memorized asso-ciations without genuine visual search. We further develop VISTA ARENA , a unified evaluation framework for MLRMs under direct an-swering, search-augmented reasoning, and multi-anchor reasoning settings. 2 Related Work 

2.1 Visual DeepSearch 

Recent multimodal search and agentic reason-ing studies, such as MMSearch (Jiang et al., 2025b), MMSearch-R1 (Wu et al., 2025), DeepMMSearch-R1 (Narayan et al., 2025), Vision-DeepResearch (Huang et al., 2026b), Web-Watcher (Geng et al., 2026), MTA-Agent (Peng et al., 2026), OpenSearch-VL (Chen et al., 2026), ProMMSearchAgent (Yan et al., 2026), and Hy-perEyes (Li et al., 2026a), show a clear shift from passive image understanding to active visual evi-dence seeking. This emerging setting places visual evidence collection, region-level grounding, and multi-hop visual reasoning at the center of the search pro-cess. Recent region-level and tool-augmented vi-sual reasoning methods, including VLM-R 3 (Jiang et al., 2025a), Chain-of-Focus (Zhang et al., 2025), TikArt (Ding et al., 2026), ToolsRL (Dong et al., 2026b), Pixelis (Zhou, 2026), and InSight-o3 (Li et al., 2025b), further demonstrate the importance of iterative visual inspection, adaptive zooming, and tool-based local evidence acquisition. Related benchmarks and agents, such as AgentVista (Su et al., 2026), VTC-Bench (Zhu et al., 2026b), TIR-Bench (Li et al., 2025c), and O3-Bench (Li et al., 2025b), also highlight the need to evaluate long-horizon visual tool use and fine-grained multimodal reasoning. However, existing work still does not fully isolate the ability to repeatedly inspect fine-grained visual evidence, revisit image regions, and construct deep visual evidence chains.

However, they remain insufficient for evaluat-ing the Visual DeepSearch capability. First, most of their tasks are not fully vision-centric: images often serve as initial context, while later reason-ing can proceed through text, web evidence, or parametric knowledge. Second, fine-grained image retrieval and repeated region-level inspection are still under-evaluated. Third, many tasks have lim-ited search depth, making them solvable through shallow visual recognition or shortcut reasoning. Fourth, some datasets are vulnerable to knowledge leakage, where answers can be inferred from tex-tual hints or memorized associations. Finally, trans-parent and reusable construction pipelines are still not consistently provided. To address these gaps, we introduce V ISTA HOP , a benchmark designed to evaluate iterative, fine-grained, and evidence-grounded Visual DeepSearch .

3.1.2 Entity Knowledge Enrichment and Seed Selection 

After extracting image-grounded entities, we enrich each entity with Wikipedia-derived textual knowl-edge and select valid seed entities for evidence-chain construction. For each entity, we query Wikipedia using its normalized name and retrieve its page content. An LLM then extracts a compact structured record, including a short description, an inferred entity type, useful properties, and related entities. These enriched records form the initial seed pool. Entities with valid descriptions or properties are retained as seed candidates. If Wikipedia is insuffi-cient, Wikidata-based properties are supplemented. Entities that still lack valid properties are discarded, as they cannot support reliable neighbor retrieval or evidence-chain expansion.

Starting from each selected seed entity, we query Wikidata to retrieve neighboring entities and rela-tion labels. The relations are normalized into 8 categories: part-whole , member-collection , causal ,temporal , spatial , comparative , attributive , and as-sociative . To reduce noise, we keep at most 30 retrieved neighbors as the pre-selection pool, from which an LLM selects up to 10 candidates most relevant to the current chain context. Each selected hop is then verified by an LLM-based relation checker. For each candidate pair, the LLM judges whether the relation is valid, assigns a confidence score, and generates a short rationale. A hop is retained only if its confidence score is no lower than τnli = 0 .9. 3.1.4 Textual QA Construction 

Given a verified evidence chain cj , we generate a natural-language question qj about the terminal node vk, and let the canonical name or normalized value of vk be the answer. The question is written in an indirect form to re-duce direct string matching, entity lookup, and text-only shortcuts. We describe the seed or interme-diate entities using visual or knowledge-grounded clues, such as “ the organization whose logo ap-pears in the image .” The output of this step is a set of candidate textual QA pairs: 

Q = {(qj , a j , c j )}mj=1 , (4) where qj is the generated question, aj is the target answer, and cj is the corresponding evidence chain. 

3.1.5 VQA Generation and Anti-Leakage Verification 

This step converts candidate textual QA pairs into VQA items and verifies that the final questions can-not be reliably solved from text alone. For each can-didate pair (qj , a j , c j ), we attach the corresponding image reference Ij , convert the evidence chain into a structured reasoning path ρj , and simplify the question into ˜qj for better readability. The simplifi-cation improves fluency while preserving the target answer and the underlying reasoning chain. 

Multi-agent anti-leakage verification. To detect potential textual shortcuts, we perform anti-leakage verification on the final VQA question ˜qj with a three-agent loop. The S OLVER receives only the question text and attempts to answer it without im-age or evidence chain. The J UDGE checks whether the answer matches the ground truth, whether tex-tual clues reveal it, or whether the question is am-biguous, under-specified, or non-unique. If issues are detected, the R EWRITE agent revises the ques-tion based on Judge feedback while preserving the original image reference, target answer, and evi-dence chain. The loop runs at most Rmax = 3 

rounds, and unresolved questions are discarded. 

Condition ablation. To further reduce hidden textual shortcuts, we remove each condition or clue one by one and ask the text-only S OLVER to answer. If the answer remains recoverable, the removed condition is considered redundant or leaking, and the question is rewritten. The final VQA item is represented as: 

yj = ( Ij , ˜qj , a j , ρ j , M j ), (5) where Mj stores metadata such as hop count, rela-tion types, source entity, verification records, and generation records. The output is the single-chain VQA set Dsingle = {yj }Nj=1 .Each retained VQA item is answerable from the intended image-grounded evidence chain but can-not be reliably solved from the question text alone. 

3.1.6 Multi-Chain Fusion 

Single-chain VQA instances mainly evaluate whether a model follows one evidence chain from a visual seed to the answer. To evaluate repeated visual evidence seeking and cross-chain reasoning, we construct fused questions by combining multi-ple related VQA chains from the same or related visual context. Each fused question requires solv-ing several component chains before deriving the final answer. Specifically, we select K ≥ 2 thematically or logically related VQA instances. For each VQA, we extract an intermediate value from its answer or chain metadata ( e.g. , year, count, ranking, numer-ical quantity). We then apply a numerical or con-ditional fusion operation to these values, including addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, max-imum, minimum, average, and conditional selec-tion. The fused question describes each component through its original visual or evidence-grounded clue without exposing the intermediate values. The model must identify multiple visual anchors, solve their evidence chains, and perform the final rea-soning. Each fused item stores the fused question, final answer, extraction details, component VQA instances, and fused reasoning path. These items form the fused VQA set. 

3.1.7 VQA Verification and Human Revision 

As a final quality-control step, three human an-notators manually verify each candidate. They check whether the reasoning path leads to the an-notated answer, whether the question is ambiguous or misleading, and whether the answer is unique. Instances that fail any check are discarded. The annotators then revise the retained questions for readability while preserving the visual grounding, reasoning path, and final answer, ensuring final questions are valid, unambiguous, uniquely answer-able, and readable.

Validation Agent. We use an LLM-as-judge to evaluate the final response. The primary metric is Pass@1 : whether the first submitted answer is semantically equivalent to the ground truth. 5.3 Ablation Study 

We compare three inference settings: No-Tool ,

Search , and Search+Crop . Table 4 shows that ex-ternal search improves over direct answering, and image cropping further helps when questions re-quire small logos, text, or local objects. The gains are not uniform: some models retrieve irrelevant evidence or fail to bind retrieved facts back to the correct image region. The multi-anchor setting remains the hardest because it requires decomposi-tion, repeated visual inspection, and final evidence fusion. 

5.4 Sensitivity Study 

Table 5 studies the effect of the maximum number of reasoning rounds N . Increasing N improves performance up to N = 10 , where the model has enough budget to inspect visual anchors, search in-termediate evidence, and verify the answer. Larger budgets introduce more redundant calls and noisy context, causing performance to saturate or decline. This suggests that Visual DeepSearch requires not only more evidence, but also accurate early visual grounding and efficient search control. 

6 Conclusion 

We introduce V ISTA HOP , a benchmark for eval-uating Visual DeepSearch with 350 VQA tasks across 25 scenarios. VISTA HOP tests repeated image inspection, visual-anchor grounding, and long-hop evidence connection with external knowl-edge. We also develop V ISTA ARENA , a unified framework for direct answering, search-augmented reasoning, and crop-assisted visual inspection. Ex-periments on 7 MLRMs show that current mod-els remain far from solving the benchmark: the best model, SenseNova-MARS-32B, achieves only 24.31% Pass@1. 

Limitations 

Construction dependence and scalability. Al-though our construction process is largely auto-mated, it still relies on strong multimodal models for entity extraction, question generation, relation verification, and anti-leakage checking, followed by human revision for readability. This design improves benchmark quality, but also introduces cost and reproducibility challenges when scaling to more images, domains, or languages. Future work can reduce this dependence by adopting stronger open-source multimodal models and more deter-ministic verification modules.
