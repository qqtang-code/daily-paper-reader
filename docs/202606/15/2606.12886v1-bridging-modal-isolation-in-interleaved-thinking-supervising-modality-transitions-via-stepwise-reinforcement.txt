However, in complex long-chain scenarios, we identify a fundamental failure mode: generated images diverge from the textual context while subsequent text ignores the visual evidence, causing the two modalities to alternate without genuinely informing each other. We term this Modal Isolation

The results demonstrate that effective interleaved reasoning requires explicit structural supervision at modality boundaries, not merely scaling or end-task optimization.

# 1 Introduction 

The ability to reason about the physical world requires coordinating abstract symbolic thinking with concrete visual imagination [ 1 , 28 , 23 ]. Unified multimodal models (UMMs), capable of natively generating both text and images [ 21 , 26 ], have enabled interleaved thinking : a reasoning paradigm where the model alternates between textual reasoning and visual world modeling within a single chain [ 13 , 5, 11 , 25 ]. Prior work has demonstrated the viability of this paradigm on short-horizon spatial and physical tasks. In this work, we investigate its behavior in long-chain visual reasoning. We observe a persistent failure mode across multiple tasks, illustrated in Figure 1. Consider a maze navigation example: the model first performs textual reasoning, planning a sequence of moves (“move left 2 steps, go down 4 steps, go up 4 steps...”). It then generates an image to visualize the resulting path. However, the generated image depicts a path that is fundamentally inconsistent with the textual plan—passing through walls and deviating from the specified moves. This constitutes text-to-image information loss ( ℓtext →image ). More critically, when the model subsequently observes its own generated image, it fails to detect these inconsistencies: it declares “the blue path goes from start to end without hitting any walls ” and confidently concludes the goal is reached—despite the path being clearly invalid. This constitutes image-to-text information loss ( ℓimage →text ). The two modalities alternate in form but never genuinely inform each other, and the model arrives at a confident but wrong 

answer. We term this phenomenon Modal Isolation : compounding information loss at modality boundaries progressively decouples the two modalities, causing each to operate in isolation despite their surface-level interleaving. 1

# 2 Related Work 

Multimodal Understanding and Generation. Recent multimodal models have gradually moved be-yond perception-centered understanding toward joint modeling of understanding and generation, con-tributing to the development of unified multimodal models (UMMs). Early approaches connect large language models with image generators, visual decoders, or generative visual tokens, enabling models to produce images or mixed-modal outputs conditioned on multimodal contexts [ 12 , 8, 29 , 19 , 18 ]. Subsequent work explores tighter integration between language and vision, including autoregressive modeling over multimodal tokens, early-fusion mixed-modal architectures, unified autoregressive-diffusion frameworks, and native image-text generation [ 22 , 20 , 27 , 4]. More recent studies further revisit the relationship between visual understanding and image generation within unified frame-works, showing that the two capabilities may require visual representations at different levels of granularity, and improving their coordination through decoupled visual encoding, model scaling, 2Supervising Modality Transitions via Stepwise Reinforcement 

and large-scale unified pretraining [ 24 , 2 , 7]. Together, these studies establish the conditions under which text and images can be jointly modeled and generated through a shared multimodal interface. However, they primarily address how to build models capable of understanding and generation across modalities, rather than how these capabilities behave when repeatedly composed over long reasoning. 

Interleaved Visual Reasoning. Building on these model-level advances, interleaved visual reasoning studies how visual representations can participate in the reasoning process itself, rather than serving only as static inputs or final outputs. Explicit visual-state generation methods produce visual imagina-tion, sketches, or progressive image-text reasoning traces to support tasks involving spatial relations, object-state changes, and multi-step visual planning [ 13 , 6 , 11 ]. Visually grounded multimodal reason-ing methods instead introduce grounding signals, visual tokens, or structured image-text supervision into chain-of-thought reasoning, strengthening the connection between verbal reasoning and visual evidence without necessarily synthesizing full images [ 9, 10 , 3 , 16 ]. Post-training approaches further encourage models to acquire, generate, or exploit visual information through reinforcement learning or process-level rewards, improving the modeling and use of intermediate visual states in complex rea-soning [ 30 , 15 ]. Complementary benchmarks and analyses show that intermediate visual information can provide state constraints that are difficult to express through language alone, especially in tasks involving spatial structure, physical state tracking, and visual world modeling [ 31 , 25 ]. Taken together, these studies establish visual content as an intermediate representation for multimodal reasoning. However, existing work primarily examines the utility, construction, elicitation, and final-task benefits of visual steps; the fidelity of the modality transitions themselves remains less explicitly characterized. Specifically, the world state encoded in textual reasoning may fail to be faithfully externalized by the generated image, and subsequent text may fail to correctly decode or use the generated visual evidence. This gap motivates our focus on Modal Isolation, where two modalities appear to alternate over long reasoning chains but progressively decouple in the world states they encode and use.

In this chapter, we systematically introduce the task selection and data construction pipeline, training workflow, hyperparameter settings, and evaluation suite. 6Supervising Modality Transitions via Stepwise Reinforcement 

## 4.1 Task Selection and Naive SFT Dataset 

Based on the varying cognitive load required to solve multimodal problems (i.e., whether the primary reasoning function is performed by the generation or the understanding module), we select four representative types of tasks: (1) Sokoban. Built upon Game-RL. This task requires the model to find the optimal path (i.e., pushing boxes to designated targets with the minimum number of steps) in a Sokoban grid environment. In this task, the understanding module bears the primary reasoning responsibility. (2) Maze. Also built upon the Game-RL codebase, this task requires the model to find the correct path from the start to the goal in a maze environment. (3) Multi-hop Manipulation. 

Inspired by works such as CLEVER, we use Blender to render composite images that contain certain spatial relationships and individual attributes. The model is required to perform operations such as “removing/adding” objects, converting object attributes (e.g., color, shape), and moving objects’ spatial positions, and then answer specific spatial understanding questions. In tasks (2) and (3), the model needs to combine the abstract symbolic reasoning provided by the visual understanding module with the physical world intuition provided by the generation module to solve the problems. (4) Ball Tracking. Based on RBench-V and Viseval, this task provides the starting position and outgoing direction of a ball, and asks after several specular reflections which region the ball finally reaches. This task heavily demands the model’s physical intuition and visual world knowledge modeling, and the visual generation module bears the primary reasoning responsibility. We use a rule-based solver to obtain the correct answers and code-synthesized chain-of-thought; meanwhile, we render the key intermediate images during the solving process. We prompt Gemini3.1-Flash-Lite to rewrite the chain-of-thought, ultimately obtaining a high-quality, image-text interleaved fine-tuning dataset. See Table 1. 

## 4.2 Model Training 

To isolate the effects of Modal Isolation across tasks, we train a separate model per task. We choose Bagel [ 7 ] as our baseline, and all experiments are done on 8 ×H200 GPUs, full hyperparameters are in the Appendix. Our pipeline has two stages mapping to the two sub-objectives of Eq. 7. 

Reward Design. To operationalize the modality transition losses, we construct two rubric-based VLM-as-Judge evaluators that explicitly quantify cross-modal hallucination and visual utilization at each modality boundary. Both produce binary (0/1) judgments, providing clean, transition-level training signals independent of end-task outcomes. Concretely, Rimg evaluates the MT →V transition: given the problem, the preceding reasoning context ending with a drawing instruction, the generated image, and the ground-truth image, it judges whether the generated image is logically equivalent to the ground truth—requiring agreement on task-relevant objects, spatial relations, and actions while tolerating superficial style differences. Rtxt evaluates the MV →T transition: for non-final steps it checks whether the <think>...</think> text correctly observes the preceding image, reasons coherently, and issues an executable drawing instruction; for final steps it verifies answer correctness and <ans>...</ans> 

formatting. Both evaluators are implemented by prompting a strong frontier VLM with detailed rubrics. 

Stage 1: Reflective SFT. This stage optimizes Lgen →und by training the model to detect and recover from erroneous visual outputs. Starting from the naive interleaving chains in Section 4, we randomly replace an intermediate image vk with a corrupted variant ˜vk to simulate a generation error, then prompt Gemini3.1-Flash-Lite to produce reflective text that identifies the discrepancy and responds. Two recovery patterns are used: (1) Detect-and-Ignore. where the model acknowledges the error and continues without regenerating. (2) Detect-and-Redraw. where it diagnoses the error and re-issues the drawing instruction for vk. # 6 Discussion 

In this paper, we identify an inherent failure mode of interleaved thinking in unified multimodal models. By decomposing it into atomic operations, we pinpoint the phenomenon of modal isolation, wherein the textual and visual modalities alternate in form but fail to transfer information across boundaries. We quantitatively evaluate its manifestation using a carefully designed rubrics-based VLM judge. We reveal that effective interleaved reasoning relies on structural supervision at modal boundaries. More broadly, we provides a principled optimization objective for multimodal reasoning that extends beyond this specific paradigm. We hope this work provides both a diagnostic framework and a practical toolkit for advancing multimodal reasoning in unified models. 10 Supervising Modality Transitions via Stepwise Reinforcement
