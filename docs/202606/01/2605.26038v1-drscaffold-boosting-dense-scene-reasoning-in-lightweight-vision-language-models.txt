## Abstract 

Lightweight vision-language models perform competitively on standard bench-marks yet fail systematically in dense-scene reasoning, where multiple objects, attributes, and relations must be jointly grounded and resolved through multi-step inference. Such capability is critical for real-world applications where models must reliably interpret cluttered environments. Yet existing training signals provide no explicit grounding between reasoning steps and the underlying visual entities and relations, leaving lightweight models free to generate fluent but visually unanchored reasoning chains. To address this gap, we first introduce DRBench , a benchmark of 14,573 questions across 2,943 images, organized into five task categories spanning three progressive reasoning layers. Building on DRBench, we propose DRScaffold ,a supervised fine-tuning framework that decomposes the supervision target into four causally ordered stages, enforcing grounded reasoning without architectural modification. Experiments on three lightweight VLMs demonstrate substantial gains on DRBench while preserving or improving performance on general-purpose benchmarks. Notably, Qwen2.5-VL-3B trained with DRScaffold surpasses the frozen Qwen2.5-VL-32B on DRBench, demonstrating that structured supervision can substitute for a significant portion of model scale in dense-scene reasoning. Our code and models are available at https://github.com/irene-shi/DRScaffold.

Given the strong reasoning ability of large VLMs, a practical solution is to leverage their outputs as supervision drafts to train light models. Yet current strategies remain insufficient due to a fundamental mismatch between the multi-stage reasoning process that dense-scene understanding requires and the undifferentiated form of existing training signals. Outcome-based supervision trains on final answers alone, discarding all intermediate reasoning [ 22 , 21 ]. Chain-of-Thought supervision improves on this by distilling the teacher’s reasoning process, but treats it as a flat token sequence without structural constraints [ 11 , 26 ]. Neither approach enforces alignment between reasoning steps and the underlying visual evidence. We argue that dense-scene reasoning requires four distinct stages that must be explicitly modeled: 

(1) entity grounding to identify and locate relevant objects, (2) relation modeling to resolve the spatial or logical dependencies among them, (3) stepwise reasoning to perform multi-step inference based on the structured relations, and (4) answer generation . This decomposition is not reflected in any existing training. Existing methods entangle reasoning stages into a monolithic sequence, which allows lightweight models to minimize training loss by mimicking fluent language patterns without verifying which visual entity each step refers to. This leads to reasoning that sounds plausible but is factually ungrounded, a failure mode that flat supervision cannot diagnose or prevent. To bridge this gap, we propose DRScaffold , a supervised fine-tuning framework that enhances 

Dense-scene Reasoning through “ Scaffold ing”, enabling the model to learn complex structure layer by layer. First, we reformulate the supervision target as a schema-constrained pipeline with fixed slot order: detected entities, scene graph, step-wise reasoning, and final answer, so that the answer token is causally conditioned on previously grounded entities and verified relations. Second, training proceeds via staged gradient masking: each of the four training phases unlocks one additional field for loss computation, compelling the student to internalize each stage before proceeding to the next. Training and evaluating such a framework requires supervision that existing benchmarks do not provide. We introduce DRBench , a Dense-scene Reasoning Bench mark of 14,573 questions across 2,943 images, organized into five categories in three reasoning layers. Beyond final correct answers, each sample contains structured reasoning process annotations, including key entities, scene graphs, and reasoning chains. These serve as supervision targets for DRScaffold. DRBench includes a False Premise Rejection category in which visually absent referents are embedded inside attribute or relation queries, providing a more challenging test than binary-polling benchmarks [19, 33, 14, 4]. ## 2 Related Work 

2.1 Supervised Fine-Tuning 

Leveraging large models to generate supervision signals for training smaller models has become a scalable paradigm for reasoning-intensive tasks [ 31 , 28 , 22 ]. Unlike traditional knowledge distillation that aligns output distributions or intermediate feature representations [ 15 , 30 , 27 ], recent approaches use large models to produce rich offline supervision, including instruction-following data [ 31 , 28 ], reasoning traces [17, 11, 16], and preference data [32]. Among these, Chain-of-Thought (CoT) distillation has emerged as a particularly promising direction for reasoning-intensive tasks. Rather than training on final answers alone [ 22 , 21 ], the student model is trained to reproduce the teacher’s step-by-step reasoning process [ 17 , 26 ]. DDCoT [ 38 ] separates multimodal reasoning into visual recognition and language reasoning, assigning each component to specialized models and integrating them into a joint CoT rationale. LLaVA-CoT [ 36 ] constructs a structured four-stage reasoning pipeline: summarization, visual interpretation, logical reasoning, and conclusion generation, demonstrating that structured stage tags significantly improve over untagged flat CoT. However, both DDCoT and LLaVA-CoT treat reasoning as a sequence of language stages without grounding each step to specific visual entities or relations. In dense-scene settings, this is where models fail: a reasoning step that mentions “the object to the left” carries no guarantee that the model has correctly identified which object that refers to. DRScaffold addresses this by explicitly conditioning each reasoning stage on a structured scene graph derived from the image. 

2.2 Benchmarks for Visual Reasoning 

Early visual question answering benchmarks such as VQAv2 [ 13 ] and GQA [ 18 ] establish quantitative foundations for evaluating visual perception and reasoning, but have become insufficient as VLM capabilities rapidly advance. More recent work addresses this by expanding both scale and task diversity. MMMU [ 37 ] tests expert-level understanding across 30 disciplines, MMBench [ 23 ]systematically decomposes capability into a three-level taxonomy covering 20 ability dimensions and introduces the CircularEval strategy, and MMStar [ 5] filters for samples that genuinely require visual evidence to reduce language-only leakage. Specialized benchmarks, such as RealWorldQA [ 35 ], MathVista [ 24 ], and ScienceQA [ 25 ], further target specific domains including spatial reasoning and scientific problem solving. DRBench addresses this gap by providing structured intermediate annotations alongside each sample, enabling evaluation at every stage of the reasoning pipeline. Despite this progress, two gaps remain. First, every sample provides only a final answer label, treating the reasoning process as a black box and making it impossible to diagnose where a model’s reasoning breaks down. Second, no existing benchmark is specifically designed for dense-scene reasoning, where multiple objects, attributes, and relations must be jointly grounded and resolved.

## 3 DRBench 

DRBench is built on a single principle: every sample must force a model to simultaneously ground multiple entities, resolve their relational dependencies, and derive an answer through a multi-step inference chain. Three properties differentiate DRBench from existing benchmarks. (1) Images are chosen for dense scenes that stress the three failure modes of relation misinterpretation, attribute misbinding, and hallucinated dependencies. (2) Every question is verified to require visual evidence. (3) Beyond the final answer, each sample carries structured annotations: key entities, a scene graph, and a staged reasoning chain, as shown in Figure 3. DRBench comprises 14,573 questions across 2,943 images, organized into five categories spanning three progressive reasoning layers. 

3.1 Image Sources and Scene Selection 

We source images from two complementary datasets that together span static indoor and dynamic outdoor dense-scene distributions, preventing models from exploiting a single-scene prior. From Hypersim [ 29 ], we select 440 photorealistic synthetic indoor scenes covering diverse room types, such as living rooms, kitchens, and offices, where objects appear in tight spatial proximity with ground-truth geometry available for answer verification. From Cityscapes [ 9 ], we select ego-centric urban driving images from 27 out of the 50 German cities recorded under varying lighting and weather conditions, featuring densely annotated street scenes with pedestrians, vehicles, cyclists, and infrastructure. Across the full dataset, we annotate 5,674 entities and 2,444 unique relation types, reflecting the compositional complexity of dense scenes, as shown in Figure 4. 

3.2 Question Design and Taxonomy 

DRBench organizes its questions into five categories across three progressive layers. The layered design is motivated by a hierarchical decomposition of the cognitive demands required for embodied scene understanding: Layer 1 isolates structural grounding as the prerequisite for all higher-order reasoning; Layer 2 builds on this foundation by requiring models to apply grounded scene understand-ing toward deployment-relevant judgments; Layer 3 introduces an orthogonal challenge of logical rigor in the face of visually absent referents. Layer 1 and Layer 2 both concern entities that are present in the scene, progressing from structural perception to contextual application, while Layer 3 concerns entities that are entirely absent, testing whether a model resists fabricating what it cannot see. Figure 5 illustrates the distribution of question categories and representative examples. 

Layer 1: Structural Grounding. (1) Perception questions anchor the target through multi-hop referring expressions, requiring the model to navigate a spatial chain to identify an entity’s attributes such as color, material, count, and state of motion. Any question that both models answer correctly is removed from the pool, yielding a retention rate of 80.2%. 

Scene-Matching Filtering. Dense scenes introduce source-specific failure modes that visual de-pendency filtering alone cannot catch. For Hypersim, a three-stage review is applied: Stage A uses Qwen-VL-Max to generate a scene summary and flag camera tilt; Stage B uses DeepSeek to identify questions implausible given the scene context; Stage C performs re-verification supported by annotations in Hypersim’s ground-truth geometry annotations, with pass, minor revision, or deletion as possible outcomes. For Cityscapes, Stages B and C are adapted to flag questions and cases where the target object is too small, ambiguous in color, or spatially unclear to support a reliable answer. 

Annotation Generation and Consistency Verification. Each sample’s structured annotations are generated in two phases. We first collect 300 human-annotated samples as exemplars. For the remaining questions, Qwen-VL-Max generates annotations conditioned on the image, question, options, and ground-truth answer. To facilitate consistency verification, the reasoning chain is structured into four ordered stages: Perception, Relation, Logic, and Conclusion. DeepSeek then checks (1) mutual consistency between the scene graph and key entities, and (2) that the reasoning chain’s conclusion entails the ground-truth answer. Failures trigger a feedback-revision loop back to Qwen-VL-Max, iterated up to 5 rounds, after which unresolved samples are discarded. A final human audit inspects the resulting annotations for correctness before inclusion. 

## 4 DRScaffold 

DRScaffold transfers dense-scene reasoning to a lightweight student model via supervision over a fixed-order schema, requiring no architectural modifications. As illustrated in Figure 7, it decomposes the supervision target into four causally ordered stages: (1) Entity Grounding , (2) Relation Model-ing , (3) Stepwise Reasoning , and (4) Answer Generation . The ordering of the four fields enforces a grounded reasoning pipeline: entities define the objects over which relations are formed, relations provide the scene graph used by subsequent reasoning, and the final answer is generated only after the intermediate evidence has been made explicit. ## 5 Experiments 

5.1 Setup Models. We apply DRScaffold to three pre-trained lightweight VLMs spanning different archi-tectures at the 2–6B scale: InternVL2.5-2B [ 6 ], Qwen2.5-VL-3B [ 3 ], and Phi4-multimodal [ 1]. Qwen2.5-VL-32B serves as the upper-bound reference. For each model, results are reported for both the unmodified base model and the DRScaffold fine-tuned counterpart. 

Training. All models are trained following the four phases and mixed fine-tuning procedure described in Section 4, using DRBench training samples as the primary supervision source, as is described in Section 3. LoRA adapters are applied to the attention projection matrices; the visual encoder, aligner, and embedding layers remain frozen throughout. All experiments are conducted on 2× NVIDIA A6000 GPUs in BFloat16 precision. 

Evaluation. We evaluate on six benchmarks spanning complementary aspects of multi-modal reasoning: MMMU [ 37 ] for expert-level multi-discipline understanding, MMStar [ 5 ] for vision-grounded reasoning, RealWorldQA [ 35 ] for spatial and physical reasoning, HallusionBench [ 14 ] for hallucination robustness, BLINK [ 10 ] for perception-demanding tasks, and DRBench test set for fine-grained dense-scene reasoning evaluation.

5.3 Ablation Studies Effect of Each Training Stage. To diagnose how each stage contributes, we track two intermediate metrics alongside accuracy: Scene Graph F1 , computed over relation triplets ⟨h, r, t ⟩ by soft-matching head, relation, and tail jointly, and Reasoning Validity , which measures whether the predicted reasoning chain entails the ground-truth answer as judged by DeepSeek. Stage Removal and Stage Reordering. Table 3 validates the necessity of both the complete stage set and the causal ordering, where all variants are trained for the same number of epochs. Removing any subset of stages consistently degrades performance. Reversing the order or applying a random ordering degrades performance further, demonstrating that grounding coarser visual entities before finer relational inference is a necessary inductive bias for lightweight models. 

## 6 Limitations and Discussions 

DRBench currently covers indoor and urban driving scenes, but may not represent the full diversity of dense-scene environments encountered in deployment. While DRScaffold demonstrates consistent gains across three VLM architectures, the comparatively modest improvement on Phi4-multimodal suggests that models with stronger pretrained dense-scene priors benefit less, and the interaction between base model capacity and scaffold design warrants further study. Additionally, the staged training procedure increases total training time proportionally to the number of stages, which may limit applicability in resource-constrained settings. We expect that exploring adaptive training strategies for dynamic adaptation to different scenarios could further improve generalization. 

## 7 Conclusion 

In this paper, we identify dense-scene reasoning as a critical yet underexplored bottleneck for lightweight VLMs. We introduce DRBench, a benchmark that provides structured intermediate annotations and a False Premise Rejection category that probes hallucination robustness. We further propose DRScaffold, a supervised fine-tuning framework that enforces grounded reasoning without architectural modification. Experiments demonstrate substantial gains on DRBench while preserving or improving performance on general-purpose benchmarks. Furthermore, structured supervision can substitute for a significant portion of model scale in dense-scene reasoning. Future work will focus on further diagnosing and improving complex reasoning in resource-constrained deployments. 9References

A.2 Step 1: Question Generation 

For each image, Qwen-VL-Max is prompted to generate 15 for HyperSim or 8–10 for Cityscapes question–answer pairs. At this stage only the question text, answer, and answer options (for MCQ) are produced; structured fields (key objects, scene graph, reasoning chain) are generated later in Step 4 after quality filtering is complete. 

Five reasoning categories. Every question must belong to exactly one of: • Perception : attribute identification anchored by multi-hop referring expressions. • Spatial Reasoning : spatial layout and simple physics. • Affordance Reasoning : object use within scene context. • Anomaly Detection : unusual or unsafe placements grounded in visible evidence. • False Premise Rejection : asks about an absent-but-plausible object; correct answer rejects the premise. 

Key design constraints. (a) Anchor only on large, unambiguous objects with sufficient pixel coverage. (b) Answers uniquely supported by visible evidence, with no plausible alternatives. (c) Multi-hop spatial chains in the question text. (d) Minimum complexity: ≥3 task-relevant objects,

For questions depending on a specific object/instance, DELETE or MODIFY when: - the target is TOO SMALL or too far away to be reliably identified; - the target’s SPATIAL LOCATION is ambiguous (e.g. “the car on the right” when several exist); - the target’s COLOR / identity is not clearly distinguishable (harsh lighting, 

16 shadow, motion blur, JPEG). If borderline, prefer fix (b) by tightening the referring expression; if no safe fix exists, delete. 7. Do NOT discuss camera tilt; treat the view as the driver would see it. Output STRICT JSON only (all input ids must appear exactly once): {“results”: [{“id”, “bad_question”: bool, “is_modified”: bool, “question”, “answer”, “options”, “reason”}]} 

A.5 Step 4: Structured Field Generation and Consistency Verification 

After Steps 2–3 some questions have their answer corrected or question text revised. For every accepted question, whether modified or unchanged, we now generate the four structured supervision fields: key_objects, scene_graph, reasoning_chain using qwen-vl-max conditioned on the image and the verified question-answer pair. A text-only consistency verifier, deepseek-v3, then checks for internal contradictions; if it returns pass: false, its feedback is appended to the generation prompt and qwen-vl-max regenerates. This loop repeats for up to 5 rounds. 

Prompt 4a: Structured Field Generation (Qwen-VL-Max) 

You fill structured supervision fields for ONE QA item. The image is your perceptual ground. Highest priority – Answer Consistency: The answer field is the ONLY ground truth. key_objects, scene_graph, and reasoning_chain MUST all support EXACTLY that answer. Do NOT describe distractor options as valid; only mention them if needed for elimination logic. Output (strict JSON, no markdown, no extra keys): {"key_objects": [...], "scene_graph": ["A –pred–> B", ...], "reasoning_chain": ["[Perception]: ...", "[Relation]: ...", "[Logic]: ...", "[Conclusion]: ..."]} Rules: reasoning_chain: EXACTLY 4 strings in order [Perception] [Relation] [Logic] [Conclusion]. [Conclusion] must explicitly state the same ground-truth answer (same letter(s) or phrase). scene_graph predicates: left_of, right_of, on, under, near, behind, in_front_of, attached_to, etc. key_objects: task-relevant only; prefer minimal set needed to derive the answer. Better include all entities mentioned in scene_graph. 

Prompt 4b: Consistency Verification (DeepSeek-V3, text-only) 

You are a strict logic reviewer for multimodal QA labels. You do NOT see the image. Check whether the proposed structured fields are logically consistent with each other AND with the ground-truth answer. The given answer is always correct. Flag: scene_graph vs key_entity contradiction; [Conclusion] vs answer mismatch; distractor options treated as correct when answer excludes them. Output STRICT JSON only: {"pass": true} or {"pass": false, "feedback": "what is wrong", "hint": "how fields should change"}

## C Training Details 

C.1 Model Architecture and Base Models 

We apply DRScaffold to three representative lightweight VLMs spanning different parameter counts and architecture families: • Qwen2.5-VL-3B-Instruct – 3B-parameter model with a native dynamic-resolution ViT; instruction-tuned baseline. • InternVL2.5-2B – 2B-parameter InternVL2.5 family model; instruction-tuned baseline. • Phi4-multimodal – Microsoft Phi-4 multimodal variant; strong general-purpose baseline. In all experiments, only the LLM decoder (transformer layers) is fine-tuned via LoRA; the ViT encoder, aligner/connector, and token embeddings are frozen throughout training. 

C.2 DRScaffold Training Phases 

Training follows four phases P1, P2, P3, and P4 that progressively introduce more of the struc-tured CoT supervision and then mixes in general instruction-following data to prevent catastrophic forgetting. 

P1 – Entity Grounding. 

Only the key_objects field of the CoT JSON is supervised; all subsequent fields are masked to weight 0.0. This phase bootstraps the LLM’s ability to ground visual entities. Epochs: 0.5×;LR: 5e-5 ; schedule: cosine with 10% warmup. 

P2 – Relation Modeling. 

Supervision extends to key_objects + scene_graph; reasoning_chain and answer remain masked. The model learns to produce structured spatial-relation triples. Epochs: 1.0×; LR: 

4e-5 ; schedule: constant with 20-step warmup. 

P3 – Stepwise Reasoning. 

Full sequence supervision excluding answer. In this phase the model learns to complete the multi-step reasoning chain before committing to an answer. Epochs: 1.0×; LR: 3e-5 ; schedule: constant with 30-step warmup. 

P4 – Anti-Forgetting Mix. 

Full sequence supervision. The LLaVA portion is increased to ∼15,000 samples to more aggressively recover general benchmark performance. A very low learning rate is used to make only small corrections without undoing the CoT capabilities learned in P1–P3. Epochs: 1.0×;LR: 3e-6 ; schedule: cosine with 5% warmup.
