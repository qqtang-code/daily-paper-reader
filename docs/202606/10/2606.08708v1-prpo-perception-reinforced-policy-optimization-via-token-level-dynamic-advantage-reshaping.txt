## Abstract 

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) has become an effective paradigm for improving the reasoning capability of Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs). However, existing RLVR methods primarily rely on trajectory-level out-come rewards, which assign identical learning signals across all generated tokens. This coarse-grained credit assignment is fundamentally mismatched to multimodal reasoning, where only a sparse subset of tokens is causally grounded in visual evidence. Consequently, these pivotal perceptual tokens receive weak supervision and are often overwhelmed by language priors or reasoning-template tokens. To address this limitation, we propose Perception-Reinforced Policy Optimization (PRPO) , a token-level reinforcement learning framework that explicitly identifies and reinforces pivotal perceptual tokens within long-horizon multimodal reasoning trajectories. PRPO introduces Robust Visual Dependency (RVD) , a principled metric that identifies tokens whose predictions are both visually grounded and perturbation-stable, filtering out brittle or noisy visual tokens. Based on RVD, we further propose Perceptual Advantage Reshaping (PAR) , a token-level credit as-signment technique that amplifies perceptually informative tokens while preserving stable gradients for non-perceptual tokens. Extensive experiments on seven mul-timodal reasoning benchmarks demonstrate that PRPO consistently outperforms strong LVLM baselines across both 3B and 7B model scales, achieving average gains of 23.3% and 21.1%, respectively. PRPO achieves state-of-the-art perfor-mance with improved training efficiency and stronger cross-task generalization. Our findings highlight the importance of fine-grained credit assignment for scalable multimodal reinforcement learning. 

## 1 Introduction 

Recent advances in reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards (RLVR) have significantly strength-ened the reasoning capability of Large Language Models (LLMs), particularly when paired with online policy optimization algorithms such as Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [Shao et al., 2024, Guo et al., 2025, OpenAI, 2024, Team et al., 2025, Yang et al., 2025a, Anthropic, 2025, Zhang et al., 2025, Qu et al., 2025, Yan et al., 2025]. These gains have been most evident in text-focused tasks and benchmarks. Inspired by this success, researchers have increasingly explored how to adapt RLVR to Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs). Current efforts can be broadly organized into three lines of work: methods that improve training data quality or composition [Li et al., 2025, Liang et al., 2025, Liu et al., 2025, Yao et al., 2025, Chen et al., 2025a, Meng et al., 2025, Huang et al., 2025b, Yang et al., 2025b]; methods centered on reward modeling, reward shaping, or

Despite these advances, existing multimodal RLVR methods largely inherit designs from language-only settings, which assign a single trajectory-level reward to the final response and broadcast the same optimization signal across all generated tokens. While this design is useful for purely textual reasoning, it becomes fundamentally mismatched in multimodal reasoning. To better characterize this mismatch, we conduct an error analysis on MathVerse [Zhang et al., 2024] using Qwen2.5-VL-7B. As shown in Figure 1, the majority of failures do not stem from insufficient reasoning capability, but from errors in visual perception occurring within long reasoning trajectories. Specifically, LVLMs often ground on incorrect visual regions, misread local evidence, or extract erroneous numerical cues at intermediate perception steps. Such behaviors either lead to isolated Step Perception Errors or accumulate into Cascading Perception Errors . In contrast, failures attributable purely to reasoning errors are relatively rare. Furthermore, we find that key perceptual tokens in multimodal reasoning (e.g., coordinates, numerical values, or geometric cues) are both sparse and highly influential, yet they receive the same supervision as non-perceptual linguistic tokens under standard RL objectives. We identify this mismatch as the Perceptual Credit Assignment Problem , which contains two key issues: (i) sparse perceptual signals cannot be explicitly identified or reinforced from trajectory-level rewards, and (ii) uniform advantage assignment causes gradient dilution, where critical perceptual signals are overwhelmed by abundant reasoning tokens. As a result, existing RL methods tend to optimize surface-level reasoning fluency rather than improving visually grounded decision-making. To address this problem, we propose Perception-Reinforced Policy Optimization (PRPO) , a dynamic token-level reinforcement learning framework that explicitly targets perceptual credit assignment in multimodal reasoning. PRPO contains two key components: (i) Robust Visual Dependency (RVD) , identifies tokens that are genuinely grounded in visual evidence and robust to perturbations, and (ii) Perceptual Advantage Reshaping (PAR) , which reshapes trajectory-level advantage into fine-grained token-level learning signals based on RVD, amplifying informative perceptual tokens while preserving stable optimization gradients on reasoning-template tokens. Extensive experiments on seven multimodal reasoning benchmarks demonstrate that PRPO consis-tently improves performance across both 3B and 7B models, with average gains of 23.3% and 21.1%, respectively. Moreover, PRPO exhibits stronger performance under the same training budget and cross-task generalization, indicating that fine-grained perceptual reinforcement is more effective than uniform trajectory-level optimization in multimodal RLVR context. Our contributions are three-fold: • We identify Perceptual Credit Assignment as a key bottleneck in multimodal RLVR: most failures arise from sparse but critical perception errors rather than insufficient reasoning capability, while existing trajectory-level rewards fail to provide effective supervision to pivotal perceptual tokens. • We propose PRPO , a token-level RL framework with RVD and PAR technique to identify and reinforce pivotal perceptual tokens while preserving stable optimization gradients. 2• We achieve state-of-the-art (SOTA) results across seven multimodal reasoning benchmarks, demon-strating the importance of fine-grained credit assignment for multimodal reinforcement learning. 

## 2 Related Work 

Dominant Strategies in Multimodal RL. Current efforts to enhance multimodal RL largely focus on augmenting elements outside the core optimization algorithm. These include data-driven methods emphasizing the construction of visually-grounded datasets [Bai et al., 2025, Li et al., 2025, Liang et al., 2025] and CoT distillation [Chen et al., 2025b, Huang et al., 2025b, Meng et al., 2025], as well as reward-centric methodologies designing improved feedback mechanisms [Ma et al., 2025, Fan et al., 2025, Xia et al., 2025]. Recognizing the vision-language gap, recent works like Perception-Aware Policy Optimization (PAPO) [Wang et al., 2025d] explicitly account for modality differences to engineer perception-sensitive signals. However, even with such modality-aware designs or modality-agnostic algorithmic advances like DAPO [Yu et al., 2025b], these strategies still broadcast a uniform learning signal across the entire sequence. This indiscriminate credit assignment creates a severe bottleneck where critical, sparse visual signals are easily submerged by high-volume linguistic priors. 

Pivotal Tokens Identification in LVLMs. To mitigate gradient dilution, it is crucial to isolate specific perceptual tokens from critical reasoning. In text-only domains, this involves identifying high-entropy “forking points” [Wang et al., 2025c] or low-confidence errors [Vassoyan et al., 2025]. For LVLMs, approaches like VPPO [Huang et al., 2025a] attempt to address this by utilizing KL Divergence-based visual dependency to reweight trajectory-level advantages, aiming to focus gradient updates on visually-grounded steps. However, relying on a single visual dependency metric risks amplifying noise-sensitive tokens, and trajectory-level reward reshaping is fundamentally insufficient to precisely reinforce visually important tokens. To tackle this, our PRPO framework introduces a precise dynamic token-level optimization paradigm. By jointly evaluating visual dependency and consistency, we systematically filter out stochastic noise to isolate genuinely pivotal perceptual tokens. We then dynamically reshape their learning advantages, explicitly amplifying critical perceptual signals without triggering gradient collapse or compromising foundational reasoning capabilities.

4.2 Token-Level Dynamic Perceptual Advantage Reshaping (PAR) 

While RVD provides a fine-grained measure of perceptual relevance, directly using it for credit assignment leads to suboptimal optimization behavior due to its highly skewed distribution: 

(1) Long-tailed distribution. RVD exhibits a heavy-tailed structure, where the majority of tokens concentrate near zero, while only a small subset of perceptually grounded tokens take large values. Directly using such signals results in inefficient credit allocation. 

(2) Gradient collapse. A naive transformation (e.g., linear or monotonic scaling) causes most token-level weights to shrink toward zero, effectively collapsing gradients onto a sparse subset of visual tokens. This leads to a biased optimization process that over-focuses on perceptual tokens while neglecting linguistic reasoning, ultimately degrading general reasoning performance. To address these issues, we propose Perceptual Advantage Reshaping (PAR) , a differentiable nonlinear mapping that transforms raw perceptual signals into bounded token-level weights. The resulting S-shaped synaptic response curve suppresses low-signal tokens, amplifies informative perceptual tokens beyond a threshold, and enforces smooth saturation, thereby preventing gradient explosion while preserving stable optimization.

5.1 Experimental Setup Baseline Models, Data and Methods. To ensure a rigorous and equitable comparison with prior SOTA approaches, we deliberately align our experimental setup—including backbone architectures and training corpora-with established conventions in recent literature. Specifically, we instantiate our PRPO framework utilizing the Qwen2.5-VL series (both 3B and 7B variants) as the base policy models, and conduct training exclusively on the ViRL39K [Wang et al., 2025a] dataset. Furthermore, we benchmark PRPO against nine prominent training-based methods, encompassing both foundational reinforcement learning paradigms and recent multimodal-specific advancements. These baselines include GRPO [Shao et al., 2024], DAPO [Yu et al., 2025b], PAPO [Wang et al., 2025d], VPPO [Huang et al., 2025a], MM-Eureka-7B [Meng et al., 2025], VL-Rethinker-7B [Wang et al., 2025a], NoisyRollout-7B [Liu et al., 2025], and R1-ShareVL-7B [Yao et al., 2025]. 

Evaluation Benchmarks. For a comprehensive evaluation of model capabilities, we assess perfor-mance across seven widely adopted benchmarks spanning complex mathematical reasoning and gen-eral multimodal understanding: Geo3K [Lu et al., 2021], MathVista [Lu et al., 2023], We-Math [Qiao et al., 2024], MMK12 [Meng et al., 2025], MathVerse [Zhang et al., 2024], LogicVista [Xiao et al., 2024], MMMU-Pro [Yue et al., 2024]. MathVerse V denotes the vision-centric subset of MathVerse. 

Training Details. To ensure a rigorous and equitable comparison with baseline training method-ologies, we align our experimental protocol with established settings of prior works. Specifically, both the 3B and 7B models are trained on the ViRL39K [Wang et al., 2025a] dataset for 2 epochs, employing a learning rate of 1e-6 and a rollout batch size of 384. The maximum response length is constrained to 2048 tokens. Comprehensive hyperparameter settings are detailed in Appendix A.3.

## 6 Conclusion 

We propose PRPO, a token-level reinforcement learning framework that addresses the Perceptual Credit Assignment Problem in multimodal reasoning. A.1 Benchmarks and Evaluation Metrics 

To provide a comprehensive evaluation and comparison of different LVLM RL methods, we align with prior literature and select the following benchmarks across diverse difficulty levels and domains: 

(1) Geometric and Mathematical Reasoning. The primary focus of our experiments lies in assessing the model’s ability to navigate various mathematical and geometric domains. • Geo3k [Lu et al., 2021]: This benchmark specializes in geometry-centric problems at the high school level. Its rigorous use of formal language annotations allows for a precise evaluation of how well models can execute symbolic reasoning within a visual context. • MathVista : Serving as a comprehensive evaluation of visually situated mathematics, this bench-mark integrates a broad spectrum of visual contexts—ranging from statistical charts and functional plots to abstract geometry and real-world scenes. It systematically challenges models to synthesize complex visual evidence with deep quantitative and logical deduction, providing a robust measure of generalized mathematical capability across highly diverse visual modalities. • We-Math [Qiao et al., 2024]: Focused on a human-centric diagnostic approach, We-Math breaks down complex problems into a hierarchy of 67 specific knowledge points. This granularity allows us to identify whether a failure stems from a lack of specific knowledge or a breakdown in the reasoning chain. • MMK12 [Meng et al., 2025]: This benchmark provides a standardized assessment of foundational math skills derived from K-12 curricula. It ensures that the model possesses the prerequisite quantitative literacy required for more specialized tasks. • MathVerse [Zhang et al., 2024]: This benchmark probes the visual dependency of Large Vision-Language Models. By offering six variants of the same problem with differing ratios of visual-to-textual information, it uncovers whether a model truly "perceives" a diagram or simply exploits linguistic redundancies. 

(2) Logical and Cognitive Foundations. Beyond pure mathematics, we evaluate the model’s underlying cognitive architecture through targeted logical tasks. • LogicVista [Xiao et al., 2024]: Designed to measure general-purpose logic, this benchmark transcends the mathematical domain by testing five core reasoning pillars: induction, deduction, and spatial, mechanical, and numerical reasoning. It offers a diverse array of visual formats to assess fundamental intellectual agility. 

(3) Advanced Multimodal Integration . To measure the upper limits of university-level understand-ing where visual and textual data must be processed as a single entity, we utilize a specialized "pro" suite. • MMMU-Pro [Yue et al., 2024]: As a more rigorous iteration of the MMMU dataset, this version is specifically engineered to defeat text-only shortcuts. By increasing the number of distractors and incorporating vision-exclusive formats, it demands that the model perform high-level academic reasoning that is fundamentally impossible without deep multimodal fusion. 

A.2 Baseline Methods 

Recently, researchers have optimized reinforcement learning for LVLMs from various perspectives. Specifically, we compare PRPO with the following closely related baseline methods: • PAPO [Wang et al., 2025d] introduces a policy gradient algorithm that encourages reasoning steps to be internally grounded in visual inputs without relying on external annotations or reward models. However, its optimization objective is applied uniformly across all generated tokens, neglecting the inherently sparse nature of critical visual signals. • VPPO [Huang et al., 2025a] attempts to address the uniform signal bottleneck through a two-tiered strategy, employing trajectory-level reward shaping combined with focusing policy updates on a sparse set of perception tokens. 13 Differences from our work: While both baselines seek to enhance visually-grounded reasoning, they exhibit key limitations that PRPO explicitly resolves. PAPO’s uniform token treatment inevitably leads to gradient dilution, where critical visual signals are overwhelmed by abundant language priors. VPPO mitigates this by focusing on sparse tokens, but its reliance on coarse trajectory-level reward shaping and single-metric token selection risks amplifying numerically brittle visual noise. In contrast, our PRPO framework operates through a highly precise, dynamic token-level paradigm. By jointly evaluating Visual Dependency and Visual Inconsistency via the Robust Visual Dependency (RVD) metric, PRPO systematically filters out stochastic noise to isolate genuinely pivotal perceptual tokens. Furthermore, instead of discrete token masking or trajectory-level adjustments, PRPO utilizes Perceptual Advantage Reshaping (PAR)—a continuous, synaptic S-shaped gating mechanism that dynamically amplifies critical visual signals while preserving stable, non-zero optimization gradients for foundational reasoning capabilities.

B.3 Ablation Study on Perturbation Strategies 

To further optimize the performance of RVD in identifying pivotal perceptual tokens, we conduct an in-depth analysis of the perturbation strategies employed in our dual-metric framework. Drawing inspiration from prior works, we systematically evaluate these strategies from two critical dimensions: Information-Corrupting and Information-Preserving perturbations. 17 B.3.1 Information-Corrupting Perturbations 

Information-corrupting perturbations are designed to severely degrade the visual evidence, forcing the model to rely solely on its language prior. This allows us to quantify the macroscopic Visual Dependency ( St). We find that there are six common degradation strategies: • Random Patch Masking: Aligned with the underlying Vision Transformer (ViT) architecture of our base model, the input image is partitioned into 14 × 14 patches. Each patch is independently masked (set to zero) with a specific dropout probability (e.g., p = 0 .5). • Additive Gaussian Noise: Intense Gaussian noise ( σ = 189 ) is added to the pixel space. This extreme variance ensures that approximately 50% of the pixels are saturated to their minimum or maximum values, effectively erasing localized visual semantics. • Heavy Gaussian Blur: A severe Gaussian blur filter ( r = 6 .0) is applied to the image, comprehen-sively destroying high-frequency fine-grained details while preserving basic color layouts. • Complete Masking: The original image is entirely replaced by a solid, neutral gray canvas (RGB: 

[128 , 128 , 128] ), acting as a continuous blinding mechanism. • Modality Truncation: The visual input is entirely omitted from the prompt, reducing the generation to a purely text-only setting. • Semantic-Aware Masking: [Wang et al., 2025d] Instead of uniform random dropout, this approach utilizes a pre-trained vision encoder (e.g., DINOv2) to extract patch-level saliency scores from aggregated middle-layer self-attention maps. Patches with the highest semantic importance are preferentially masked, strategically corrupting the core visual evidence. Consistent with findings from previous studies [Huang et al., 2025a, Wang et al., 2025d], our preliminary experiments indicate that Random Patch Masking yields the most reliable gradient signals for identifying visually dependent tokens. B.4 Lexical Analysis: The Necessity of Dual-Metric Filtering 

To better understand the linguistic characteristics of visually dependent tokens, we visualize the lexical distributions of Pivotal Perceptual Tokens (High VD, Low VI) and Brittle Anchors (High VD, High VI) using word clouds. 18 Figure 7: Word cloud of Brittle Anchors (High VD, High VI). 

Figure 8: Word cloud of Pivotal Perceptual To-kens (High VD, Low VI). A visual inspection reveals a striking semantic and lexical overlap between the two categories. Both sets are predominantly composed of core mathematical and geometric vocabulary. Specifically, the high-frequency tokens consistently cluster into the following categories: • Numerical Values: Digits representing quantities or coordinates (e.g., 1, 2, 3, 4 ). • Geometric Entities: Nouns defining structural components (e.g., angle, triangle, line, circle ). • Spatial/Vertex Identifiers: Capitalized letters denoting points or segments (e.g., A, B, C, X, AB ). • Mathematical Symbols: Operational or relational tokens (e.g., <, / ). This high degree of lexical overlap exposes a critical vulnerability in single-metric token selection strategies (such as standard KL divergence). It demonstrates that a token’s semantic class—such as being a number or a coordinate—does not intrinsically guarantee its perceptual robustness. A high Visual Dependency (VD) score alone cannot distinguish whether the token “angle” or a specific digit is genuinely grounded in stable visual evidence, or if it is merely a stochastically fragile guess that will collapse under minor perturbations. Consequently, this observation strongly reinforces the absolute necessity of our dual-metric approach. By incorporating Visual Inconsistency (VI) as a stability penalty, our Robust Visual Dependency (RVD) metric successfully disentangles brittle artifacts from genuinely grounded perceptual anchors, elegantly resolving the ambiguity even when they share identical surface-level vocabularies.
