# Abstract 

Speech Large Language Models (SLLMs) underperform their text counterparts on complex reasoning. We reveal that this modality gap is not a uniform cognitive deficit. Evaluating three diverse SLLMs, we show speech-to-text (S2T) matches or exceeds text-to-text (T2T) on spatial, syntactic, and factual tasks. However, on logical tasks requiring entity tracking, S2T accuracy collapses to chance. We diagnose this localized degra-dation as an entity binding failure: continuous speech features cause models to lose precise entity-property associations during implicit reasoning. To resolve this, we propose Entity-Aware Chain-of-Thought (EA-CoT), forcing SLLMs to explicitly enu-merate entities and bind them to claims before reasoning. Strik-ingly, EA-CoT bridges the gap, even when spoken names are misrecognized, yielding up to a 24.4% absolute accuracy im-provement. Ablations confirm these gains stem entirely from explicit semantic binding, reframing the gap as a resolvable bot-tleneck. 

Index Terms : speech language models, reasoning, entity bind-ing, chain-of-thought, modality gap 

# 1. Introduction 

Speech Large Language Models (SLLMs) can directly process spoken input, enabling natural conversational interactions with-out relying on cascaded text transcription [1, 2, 3, 4]. However, recent benchmarking indicates that these models consistently underperform their text-based counterparts on complex reason-ing tasks [5, 6, 7, 8]. Previous studies have provided valuable macroscopic insights into this speech-to-text (S2T) versus text-to-text (T2T) modality gap, often attributing it to generalized in-formation dilution or modality-level representation divergence [9, 10, 11, 12]. Building upon these broad observations, we investigate the modality gap at a fine-grained, task-specific level. By evalu-ating two architecturally diverse SLLMs (Qwen2.5-Omni and Phi-4-Multimodal) across multiple reasoning categories, we re-veal that the performance degradation is highly uneven. On many tasks, such as spatial navigation, syntactic ordering, and factual understanding, S2T performance is fully comparable to T2T performance. Instead, the severe modality gap is dispro-portionately concentrated in specific categories, most notably logical reasoning tasks that require entity tracking. For instance, on the web of lies task, S2T accuracy across both models plum-mets to chance levels, while T2T baselines range up to 92%. We diagnose that this localized degradation stems from the inherent nature of speech encoding. To efficiently process continuous audio sequences, SLLM encoders heavily rely on

temporal pooling and downsampling mechanisms [13, 14, 15, 16]. While these operations effectively extract global semantic information—preserving performance on general comprehen-sion and spatial tasks—they inevitably blur fine-grained acous-tic details and discrete token boundaries. Consequently, when the model attempts to implicitly track entities and their chang-ing properties, the smoothed-out continuous features cause it to lose track of precise details. This manifests as entity binding failures: the model understands the overall context but fails to maintain strict associations between specific entities and their changing states within its deep internal reasoning layers. To overcome this structural bottleneck, we propose Entity-Aware Chain-of-Thought (EA-CoT), an inference-time inter-vention that bypasses the fragile implicit acoustic tracking. EA-CoT forces the SLLM to explicitly enumerate entities and bind them to their respective claims in the generated text space be-fore executing step-by-step reasoning. Strikingly, enforcing this explicit structural binding allows S2T performance to recover to near-T2T levels. Furthermore, we observe that this intervention succeeds even if the SLLM’s transcription of the entity name is phonetically altered or not totally accurate (e.g., mapping the spoken name “Ka” to “Cass”). As long as the model establishes a consistent textual anchor in the CoT to bind properties to, the logical reasoning chain remains intact. This profoundly demon-strates that the critical failure in SLLM logical reasoning is not merely a superficial speech recognition error, but a structural semantic binding failure caused by acoustic detail loss. Our core contributions are: • Task-Specific Gap Diagnosis: We demonstrate that the S2T/T2T modality gap is not a uniform cognitive deficit, but is heavily concentrated in entity-tracking tasks. Excluding these, S2T reasoning is largely comparable to T2T. • Mechanistic Explanation: We link SLLM logical failures to the loss of acoustic details through encoder pooling, showing that blurred token boundaries disrupt implicit entity binding while global semantics remain preserved. • Effective Targeted Intervention: We introduce EA-CoT to force explicit entity-property binding. Compared against structured control prompts on other tasks, this targeted in-tervention uniquely bridges the modality gap in logical rea-soning (yielding up to a +24.4 pp improvement) and remains effective even when exact entity names are misrecognized. 

# 2. Related Work 

The Speech-Text Modality Gap. Recent end-to-end Speech Large Language Models (SLLMs) achieve strong conversa-tional abilities but consistently underperform their text coun-terparts on complex reasoning benchmarks [5, 6, 7, 17]. Prior 

> arXiv:2606.04474v1 [cs.CL] 3 Jun 2026

studies have approached this modality gap primarily from a macroscopic perspective. Layer-wise and representation-level analyses attribute the performance degradation to generalized information dilution [9] or modality alignment divergence [10], suggesting that the continuous and redundant nature of speech tokens hinders stable late-layer decisions. Concurrent works at-tempt to close this gap via training-time cross-modal distillation or trajectory alignment [18, 19]. Building upon these founda-tional observations, our work takes a task-specific diagnostic approach. We demonstrate that the SLLM reasoning gap is not a uniform cognitive deficit, but rather a highly localized fail-ure. Continuous speech encoding preserves global semantics for most tasks but specifically disrupts the precise tracking re-quired for entity-centric logical reasoning. 

Entity Binding in Foundation Models. The “binding problem”—the cognitive capacity to robustly associate specific entities with their dynamic properties and states—is a recog-nized vulnerability in neural architectures [20]. Mechanistic in-terpretability studies reveal that text-based LLMs rely on spe-cialized internal vectors to maintain these links [21], and entity tracking remains fragile even in pure text scenarios [22, 23]. Similarly, binding failures have been shown to drive multi-object reasoning errors in Vision-Language Models [24]. We are the first to extend this critical theoretical lens to the speech modality. We identify that the temporal pooling and downsam-pling mechanisms inherent to SLLM encoders, while effective for extracting global semantics, severely blur the discrete token boundaries required for semantic binding. This architectural trait makes implicit spoken entity tracking catastrophically vul-nerable, establishing binding failure as the primary bottleneck of the SLLM modality gap. 

Chain-of-Thought for Modality Adaptation. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting fundamentally enhances LLM rea-soning by externalizing intermediate deductive steps [25, 26, 27, 28, 29]. In the audio domain, recent multimodal CoT adap-tations primarily focus on analyzing explicit acoustic events (e.g., environmental sounds) [30] or internalizing ASR tran-scripts to reduce response latency [31]. However, applying generic CoT (e.g., “Let us think step by step”) to SLLMs on logical tasks yields minimal improvement because the implicit mapping between continuous audio features and discrete en-tities is already corrupted. Unlike prior methods, our Entity-Aware CoT (EA-CoT) serves as a targeted structural interven-tion. Rather than relying on fragile implicit acoustic tracking, EA-CoT forces the model to explicitly enumerate and project blurred acoustic entities into a stable text space before reason-ing. By establishing robust textual anchors, EA-CoT success-fully bypasses the modality-induced binding bottleneck, even when phonetic recognition is imperfect.

3.2. Task-Specific Structured Chain-of-Thought 

To address the entity binding bottleneck diagnosed in Section 1, we design a prompting strategy that forces the model to make the implicit binding process explicit. For web of lies , the Entity-Aware Chain-of-Thought (EA-CoT) prompt prepends four in-structions before the task input (as illustrated in Figure 1): 1. Entity enumeration. List all people mentioned. 2. Claim recording. Write down each statement linking a per-son to a property. 3. Step-by-step reasoning. Resolve each claim in sequence. 4. Answer extraction. Produce the answer in the required for-mat. This forces the SLLM to externalize entity-property asso-ciations into generated text, converting fragile implicit track-ing into explicit textual anchoring. The maximum generation length is expanded to 1,024 tokens to accommodate the reason-ing trace. To rigorously isolate the entity binding bottleneck, we deploy structured control prompts for the remaining cate-gories. These force explicit step-by-step reasoning without en-tity tracking: listing and classifying adjectives by type ( hyper-baton ), sequentially tracking coordinate changes from the ori-gin ( navigate ), and identifying sports to evaluate action plausi-bility ( sports ). These isomorphic controls ensure that EA-CoT’s disproportionate S2T gain on web of lies reflects targeted se-mantic repair rather than generic CoT benefits.

# 4. Experiments 

4.1. Setup 

Two state-of-the-art speech LLMs spanning different architec-tures are evaluated. Qwen2.5-Omni-7B [33] employs a dedi-cated thinker module that generates internal reasoning tokens before the visible response. Phi-4-Multimodal [34] generates responses directly without a separate reasoning module. Both models are evaluated in their released configurations without fine-tuning. Four categories from VoiceBench BBH [5] are used, each containing 250 items for a total of 1,000 samples. Each item is evaluated with both speech and text input using the same model. A format guard ensures responses match the expected answer format, with fallback extraction when needed. Statistical sig-nificance is assessed using McNemar’s test on paired outcomes. The baseline generation budget is 256 tokens. CoT experiments use 1,024 tokens, as described in Section 3.2.

Using the decomposition in Equation (1), we verify that the speech improvement is instruction-driven rather than a side ef-fect of the larger token budget. Increasing the generation limit from 256 to 1,024 tokens without changing the instruction pro-duces no improvement on speech input, with ∆ ≤ 0.2 pp for both models. to +9 .1 pp on speech, the entire gain comes from the instruc-tion content, not from additional generation capacity. This dy-namic is visually summarized in Figure 3. This asymmetry di-rectly supports the entity binding hypothesis. Speech inputs suffer from a binding failure that extra tokens cannot resolve, but structured instructions can. Text inputs encode entities in a form the model can already bind, so more tokens enable deeper reasoning.

This confirms that the bottleneck lies in maintaining semantic associations across reasoning steps, not in acoustic perception. Recovery Requires Text-Level Capability. EA-CoT’s ef-ficacy inherently depends on foundational text-level capability. On web of lies , the degree of S2T recovery scales monotoni-cally with T2T accuracy. Both models boast strong T2T base-lines ( > 86% ) and recover substantially in speech ( +24 .4 and 

+16 .8 pp). Output-level analysis reveals the mechanism: Phi-4’s speech baseline mostly produces bare answers (97% of out-puts under 30 characters) despite generating full step-by-step chains on text input. CoT prompting successfully restores this reasoning activation. By bypassing modality-induced bottle-necks, CoT can effectively externalize the binding capability that the SLLM already possesses in its text representation.

# 6. Conclusion 

Entity binding failure is the primary driver of the S2T/T2T modality gap in complex reasoning. We demonstrate that SLLMs maintain robust performance on spatial and factual tasks, but tasks requiring continuous entity tracking collapse to chance-level accuracy on speech inputs. To address this, we in-troduce Entity-Aware Chain-of-Thought (EA-CoT), which by-passes fragile implicit acoustic tracking by forcing models to explicitly enumerate and bind entities in text before reasoning. This intervention bridges the S2T gap by up to +24 .4 pp on entity-centric tasks, effectively repairing the logical chain even when acoustic entities are phonetically misrecognized. While highly effective, EA-CoT is an inference-time prompting strategy that roughly triples token generation and latency. Additionally, our evaluation relies on TTS-generated speech and 7B-class models; real-world acoustic noise and larger architectures may introduce varied compound effects. Future work should explore representation-level architectural alignments, such as intrinsically synchronizing cross-modal en-tity binding subspaces [21], to dynamically stabilize continuous entity tracking without the overhead of explicit prompting.
