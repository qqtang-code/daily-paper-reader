Abstract 

Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) are widely applied to visual document under-standing. However, comprehending long docu-ments remains an issue by the limited context window. Though recent multimodal retrieval-augmented generation (MMRAG) can address this challenge by retrieving relevant pages. It still struggles with the visual question answer-ing (VQA) requiring holistic comprehension of a document. To cope with this, knowledge graph (KG) that summarizes global knowledge of a document can provide an effective solu-tion. However, most existing LLM-based KG construction methods handle only the language modality, leaving the automatic creation of mul-timodal KGs (MMKGs) for visually rich doc-uments largely unexplored. In this paper, we introduce a multimodal graph-based RAG ap-proach to tackle this problem. Existing LLM-based KG methods evaluate the QA perfor-mance relying on indirect evidence such as comprehensiveness, diversity, empowerment, and so on. The lack of annotated datasets for comprehensive document-level VQA poses a significant challenge to effective model evalua-tion. To overcome this limitation, we also intro-duce a new benchmark, DLVQA (document-level VQA), which provides reference sum-maries and corresponding supporting facts for global document-level questions. Experimen-tal results show that our approach outperforms existing MMRAG or KG-based approaches on multi-hop QA/VQA benchmarks and DLVQA. Our code is available on https://github.com/AI-Application-and-Integration-Lab/KG4VD .

KGs and graph-based RAG methods have been ex-tensively studied (Gutiérrez et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2025; Guo et al., 2025b; Edge et al., 2024). How-ever, automatically constructing MMKGs for vi-sually rich documents introduces challenges be-yond text-only graph construction. First, visual document pages have highly uneven information density: a sparse title page and a dense table- or diagram-heavy page should not receive the same KG construction budget. Second, grounding ex-tracted entities and relations to concrete page re-gions is crucial for verification, helping determine whether they actually appear in the document or are hallucinated by the model. Several recent works have investigated MMKG-augmented generation and multimodal graph-based RAG (Lee et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025; Guo et al., 2025a; Hsiao et al., 2026), high-lighting the potential of global multimodal knowl-edge. However, these approaches either depend on manually constructed graphs (Lee et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2025), focus primar-ily on social media imagery rather than visually rich documents (Liu et al., 2025), or leave adaptive, layout-grounded MMKG construction for long vi-sual documents underexplored. To this end, we propose KG4VD, a zero-shot method for constructing and using MM KG s for 

visual documents that contain both textual and vi-sual content. KG4VD grounds page graphs to lay-out components, uses adaptive extraction to handle uneven page complexity, and connects page graphs into a document-level MMKG for long-range rea-soning. During inference, KG4VD reduces noise from direct graph retrieval by anchoring on relevant page images before performing query-adaptive per-sonalized PageRank (PPR) (Jeh and Widom, 2003) expansion over the graph. This design combines page-level visual context with graph-based cross-page reasoning for final answer generation. Reliable evaluation for document-level visual un-derstanding is also underdeveloped. Existing multi-hop QA/VQA benchmarks (Cheng et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024b; Trivedi et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2018) usually require evidence from only a small number of pages (2 ∼7), and therefore do not fully capture document-level understanding over dispersed evi-dence. Although some methods (Guo et al., 2025b; Procko and Ochoa, 2024) automatically generate document-level questions for query-focused sum-marization (Edge et al., 2024), the lack of refer-ence summaries and supporting facts often limits evaluation to LLM-as-a-judge metrics such as com-prehensiveness, diversity, empowerment, and over-all quality (Guo et al., 2025b; Edge et al., 2024), rather than factual correctness. To address this gap, we introduce DLVQA, a document-level VQA benchmark with reference summaries and support-ing facts for grounded evaluation. Our contributions are summarized as follows. 

• We propose KG4VD, a zero-shot method that automatically constructs MMKGs for visually rich documents with varied page complexity. 

• We introduce DLVQA dataset for evaluating document-level VQA and query-focused visual document summarization in terms of faithfulness, completeness, and conciseness. 

• KG4VD outperforms MMRAG and graph-based RAG baselines on multimodal tasks while remain-ing competitive in text-only settings. 

2 Related Work 

MMRAG. Recent MMRAG methods address context-window limitations by retrieving relevant visual document units instead of feeding the whole document to an MLLM. DSE (Ma et al., 2024a) directly encodes document screenshots, preserving visual layout, text, and images in a unified repre-sentation. ColPaLi (Faysse et al., 2025) introduces multi-vector embeddings for document images to capture fine-grained visual cues, and ColQwen im-proves retrieval by replacing PaLI-Gemma (Beyer et al., 2024) with Qwen2.5-VL (Bai et al., 2025). VisRAG (Yu et al., 2025) extends page-image re-trieval to the full RAG pipeline by retrieving doc-ument images and reusing them for answer gener-ation. Beyond page-image retrieval, UniIR (Wei et al., 2024) and GME (Zhang et al., 2025) explore unified multimodal retrieval across text and image. These methods are effective for page- or component-level retrieval, but they do not explic-itly model document-level structure. As a result, they remain limited for questions whose evidence is scattered across pages and connected through recurring entities, visual concepts, or relations. 

Graph-based RAG. Graph-based RAG improves long-range reasoning by organizing documents into entity-relation structures. Earlier KG-augmented generation methods retrieve or reason over sub-graphs from existing KGs (Li et al., 2025; He et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2025), while re-cent LLM-based systems construct graphs directly from raw documents. Despite these advances, existing graph-based RAG methods remain text-centric: they do not ground entities and relations to visual regions or model figures, layouts, and other visual elements that are central to visually rich documents. 

Multimodal Knowledge Graphs. Multimodal knowledge graphs extend conventional KGs by associating entities with visual information (Liu et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2023), and have been studied for KG completion (Mousselly-Sergieh et al., 2018; Xie et al., 2017), recommendation (Sun et al., 2020), image captioning (Zhao and Wu, 2023), and multimodal generation (Bu et al., 2025; Lee et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025). Recent multimodal graph-based RAG meth-ods further adapt MMKGs to document understand-ing. RAGAnything (Guo et al., 2025a) follows a component-level design: it parses documents into text, figures, tables, and equations, then extracts and aligns component-level graphs into a unified MMKG. MegaRAG (Hsiao et al., 2026), in con-trast, operates at the page level. It first builds an initial multimodal graph from page-level evidence, then uses this graph to guide a second graph refine-ment stage, strengthening cross-modal links. 

3 Methodology 

Our method builds on recent MMRAG advances (Ma et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2025; Faysse et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025). While text-only RAG relies on chunking documents into smaller text units, vi-sually rich documents contain figures, tables, and layout cues that require stronger visual and spatial understanding. Recent MMRAG methods there-fore use direct page image embedding for retrieval (Ma et al., 2024a; Faysse et al., 2025), achieving strong performance with models such as GME and ColQwen (Zhang et al., 2025; Faysse et al., 2025). KG4VD follows this page-centric strategy, but ex-tends pure page retrieval with a reusable MMKG that connects page-level evidence through entities and relations to guide final answer generation. 33.1 KG4VD Overview 

Given a document with N pages, KG4VD consists of two phases: (1) an offline MMKG construction phase, and (2) an online retrieval and answer gen-eration phase. Figure 2 shows the overall pipeline. 

Offline MMKG construction. KG4VD builds a reusable document-level MMKG from page-local extractions. This page-level design preserves visual and layout context while allowing pages to be pro-cessed in parallel. Graph construction for visually rich documents raises two main challenges: dense pages may be under-extracted under a fixed budget, and extracted graph elements must be grounded to verifiable page regions. KG4VD addresses these challenges with adaptive extraction and layout-component grounding. Each page is converted into a grounded page graph, and then connected into a document-level MMKG. 

Online retrieval and answer generation. Once the MMKG is built, directly retrieving entities and relations from the whole graph can be noisy because large graphs contain many semantically similar but contextually irrelevant nodes and edges (Gutiérrez et al., 2025). KG4VD instead retrieves relevant page images as entry points, then uses personalized PageRank (PPR) to expand from these anchors through the MMKG. A query ana-lyzer controls the expansion range of PPR accord-ing to the question’s information needs. 

3.2 MMKG Construction 

We define the multimodal knowledge graph as 

G = ( V, E), where each node v ∈ V represents an entity and each edge e ∈ E represents a relation between two entities. Each entity contains a name ,

type , modality tag , and description . The modal-ity tag indicates whether the entity is text-derived, such as a person or organization mentioned in a paragraph, or visual, such as a chart element, di-agram region, illustrated character, or object in a figure. Each relation contains its source entity , tar-get entity , relation label , and description .KG4VD performs extraction at the page level to preserve visual, textual, and layout context. In addition to extracting entities and relations, each extracted item is grounded to concrete page regions, making the graph easier to inspect and verify. 

Document Preprocess. For each page Pi, we ren-der a page image Ii. A direct approach would ask the MLLM to extract entities and relations while predicting their bounding boxes from Ii, but free-form coordinate prediction is unreliable on com-plex document pages. KG4VD therefore reframes localization as visual region selection. Following a Set-of-Marks (SoM) strategy (Yang et al., 2023), we use an effective document parser (Wang et al., 2024) to detect layout components on the page: 

Ci = {ci, 1, c i, 2, . . . , c i,M i }. Each component ci,j 

corresponds to a parser-detected page region. It contains a unique component identifier, such as P1 

for a paragraph, T1 for a table, or IM1 for an im-age region; a parser-provided bounding box; and parsed content when available, such as OCR text or table markdown. We draw each component’s bounding box and identifier tag on Ii to obtain the annotated page image ˆIi. The MLLM is then re-quired to cite component identifiers for extracted entities and relations instead of generating coor-dinates directly. These region citations are later resolved to corresponding bounding boxes.

G = ( V, E). Existing KG systems (Edge et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025b) typically merge or dedu-plicate entities based on entity names. However, the same entity may appear under different surface forms across pages. KG4VD performs cross-page connection in three steps: candidate entity retrieval, MLLM judg-ing, and canonicalization. For each entity v ∈ V ∗ 

> i

,KG4VD first retrieves top-ranked candidate enti-ties from other pages using entity embedding sim-ilarity, as detailed in Section 3.2.3. A cross-page MLLM judge then compares v with its retrieved candidates and predicts whether each pair refers to the same entity, is merely related, or is unrelated. When grounded visual regions are available, the judge considers both entity textual descriptions and cropped image evidence, helping align visually am-biguous entities that cannot be reliably matched by name alone. If two entities are judged to be the same, KG4VD first connects them with a same-entity link. We then perform entity fusion/canonicalization fol-lowing standard entity resolution practice (Bhat-tacharya and Getoor, 2007; Hofer et al., 2024): each same-entity cluster is merged into a single document-level node v ∈ V . Relations incident to merged entities are redirected to the canonical node, and duplicate edges are removed. Candi-date pairs judged as related but not identical can be retained as new cross-page relation links in E,while unrelated pairs are discarded. The result is the document-level MMKG G.

3.2.3 Multimodal Indexing 

To support retrieval over both page images {Ii}Ni=1 

and the constructed MMKG G, KG4VD builds a multimodal index over page-level and entity-level representations. We use a unified multimodal en-coder, GME, to embed all representations into the same shared embedding space. For page-level in-dexing, KG4VD encodes each page image Ii as 

zPi = GME( Ii), preserving visual context such as layout, figures, diagrams, and tables. Entity-level indexing provides fine-grained ac-cess to graph nodes in V. For each textual entity 

v ∈ V , KG4VD concatenates its name, type, and 5description into a text string xv, and encodes it as 

zv = GME( xv). For each visual entity v ∈ V ,KG4VD combines its textual description with its grounded image crop. The crop is obtained from the parser-provided bounding boxes of the source components cited by v. The visual entity is then en-coded as zv = GME( xv, crop( v)) , allowing graph nodes such as chart elements, diagram regions, il-lustrated characters, and objects in figures to be retrieved through either textual or visual cues. The final index consists of a page index Ipage =

{zPi }Ni=1 and an entity index Ient = {zv : v ∈ V} .

3.3 Retrieval and Answer Generation 

Direct retrieval over the full MMKG can intro-duce substantial noise (Gutiérrez et al., 2025). KG4VD follows the PPR-based graph retrieval strategy of HippoRAG2, but adapts it to visu-ally rich documents with page-image anchoring and query-adaptive graph diffusion. Specifically, KG4VD first anchors retrieval on relevant page im-ages and then applies PPR for controlled expansion over the MMKG G. Given a query q, an MLLM-based query analyzer predicts one of three expan-sion modes: local lookup , multi-hop reasoning , or 

document-level reasoning . These modes determine how far PPR should diffuse from the anchor-page entities: local lookup stays close to the anchor page for fact-seeking questions, multi-hop reasoning ex-pands through connected entities and relations for compositional questions, and document-level rea-soning allows broader multi-page exploration for questions that require document-level reasoning. KG4VD retrieves a small set of relevant page images from the page index Ipage and treats them as visual anchors. Entities grounded to these an-chor pages are used as seed nodes, and their restart weights are initialized using their query similarity scores from the entity index Ient . Thus, among entities on the anchor pages, those more similar to 

q receive higher initial PPR probabilities. Unlike fixed-depth graph expansion, the query analyzer controls the diffusion range according to the pre-dicted expansion mode. This limits unnecessary graph diffusion for local lookup queries while still collecting dispersed evidence for multi-hop reason-ing and document-level reasoning queries. The expansion retrieves relevant entities, connected re-lations, and the page images grounded to those graph elements. To further reduce noise, KG4VD filters the ex-panded candidates with a reranker, retaining the top-K entities and relations and the top-M page images. Following the two-stage generation de-sign of MegaRAG, KG4VD first uses an MLLM to organize the retrieved entities and relations into a preliminary answer, and then combines this pre-liminary answer with the retrieved page images to produce the final response grounded in the original document context. 

4 DLVQA Dataset 

DLVQA targets document-level VQA over visu-ally rich documents, where questions require ag-gregating evidence across many pages rather than retrieving a small local span. We build it with a human-in-the-loop pipeline assisted by Gemini-3.1-pro-preview . For each document, the model first produces page and document summaries, then extracts grounded textual and visual atomic facts from page images. Annotators revise summaries and facts for faithfulness and grounding. We then cluster related facts across pages and generate document-level VQA instances from re-tained long-range clusters. Each instance contains a question, answer summary guidance, a refer-ence summary, and gold supporting facts. Annota-tors validate question quality, citation correctness, supporting-fact coverage, hallucinations, concise-ness, and page span; questions supported by fewer than 10 pages are removed. The final dataset con-tains 525 VQA pairs over 3,441 pages from en-vironmental reports, a history textbook, computer vision slides, and picture books. Additional con-struction details, statistics, and examples are pro-vided in the appendix A.

Baselines. We compare KG4VD with retrieval-based and graph-based baselines, including No Documents , NaiveRAG, VisRAG (Yu et al., 2025), ColQwen (Faysse et al., 2025), GME (Zhang et al., 2025), GraphRAG (Edge et al., 2024), Ligh-tRAG (Guo et al., 2025b), HippoRAG2 (Gutiérrez et al., 2025), RAGAnything (Guo et al., 2025a), and MegaRAG (Hsiao et al., 2026). Metrics. For short-answer QA, we use an LLM judge to evaluate semantic equivalence and report accuracy. For DLVQA, we use reference-based FineSurE metrics (Song et al., 2024): Faithful-ness , Completeness , and Conciseness . We addition-ally report GraphRAG-style reference-free win-rate metrics in Appendix C; judge evaluation details are provided in Appendix E. 

Implementation Details. We use GPT-4o-mini 

with temperature 0 for graph construction and an-swer generation, and all methods share the same answer prompt. All page-retrieval methods use a top-10 page budget, and graph-based methods use the same predefined entity type schema when applicable, except HippoRAG2, which is schema-free. For KG4VD, we use GME for multimodal indexing and text-embedding-3-small for text-only settings. The adaptive extraction loop is capped at 5 rounds, and cross-page alignment compares each entity with its top-10 candidates. DLVQA is constructed with a human-in-the-loop pipeline assisted by Gemini-3.1-pro-preview ; further imple-mentation details are in the appendix B. We will release the code and dataset upon publication.

6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we presented KG4VD, a graph-based RAG method for automatically constructing and using MMKGs from visually rich documents. KG4VD addresses key challenges in document-level VQA through adaptive page-level extraction, which handles varying page complexity; cross-page connection, which links recurring entities and ev-idence across pages; and page-anchored, query-adaptive graph retrieval, which balances visual grounding and long-range reasoning. We also in-troduced DLVQA, a document-level VQA bench-mark with reference summaries and supporting facts for more faithful evaluation. Experiments on open VQA benchmarks and DLVQA show that KG4VD outperforms strong MMRAG and graph-based baselines. Future work will explore combin-ing KG4VD with thinking-capable graph reasoning methods, such as Think-on-Graph (Ma et al., 2025), to further improve deep multimodal reasoning. 8Limitations 

While KG4VD demonstrates strong performance, it still has several limitations. First, its retrieval process mainly expands evidence from the con-structed MMKG rather than performing explicit multi-step graph reasoning. Integrating KG4VD with reasoning-oriented graph traversal methods such as Think-on-Graph (Ma et al., 2025) could enable deeper evidence reasoning, but doing so efficiently over large multimodal graphs remains an open challenge. Second, KG4VD currently fo-cuses on textual and visual modalities. Real-world applications may involve richer signals, such as au-dio, video, animations, or interactive content. Ex-tending MMKG construction and retrieval to these modalities would broaden document-level multi-modal reasoning, while requiring new grounding, indexing, and evaluation strategies. We leave these directions for future work. 

Ethical Considerations 

All documents used in this work are publicly avail-able. We do not collect private user data or infer per-sonally identifying information beyond what may already appear in the original sources. KG4VD relies on MLLMs for graph construction, cross-page judging, and answer generation, and these models may produce incorrect or biased outputs. To mitigate this risk, KG4VD grounds extracted entities and relations to source page regions and generates answers from retrieved subgraphs and source pages, making outputs more inspectable and verifiable against the underlying documents.

Page and document summarization. For each document, the MLLM first produces a summary for every page image. The page summaries are then aggregated into a document-level summary. Anno-tators revise document summaries for faithfulness, coverage, and clarity. The revised document sum-maries provide global context for subsequent fact extraction and question generation. 

Atomic fact extraction. The MLLM extracts self-contained textual and visual atomic facts from each page, conditioned on the page image, the page sum-mary, and the document-level summary. Each fact is grounded with page-level provenance. Textual facts describe information stated in paragraphs, tables, or other text regions, while visual facts describe information conveyed by figures, charts, maps, diagrams, or illustrations. Annotators re-move facts that are redundant, weakly grounded, or not useful for document-level reasoning, revise facts with unclear or incomplete wording, and add missing facts when necessary. 

Topic clustering and selection. We encode atomic facts using OpenAI’s text-embedding-3-large 

model and cluster the resulting fact embeddings across pages with HDBSCAN (McInnes et al., 2017). This step groups semantically related evi-dence without requiring a fixed number of clus-ters and filters sparse noise points. The result-ing clusters serve as candidate evidence groups for document-level questions. Annotators remove clusters that are noisy, incoherent, or insufficiently useful for long-range reasoning. 

QA generation and validation. For each retained cluster, the MLLM generates a document-level VQA draft using the cluster facts and the revised document summary. Each draft includes a question, a reference summary following STORM (Shao et al., 2024), answer summary guidance, and gold supporting facts. The answer summary guidance specifies only the expected response style, orga-nization, and length range, such as requiring a Wikipedia-style article with Markdown section headings and flowing paragraphs. It does not reveal the reference summary, gold supporting facts, or any question-specific evidence. This guidance is provided to all evaluated methods to make long-form outputs more comparable and to reduce varia-tion caused by formatting choices or overly short and verbose responses. Annotators revise unclear or inaccurate QA drafts, remove invalid drafts, and verify that the answer is supported by its associ-ated gold facts. The final dataset contains only QA pairs whose questions, reference summaries, and supporting facts are suitable for evaluating faithful-ness, completeness, and conciseness. 

A.4 Human Review and Agreement 

Table 6 summarizes the construction and review statistics for DLVQA. The left block reports the inputs and outputs of MLLM-assisted construction stages, while the right block reports human curation actions and the artifacts retained after review. 

Two-reviewer protocol. For stages that require human judgment, two annotators independently inspect the MLLM-assisted outputs using stage-specific guidelines. Before any discussion, each annotator records a binary decision, such as keep-/revise or valid/invalid, and suggests edits when needed. We compute agreement and Cohen’s kappa from these independent pre-adjudication decisions. After agreement is measured, the two annotators discuss disagreements and reach a consensus de-cision for each disputed item. Artifacts accepted by both annotators are retained. Artifacts with fix-able issues, such as factual errors, unclear wording, 14 incomplete evidence, or incorrect grounding, are revised according to the consensus decision. Arti-facts judged invalid or not reliably correctable are removed. Thus, the agreement scores in Table 6 reflect reviewer consistency before adjudication, whereas the curation counts and retained artifacts reflect the final consensus-reviewed dataset.

Reference summary Prehistory and Early Human Settlements Early human material culture encompasses Acheulean and Mousterian stone tools alongside physical remnants ranging from marsupial lions to hominin fossils like Lucy. Early human creativity is preserved in Venus statuettes carved to represent fertility. Prominent examples include the forty-thousand-year-old Venus of Hohle Fels, a suspended two-inch figure carved from mammoth ivory, and the corpulent Venus of Willendorf, theorized by some to be a self-portrait. Prehistoric environments were captured by forty-thousand-year-old Spanish cave paintings depicting prey, utilizing natural rock protrusions to create three-dimensional depth within restricted spaces likely meant for ritual exhibition rather than public viewing. As the transition into the Neolithic era continued, monumental architecture and permanent settlements emerged at foundational archaeological sites like Göbekli Tepe and the walls of Jericho. Within the complex layout of Çatalhöyük, inhabitants venerated bulls through elaborate interior murals describing animal sacrifices directed toward a mother-deity, while genuine physical bull horns were explicitly mounted onto domestic furniture. Ancient Civilizations and the Dawn of Writing Urban settlement coalesced in Sumer at places like Uruk, leading to the invention of a phonetic cuneiform script around 3000 BCE. This system evolved from clay tokens encapsulated within bullae representing agricultural exchange, eventually growing into sophisticated stylus imprints detailing civic transactions. Indexing Cost. KG4VD builds the MMKG in a one-time offline process. For each page, KG4VD parses layout components and constructs a layout-grounded page graph with adaptive extractor– reflector loops. E LLM Judges and Evaluation Details 

E.1 Judge Models and Aggregation Evaluation criteria. For reference-based DLVQA evaluation, we follow FineSurE (Song et al., 2024) and measure Faithfulness , Completeness , and Con-ciseness . Faithfulness measures whether the an-swer is supported by the reference summary and supporting facts; Completeness measures whether it covers the required evidence; and Conciseness 

measures whether it avoids unsupported, redun-dant, or irrelevant content. For reference-free pair-wise evaluation, we follow GraphRAG-style evalu-ation criteria (Edge et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025b): 

Comprehensiveness , Diversity , Empowerment , and 

Overall . These criteria measure coverage of impor-tant information, breadth of relevant perspectives, usefulness for understanding the topic, and aggre-gate answer quality. 

Judge models. We use two LLM judges gpt-5.4-mini and gemini-3.1-flash-lite . Using two model families reduces reliance on a single judge and helps mitigate model-specific bias. 

FineSurE aggregation. For DLVQA, each judge scores every method independently. Since different judges may use different score ranges, we normal-ize each method’s score by the oracle score from the same judge: ¯sj,b /¯sj, oracle , where j is the judge and b is the evaluated method. The oracle score is computed by evaluating the reference summary with the same judge. We report the unweighted average of the two normalized scores. 

VQA accuracy aggregation. For MMLongBench-Doc-2k, each judge gives a binary correctness ver-dict. We average the two verdicts directly. 

Pairwise win-rate aggregation. For reference-free pairwise evaluation, each judge evaluates every an-swer pair in both answer orders to reduce positional bias. A win is counted only if the judge selects the same winner in both orders; order-dependent verdicts are treated as ties. We then average the outcomes across the two judges. 

Inter-judge agreement. We also measure agree-ment between the two LLM judges. For DLVQA FineSurE, we report the range of per-query Pearson correlations across evaluated methods. The corre-lations are moderate for faithfulness ( r = 0 .47 –

0.70 ) and conciseness ( r = 0 .49 –0.70 ), and lower for completeness ( r = 0 .22 –0.48 ), reflecting the more subjective nature of evidence coverage. For MMLongBench-Doc-2k, binary VQA judgments are highly consistent across major methods, with agreement ranging from 97 .6% to 99 .7% and Co-hen’s κ ranging from 0.948 to 0.994 .
