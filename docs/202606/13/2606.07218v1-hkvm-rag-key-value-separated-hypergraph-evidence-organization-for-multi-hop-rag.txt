# HKVM-RAG: Key-Value-Separated Hypergraph Evidence Organization for Multi-Hop RAG

Abstract —Multi-hop RAG poses a data-engineering problem beyond passage matching: under fixed retrieval budgets, a system must organize retrieved text into evidence units that expose answer chains. Dense retrievers score passages independently, while graph-based memories make associations explicit but often rely on pairwise or entity-centered keys that fragment multi-hop evidence. We present HKVM-RAG, a key-value-separated evidence-organization layer. It assembles answer-path hyperedges from cached passage-level LLM evidence tuples and uses them as retrieval keys, while retaining passage text as answer values. To isolate key-space design, our fixed-substrate protocol holds the tuple cache, candidate passages, reader, and evaluation budget constant across pairwise graph and hypergraph variants. Weighted hypergraph key-value retrieval improves over KG-PPR by +3.426 F1 on 2WikiMultiHopQA and +3.592 F1 on MuSiQue; HotpotQA shows that higher structured support coverage need not yield standalone answer-F1 gains. We therefore study WHG-KV as an evidence-control signal rather than a dense-retrieval replacement. Oracle and train-to-dev analyses identify support selection as repairable, and a dense-aware controller combines frozen ColBERTv2 and HKVM rank/score features using out-of-fold HKVM predictions. It reaches 88.846, 65.073, and 85.810 F1 on the three benchmarks, improving over ColBERTv2 by +11.084, +6.763, and +5.966 F1. Source-level ablations show that matched non-WHG structured signals do not match the WHG-KV gains. These results provide bounded evidence that key-value-separated hypergraph organization can serve as a reusable evidence-control mechanism for multi-hop RAG. 1

Index Terms —retrieval-augmented generation, evidence index-ing, hypergraph retrieval, key-value memory, multi-hop question answering

In multi-hop question answering benchmarks [7]–[9], the retriever must therefore do more than find in-dividually relevant passages. It must organize passages into evidence units that preserve how facts connect across hops. A system can fail even when the answer-bearing text is present in the corpus if the retrieved context does not expose the evidence chain needed by the reader. Two major retrieval routes address different parts of this problem. Lexical, dense, and late-interaction retrievers are strong matching models [10]–[13], but their scores do not explicitly encode the structured evidence units used by a multi-hop answer. Graph-based RAG and knowledge-graph mem-ory systems make associations explicit by indexing extracted entities and relations and traversing the resulting graph [4]– [6], [14], [15]. This structure is useful, but the retrieval keys remain primarily pairwise or entity-centered graph objects. When answer evidence is better represented as a group of connected facts, flattening that group into independent edges can weaken the retrieval signal. tation for higher-order relations [16]–[19], and recent RAG systems have begun to explore hypergraph-driven retrieval and memory [20], [21]. The remaining systems question is how to test such higher-order retrieval keys without changing the underlying evidence substrate at the same time. Improvements can become ambiguous when multiple experimental axes change together. Extraction design and model scale are two major confounds. Online filtering budgets and high-order re-lation schemas add further confounds. The result may reflect a stronger extraction pipeline rather than the key structure itself. This paper isolates the key-space question: given the same evidence tuple cache and candidate passages, does an answer-path hypergraph key space organize multi-hop evidence better than a pairwise graph key space? We propose HKVM-RAG, a structured evidence-indexing design for testing this mechanism. In HKVM-RAG, HKVM names an operational key-value memory abstraction rather than a biological claim: extracted entities, pairwise edges, answer-path hyperedges, and confidence weights form the retrieval-key side, while passage text remains the value side used for answer extraction. The system constructs answer-path hyperedges from LLM-extracted evidence tuples and uses them as retrieval keys over passage values. This design gives the paper two connected tests. The first asks whether hypergraph keys improve evidence organization over pairwise graph keys under a fixed extraction substrate. The second asks whether the resulting hypergraph key scores are useful as control signals alongside strong lexical and dense passage scores. Figure 1 contrasts the three retrieval views: dense scoring over separate passage values, query-seeded pairwise KG keys with provenance links to passages, and a single answer-path hyperedge key that maps back to multiple passage values. The evaluation follows this mechanism question rather than a leaderboard narrative. We ask four bounded ques-tions: whether answer-path hypergraph keys improve over pairwise graph keys under a shared tuple cache; whether standalone failures reflect extraction limits or support-selection errors; whether WHG-KV rank/score signals complement Col-BERTv2 in a train-to-dev controller; and whether the gain persists when the structured source or first-stage retriever is changed. The answers are correspondingly scoped. Weighted-HG-KV improves over a HippoRAG-style KG-PPR reference by +3.426 F1 on 2WikiMultiHopQA and +3.592 F1 on MuSiQue, but HotpotQA shows that standalone structure is not a dense-retrieval replacement. Oracle answer-path selection raises 2WikiMultiHopQA F1 from 79.299 to 90.884 and MuSiQue F1 from 39.925 to 74.330, identifying support selec-tion as a major bottleneck. A dense-aware controller reaches 88.846 F1 on 2WikiMultiHopQA, 65.073 F1 on MuSiQue, and 85.810 F1 on HotpotQA, while source-level diagnostics show that matched non-WHG sources do not match the gains. These results position HKVM-RAG as a lightweight evidence-organization component whose key-side signal can comple-ment lexical and dense retrievers under the tested protocol. The contributions are:  

> •

We formulate multi-hop RAG retrieval as fixed-budget evidence organization and instantiate HKVM-RAG as a key-value-separated framework with hypergraph key-side objects and passage values.  

> •

We define a controlled comparison protocol that isolates key-space design by holding the evidence tuple cache, candidate passages, reader, and evaluation budget fixed across pairwise graph and hypergraph rows.  

> •

We diagnose the standalone hypergraph failure mode with oracle gap decomposition and train-to-dev support scoring, identifying support selection and ranking as a major repairable bottleneck on MuSiQue under the current cached extraction substrate.  

> •

We introduce a dense-aware HKVM controller and report train-to-dev gains over ColBERTv2 and learned HKVM calibration, with source-level and first-stage diagnostics showing that the gain is WHG-KV-specific under the tested protocol rather than a generic effect of adding any structured prediction source. We release a reproducibility-oriented artifact that includes code, processing scripts, frozen manifests, prediction files, and redistributable processed outputs needed to verify the paper-facing tables and figures. II. R ELATED WORK 

A. Lexical, Dense, and Multi-Hop Retrieval 

We organize related work by the data state each line contributes to our controlled evidence-indexing view: passage scoring, graph-key memory, and hypergraph key-value mem-ory. Retrieval-augmented generation and retrieval-augmented language-model pre-training show that external evidence can improve knowledge-intensive NLP and open-domain question answering [1], [2]. Retrieval-reader architectures such as FiD fuse independently encoded retrieved passages during genera-tion [3]. At the retriever level, BM25 remains a robust lexical reference point [10], dense passage retrieval learns query and passage representations in a shared vector space [11], Contriever represents unsupervised dense retrieval [12], and ColBERTv2 uses late interaction to retain token-level match-ing signals [13]. At the systems layer, dense retrieval depends on approximate nearest-neighbor and vector-data infrastructure such as HNSW, FAISS, anisotropic vector quantization, and vector database systems [31]–[34]. Multi-hop retrieval systems such as MDR, Baleen, and IRCoT make evidence gathering iterative or reasoning-guided [22]–[24]. We include these sys-tems as strong matching and multi-hop retrieval context, while asking a complementary question: under the same extracted evidence substrate and answer budget, does changing the structured key space from pairwise KG edges to answer-path hyperedges improve passage organization? 

B. Knowledge-Graph Memory for RAG 

Graph-based RAG and KG-augmented reasoning meth-ods organize evidence through entity-relation graphs or KG traversal [14], [15]. This connects RAG evidence retrieval to a broader data-management setting in which querying knowledge graphs, including graph traversal and path-oriented access, is a long-standing problem [35]. Knowledge-graph memory systems make associations explicit by indexing ex-tracted entities and relations and then traversing the resulting graph. HippoRAG organizes LLM-extracted triples into aKG and uses personalized PageRank for associative retrieval [4]. HippoRAG 2 strengthens this line with passage nodes, query-to-triple linking, and online LLM triple filtering [5]. EcphoryRAG follows a related entity-centric route, where cue entities activate entity-centered engrams for associative search [6]. These systems support the premise that explicit structure can help multi-hop retrieval, but their retrieval keys remain pairwise or entity-centered graph units, often coupled with additional online filtering. HippoRAG 2 and EcphoryRAG are the closest contem-poraneous KG-memory systems to our setting [5], [6]. We position them as full-system KG-memory references, while our numeric matrix uses a controlled key-space isolation pro-tocol. HippoRAG 2 uses Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct, NV-Embed-v2, and online LLM triple filtering [5]; EcphoryRAG reports Phi4/Ollama, bge-m3 embeddings, 500 random questions per dataset, and dataset-specific context tuning [6]. These dif-ferences in models, splits, prompts, filtering policies, and context budgets would change several axes at once, so the main comparison isolates pairwise KG keys and answer-path hypergraph keys under a shared substrate. 

C. Hypergraph Retrieval and Key-Value Memory 

Hypergraph neural networks and representation-learning methods model higher-order relations that are not captured cleanly by independent pairwise edges [16]–[19]. Hyper-RAG and HGMem bring related hypergraph ideas into RAG and working memory [20], [21]. Our construction is narrower: answer-path hyperedges are assembled from the same evidence tuple cache used by the pairwise KG-PPR baseline, so the experiment tests whether changing the retrieval key space improves evidence organization under a fixed extraction sub-strate. We include Co-occurrence-HG as a Hyper-RAG-style matched source constructed from the same cached evidence tuples and candidate passages. This row tests whether ageneric co-occurrence hypergraph source is sufficient under the same controller protocol, while avoiding additional ex-traction, high-order schema, filtering, retrieval-pipeline, and answer-assembly differences. The memory framing is also scoped. End-to-end and key-value memory networks support multi-hop reads over exter-nal memory [25], [26], Modern Hopfield networks connect associative memory and attention [27], and cognitive key-value memory motivates separating retrieval keys from stored values [28]. HKVM-RAG adopts this separation operationally: graph or hypergraph objects form the traversal key side, while passages remain the value side used for answer extraction. We use these analogies to motivate retrieval organization, not to claim biological fidelity. III. P ROBLEM FORMULATION 

Given a query q and a candidate passage set P = {pi}, the evidence-organization layer must rank passages and produce an answer context. We study this layer after candidate passage construction, rather than claiming a new full-corpus indexing stack. This makes key-space design a controlled intervention over fixed candidate passages, extracted evidence, and answer modules. A dense retriever assigns direct scores s(q, p i) to candidate passages, treating them primarily as separate values. Each passage pi is also processed by an LLM extractor to produce evidence tuples Ri = {(h, r, t, p i, c f , c s, c b)}. Let

HKVM-RAG builds a hypergraph H = ( V, E h) over the same vertices and the same extracted tuple cache, where each hyperedge connects a group of vertices and passage ids that participate in a candidate answer path. We distinguish memory keys from memory values. Nodes, pairwise edges, hyperedges, and their weights define the key space used for retrieval. Passage text remains the value side used for answer support. A pairwise key links two entities at a time, whereas an answer-path hyperedge can serve as one higher-order key that binds several entities and then maps score mass back to its associated passage values. All structured methods finally produce passage scores s(pi) and pass the top-ranked passages to the same answer module. The central comparison is therefore not between different answer modules, but between two structured key spaces over the same extracted evidence: pairwise KG-PPR and answer-path hypergraph traversal. IV. M ETHOD 

A. Overview 

HKVM-RAG is a structured evidence-organization pro-cedure over a fixed candidate-passage substrate. The unit of comparison is a query-specific evidence table containing candidate passage ids, dense prediction scores, extracted tuple fields, structured prediction scores, and final answer-module inputs. Figure 2 tracks these data states rather than presenting a separate end-to-end retrieval pipeline. First, an LLM creates a seed-invariant passage-level evidence tuple cache, where each tuple contains a local relation triple, its passage id, and confi-dence fields (h, r, t, p i, c f , c s, c b); the extractor does not emit direct high-order hyperedges. Second, HKVM consumes these tuple fields: entity heads and tails define key vertices, shared bridge vertices assemble answer-path hyperedge keys, passage ids define value addresses, and extractor confidence fields weight key-side objects before query-seeded diffusion maps key scores back to passage-value scores. Third, in the dense-aware setting, frozen ColBERTv2 predictions and frozen HKVM predictions are aligned by passage id into per-passage rank/score features, and a passage-level controller learns to reorder the final evidence context. Table III tests whether changing the key space from pairwise tuple components to answer-path hyperedges improves evidence organization under the same substrate. The controller setting tests whether the same hypergraph signal is more useful as a complement to dense candidates than as a standalone retriever. 

B. Shared LLM Evidence Tuple Extraction 

All structured methods start from a shared passage-level evidence tuple cache. The relation component is triple-only: the extractor emits local (h, r, t ) relation triples with passage ids and confidence fields, but does not emit direct high-order hyperedges. For each passage pi, the extractor returns a small set of evidence tuples 

(h, r, t, p i, c f , c s, c b), (1) where h and t are entity strings, r is a relation phrase, and 

cf , cs, and cb denote factual confidence, semantic salience, and bridge potential. The extraction schema is passage-level and does not ask the LLM to produce high-order hyperedges directly. This keeps the LLM output local and auditable, while leaving cross-passage grouping to the deterministic answer-path assembly step. Evidence tuple extraction is cached once and treated as seed-invariant. The three evaluation seeds reuse the same extracted tuples and do not trigger new LLM calls. This is important for the comparison because it makes the structured rows differ in key-space construction and traversal rather than in repeated stochastic extraction.

F. Dense-Aware Evidence Controller 

The standalone structured comparison shows whether hy-pergraph keys improve over pairwise graph keys, but it does not assume that hypergraph retrieval should replace dense re-trieval. The dense-aware controller instead treats ColBERTv2 and HKVM outputs as two frozen prediction bundles over the same candidate passage ids. For each query-passage row, we align the dense and HKVM predictions by passage id and form a per-passage feature vector containing ColBERTv2 rank and score, HKVM rank and score, reciprocal-rank features, nor-malized score features, and dense-HKVM interaction terms. These are inference-time ranking signals; the controller does not use dev labels at inference time and does not run a new dense encoder or a new LLM extraction pass. We use three names for the train-to-dev ranking stages. The train-derived support scorer is the first HKVM-only passage ranker trained from train-side support labels over cached structured outputs. The learned HKVM calibration scorer is the stronger HKVM-only calibration variant over candidate-evidence features. The dense-aware controller is the final passage-level ranker that adds dense rank/score features to the HKVM signals. All three stages are trained on train-side labels and evaluated on frozen development predictions. The controller is a passage-level logistic ranker trained on train-side support labels. To avoid train-side HKVM stacking artifacts, the final paper-facing run uses out-of-fold HKVM predictions for the controller’s train-side HKVM inputs. Each train example is predicted only by a model trained on the other folds, while dev evaluation uses the fixed learned HKVM calibration predictions. The final configuration uses 80 epochs, learning rate 0.01, L2 = 10 −4, negative ratio 8, top-10 candidate retrieval, and top-5 answer context. We report dense-only, HKVM-only, RRF fusion, and dense+HKVM controller variants to separate source-only evidence, simple list-fusion effects, and learned passage-level control. For the source-level controller ablation, the first-stage pre-diction source, train-to-dev split, controller family, and hy-perparameters are held fixed while the structured bundle is swapped. The primary ablation uses ColBERTv2 as the first-stage source and replaces only the structured prediction bun-dle supplied to the controller, using matched Co-occurrence-HG, Static-HG, Weighted-KG-PPR, or WHG-KV predictions over the same datasets and seeds. We then repeat the same diagnostic with BM25 and Contriever in the first-stage slot to test whether the WHG-KV ordering is tied to late-interaction ColBERTv2. The non-WHG train-side prediction files are generated from existing cached train extraction artifacts, with no new LLM extraction calls, and the dev-side prediction files reuse the same frozen structured outputs used by the main result matrix. V. E XPERIMENTAL SETUP 

A. Evaluation Splits and Metrics 

The experimental setup fixes data states before comparing evidence-organization choices, rather than ranking unrestricted retrieval systems. We evaluate on three public multi-hop QA benchmarks: 2WikiMultiHopQA [8], MuSiQue [9], and HotpotQA [7]. This is the same family of benchmarks used by HippoRAG [4], but we report our exact official development splits. Table I summarizes the exact splits: the full 2Wiki-MultiHopQA development set, the MuSiQue development answerable subset, and the HotpotQA development distractor set. No hidden-test or server-submission results are included in the current result matrix. Metrics are all-recall at 10 (AR@10), answer F1, and exact match (EM). AR@10 measures whether all gold supporting passages are present in the top-10 retrieved passages. F1 and EM are computed from the common extractive answer output. The fixed-substrate key-space comparison uses seed-13 unique-example paired bootstrap with 1,000 samples and 

α = 0 .05 . Train-to-dev controller and source-level diagnostics use 5,000-sample paper-export paired bootstrap over frozen prediction files; the supplementary material reports the corre-sponding provenance and intervals by export family. 

B. Matched-Budget Protocol 

All methods share the same passage preprocessing and answer-module budget: 1,200-token chunks with 100-token overlap and top-10 retrieval. The extractive answer module receives the top five passages under a 3,000-token context budget. This setup evaluates evidence indexing and passage ordering within a controlled candidate-passage substrate; it is not intended to measure deployment-time corpus indexing throughput or full-corpus retrieval recall. The answer module and evaluation script are fixed across rows, so the main comparison concerns retrieval and memory organization rather than answer-module variation. 

C. Methods 

The main table includes BM25, Contriever, ColBERTv2, KG-PPR, Weighted-KG-PPR, Co-occurrence-HG, Static-HG, and Weighted-HG-KV. This covers lexical retrieval, dense retrieval, pairwise structured memory, matched co-occurrence hypergraph memory, unweighted answer-path hypergraph memory, and weighted hypergraph key memory within the same candidate-passage budget. ColBERTv2 is implemented as candidate-passage late interaction over the same candidate chunks, not as a separate corpus-level ColBERT index. The Co-occurrence-HG row is a Hyper-RAG-style matched base-line constructed from the same shared evidence tuple cache and candidate passages; it is included to control for generic co-occurrence hypergraph structure, not to reproduce the original Hyper-RAG pipeline. 

D. Fairness and Reproducibility 

Dense and structured rows were merged from compatible source runs because of PyTorch/CUDA compatibility con-straints. The merge happens at the prediction-table level: each row preserves the query id, passage id, method score, seed, split, and evaluation setting recorded in the source manifest. The comparison preserves identical data splits, candidate-passage budgets, evaluation metrics, answer-module settings, and seed set (13, 29, and 43). This controlled-workload design follows the same broad benchmarking principle used in database evaluation: fixed workloads and explicit evaluation states make system comparisons more interpretable [36]. The merged manifest records the source split and has no missing rows. This separation ensures that dense encoding does not share GPU memory or runtime state with structured key-space construction, making measured metrics independently attributable. 

E. LLM Extraction Disclosure 

Structured methods use DeepSeek V4 Flash with a deter-ministic relation-triple extraction schema: no direct high-order extraction, temperature 0.0, passage-level extraction units, and at most five evidence tuples per passage. Table II reports the fixed extraction scope used by the structured rows in the main result matrix; seeds 13, 29, and 43 reuse the same cached evidence tuples rather than regenerating extraction outputs. The train-side extraction scopes are used only for the train-to-dev support scorer, learned HKVM calibration, and dense-aware controller across 2WikiMultiHopQA, MuSiQue, and HotpotQA. Gold support is not sent to the extractor; train support labels are used only after cache construction to train the support scorer, learned HKVM calibration, and dense-aware controller. The tuple cache is intentionally shared and auditable, but it is sparser than our earlier heuristic OpenIE cache and is not claimed to improve the pairwise KG baseline by itself. The extractor is implemented behind a provider abstraction compatible with OpenAI-style endpoints; replacing the endpoint requires regenerating and reporting a new extraction manifest. We treat these cached evidence tuples as reproducibil-ity artifacts rather than as new benchmark labels. Released cache files will mark tuple records as AI-generated extraction outputs, preserve source-dataset attributions, and follow the redistribution limits imposed by dataset licenses and provider terms. The artifact plan below specifies the code, manifests, scripts, and hashes used to verify or regenerate the frozen result matrix.

A. Fixed-Substrate Structured Memory Comparison 

To test whether key-space design matters, we first hold extraction, passages, and answer generation fixed. Table III reports the complete method matrix. The primary compari-son is between the pairwise graph reference, KG-PPR, and the answer-path hypergraph key memory, Weighted-HG-KV. These rows share the same evidence tuple cache and differ in the retrieval key structure. Under this fixed substrate, answer-path hypergraph keys improve over the pairwise graph reference on the two datasets where standalone structure helps answer extraction. On 2WikiMultiHopQA, Weighted-HG-KV reaches 79.299 F1 and 78.650 EM, compared with 75.873 F1 and 75.358 EM for KG-PPR. On MuSiQue, it reaches 39.925 F1 and 39.222 EM, compared with 36.332 F1 and 35.540 EM. Table IV therefore reports paired-bootstrap intervals only for Weighted-HG-KV minus KG-PPR, while the lexical and dense rows in Table III remain contextual references. The intervals are strictly positive on 2WikiMultiHopQA and MuSiQue, supporting the fixed-substrate key-space claim. HotpotQA separates evidence coverage from answer utility. In the standalone matrix, Weighted-HG-KV improves AR@10 over KG-PPR (98.190 versus 95.381), but its F1 is lower (72.957 versus 73.645), and the paired-bootstrap F1 difference is negative with a confidence interval crossing zero. Thus denser structured retrieval does not automatically improve answer extraction when lexical and dense baselines are already strong; the HotpotQA controller results below test whether the same WHG-KV signal can still help learned evidence ordering. The remaining structured rows separate confidence weight-ing from key structure. Weighted-KG-PPR adds the same confidence weighting to pairwise KG-PPR and changes F1 by only +0.021 on 2Wiki, +0.046 on MuSiQue, and +0.050 on HotpotQA. Static-HG keeps the answer-path hypergraph key structure without the default weighting; compared with Static-HG, Weighted-HG-KV is higher by +1.201 and +2.211 F1 on 2Wiki and MuSiQue. This comparison is descriptive; the formal test remains Table IV, and HotpotQA again limits the interpretation because Weighted-HG-KV is 0.383 F1 below Static-HG. 

B. Oracle Decomposition and Train-to-Dev Support Scoring 

To interpret the standalone boundary, we next ask whether the hypergraph tuples lack useful evidence or whether the system selects the wrong support from them. Table V orga-nizes this diagnosis into a no-gold baseline, an oracle answer-path upper bound, and train-to-dev variants across the three benchmarks. The oracle row uses support information and is excluded from bolding. On 2WikiMultiHopQA, no-gold weighted hypergraph retrieval reaches 79.299 F1, while oracle answer-path selection raises F1 to 90.884. On MuSiQue, no-gold Weighted-HG-KV reaches 39.925 F1 and oracle answer-path selection raises it to 74.330. HotpotQA shows the same pattern: no-gold Weighted-HG-KV reaches 72.957 F1, while oracle answer-path selection reaches 91.111 F1. Thus the cached evidence tuples are not the hard ceiling; the bottleneck is selecting and ranking answer-supporting evidence. The train-to-dev group tests how much of this selection gap can be recovered without dev labels. The train-derived support scorer raises F1 from 79.299 to 87.212 on 2Wiki, from 39.925 to 54.252 on MuSiQue, and from 72.957 to 81.525 on HotpotQA, closing 68.3%, 41.6%, and 47.2% of the oracle-selection gaps. A learned HKVM calibration scorer then reaches 88.391 F1 on 2Wiki, 56.754 F1 on MuSiQue, and 82.929 F1 on HotpotQA. For 2Wiki and MuSiQue, the cali-bration step improves over the matched deterministic support-scorer control by +1.179 and +2.133 F1 with positive paired-bootstrap intervals; HotpotQA shows a descriptive +1.404 F1 calibration step before the final controller. These results mo-tivate testing WHG-KV as an evidence-control signal, while leaving mechanism attribution to the controller and source-ablation analyses below. They do not show that structural proxy features alone caused the improvement.

VIII. L IMITATIONS AND DISCUSSION 

The main implication is that evidence organization can be studied as a controllable layer between candidate retrieval and answer generation. In this view, structure need not replace lexical or dense retrieval to be useful; it can provide a reusable signal for selecting and ordering answer-supporting context. This interpretation is deliberately tied to the fixed extraction substrate and evaluation protocol. HKVM-RAG depends on the quality, cost, and reproducibility of the LLM extraction cache. The current system uses a fixed DeepSeek V4 Flash evidence tuple cache with temperature 0.0 and a passage-level relation-triple schema, which makes the structured comparison reproducible across seeds. The same design also means that endpoint, prompt-schema, high-order extraction, or extraction-budget changes can alter the evidence substrate. The frozen passage, tuple, and prediction manifests make evidence trace-ability explicit, echoing the database provenance principle that output claims should be connected to their contributing source data [38]. The shared tuple cache was selected for controlled no-dev-gold comparison and confidence fields, not because it dominates denser heuristic OpenIE extraction; in prior diagnostics, pairwise KG-PPR was stronger with heuristic OpenIE than with the sparser LLM tuple cache. Such changes should be reported as new cache manifests rather than mixed with the current results. This framing also explains why HippoRAG 2 and EcphoryRAG are not direct numeric rows in the current matrix. They are closest related systems, but the paper’s controlled claim is key-space isolation rather than full-system performance ranking. HippoRAG 2 combines Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct, NV-Embed-v2, and online LLM triple filtering [5]; EcphoryRAG uses Phi4/Ollama, bge-m3 embeddings, 500 random questions per dataset, dataset-tuned final context size, and no exact public split/code path in our current audit [6]. Reproducing either system without aligned models, splits, prompts, and online filtering budgets would confound the structured-vs-KG comparison rather than isolate key-space design. The positive standalone-structured result is limited to the structured comparison on 2WikiMultiHopQA and MuSiQue. HotpotQA separates support coverage from answer utility: standalone Weighted-HG-KV improves AR@10 over KG-PPR but does not improve answer F1, while the dense-aware con-troller reaches 85.810 F1 and improves over both ColBERTv2 and learned HKVM calibration. C ONCLUSION 

This paper studied multi-hop RAG as a fixed-budget evidence-organization problem rather than as only passage matching. HKVM-RAG treats answer-path hyperedges as key-side evidence-indexing objects while keeping passage text as the value side. Under this fixed substrate, answer-path hyper-graph keys improve over pairwise graph keys by +3.426 F1 on 2WikiMultiHopQA and +3.592 F1 on MuSiQue. HotpotQA, however, shows that better structured support coverage does not necessarily translate into standalone answer-F1 gains. The support-selection and controller experiments sharpen this conclusion. Oracle decomposition shows large support-selection headroom, train-to-dev support scoring recovers part of it, and the dense-aware controller reaches 88.846 F1 on 2WikiMultiHopQA (+11.084 over ColBERTv2), 65.073 F1 on MuSiQue (+6.763), and 85.810 F1 on HotpotQA (+5.966). Source-level and first-stage diagnostics show that the con-troller gain is WHG-KV-specific under the tested protocol, not a generic effect of adding any structured source. The con-tribution is therefore bounded but useful. The paper provides fixed-substrate evidence that answer-path hypergraph keys can improve evidence organization over pairwise KG keys, and cached train-to-dev diagnostics showing that WHG-KV can act as a dense-aware evidence-control signal. These findings can inform future evidence-organization benchmarks and end-to-end models that learn key-value-separated structures directly, while still requiring broader retriever-family coverage, answer-module sensitivity tests, and official evaluation settings under frozen extraction manifests.
