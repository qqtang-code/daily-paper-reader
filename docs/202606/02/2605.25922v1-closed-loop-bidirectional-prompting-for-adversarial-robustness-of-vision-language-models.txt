Abstract 

Vision Language Models adapt well to downstream tasks but are highly vulnerable to adversarial perturbations that disrupt cross-modal semantic alignment. Existing defenses are largely unidirectional or structural, failing to exploit bidirectional cross-modal complementarity and instance-wise adaptive protection. To overcome the limitations of unidirectional and static defenses in adversarial settings, we propose Closed-Loop Bidirectional Prompting, casting robust adaptation as cross-modal agreement recovery via a dynamic feedback loop on frozen encoders. A Semantic Anchor is introduced as a stable prior to constrain cyclic updates and mitigate perturbation-induced feature corruption. Through anchor-based bootstrap-ping, textual semantics denoise visual representations, while the refined visuals enable instance-adaptive prompt updating, yielding a rectified and robust consensus. Extensive evaluations across 11 datasets validate state-of-the-art robustness and strong base-to-new generalization, while maintaining a favorable trade-off between computational cost and accuracy. 

1 Introduction 

Vision Language Models (VLMs) [ 30 , 21 , 16 , 39 , 19 ] have advanced representation learning by aligning visual and textual modalities in a joint embedding space through self-supervised pre-training [ 5, 12 , 6]. The resulting cross-modal alignment supports strong generalisation across a range of tasks, from classification [ 36 ] to dense prediction such as segmentation [ 45 ] and object detection [42], and underpins a growing set of real-world applications [44, 23]. The same tight visual‚Äìtextual coupling that drives this performance, however, can also be a source of fragility [ 26 ]: small input perturbations can disturb the alignment and propagate to the output. The concern is amplified in deployment, since most widely used VLMs are built on publicly released foundations such as CLIP, granting potential adversaries white-box access to the encoder and making gradient-based attacks readily available [ 17 , 24 , 4]. These factors motivate a closer examination of VLM robustness under adversarial settings. Defences for VLMs fall broadly into training-time and test-time paradigms, and existing methods within each paradigm have complementary limitations. Adversarial Fine-Tuning (AFT) [ 27 , 31 , 35 ]adversarially updates the visual encoder; while effective on benchmarks such as ImageNet, it is

Adversarial Prompt Tuning (APT) [ 20 ,47 , 43 ] avoids modifying the backbone by optimising a small set of soft prompts, but the resulting prompts are static and shared across all inputs, which constrains adaptation to instance-specific perturbations and tends to overfit under few-shot supervision. Test-time defences avoid this overhead by adapting per-instance during inference [ 38 , 22 ], but most focus on attack-side characteristics (e.g., perturbation magnitude) rather than the underlying cross-modal alignment. A common thread across these paradigms is that they adjust a single modality‚Äîeither the image branch (Figure 1a) or the text branch‚Äîwhile treating the other as fixed context. Even APT methods that bridge modalities typically do so as a unidirectional projection (Figure 1b), without an iterative exchange that lets each branch correct the other. Since adversarial attacks on VLMs are designed precisely to decouple the two branches, a defence that performs bidirectional recovery‚Äîwhere visual and textual features actively interact to re-establish their alignment‚Äîis a natural fit for the threat model. Building on this observation, we propose Closed-Loop Bidirectional Prompting (CLBP) , a defence that establishes a feedback mechanism between the two branches while keeping the pre-trained encoders frozen (Figure 1c). CLBP couples two lightweight adapters. Text-to-Vision (T2V) denoising :a T2V adapter consumes the current text embeddings and emits visual prompts that are prepended to the image patches, so that adversarial noise in the visual feature is filtered through cross-attention against the relatively stable language signal. Unlike VPT [ 14 ], which inserts static learnable parame-ters into every block of the vision encoder, our visual prompts are generated dynamically per image. 

Vision-to-Text (V2T) refinement : a V2T adapter (in the spirit of CoCoOp [ 46 ]) maps the resulting visual feature back into a per-instance shift on the text prompt, yielding a textual description that is tied to the specific image rather than shared across the dataset. Iterating these two updates alone, however, risks error accumulation: noise in one branch can propagate to the other, and the iterates may drift away from the pre-trained manifold. We therefore introduce a Semantic Anchor : the loop is initialised, and implicitly constrained, by the embedding of a generic class template (a Semantic Bootstrapping step). Together with a multi-view aggregation module that suppresses per-augmentation sampling noise, the anchor combines the stability of the pre-trained prior with the flexibility of per-instance adaptation [22, 27, 32, 41]. Our main contributions are as follows. ‚Ä¢ We identify the independent treatment of modalities as a common limitation of existing VLM defences, and propose CLBP‚Äîa bidirectional feedback loop in which visual and textual features mutually condition and correct each other at inference time, with frozen pre-trained encoders. ‚Ä¢ We complement the loop with a Semantic Anchor that initialises and constrains the iteration to remain near the pre-trained semantic manifold, and we analyse the resulting fixed-point dynamics: a single bidirectional update is provably and empirically already close to the loop‚Äôs fixed point. ‚Ä¢ Across 11 datasets and three evaluation regimes (cross-dataset zero-shot, few-shot adaptation, and base-to-new generalisation), CLBP improves robustness over representative AFT, APT, and test-time baselines while preserving the clean accuracy of the underlying CLIP backbone. 

2 Related Work 

Adversarial Vulnerability of Vision Language Models. Despite their impressive zero-shot capa-bilities, VLMs are sensitive to adversarial perturbations [ 27 ]. In contrast to attacks on uni-modal classifiers that aim to cross a decision boundary [ 11 ], attacks on VLMs typically introduce imper-ceptible noise that maximises the semantic distance between matched image‚Äìtext pairs. Standard gradient-based attacks such as Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) [ 24 ] and ensemble frameworks like AutoAttack [ 8 ] are routinely used to exploit white-box or gray-box access to the encoder, motivating defences that mitigate adversarial threats without compromising the model‚Äôs generalisation.

Both are effective on benchmarks such as ImageNet-1k but are computationally heavy and risk distorting the pre-trained feature space, which can hurt downstream performance. Adversarial Prompt Tuning (APT) [ 20 , 47 , 43 ] sidesteps this cost by optimising a small set of soft prompts while keeping the backbone frozen. Early vari-ants‚ÄîAdvVP [ 27 ], AdvPT [ 43 ] and AdvVLP [ 48 ]‚Äîuse independent prompts for each modality and largely ignore cross-modal correlations (Figure 1a). Subsequent works bridge the two branches via a unidirectional projection (Figure 1b): AdvMaPLe [ 48 ], built on MaPLe [ 15 ], projects text prompts into the visual branch, while FAP inverts the direction. These methods, however, still produce static, instance-agnostic prompts and lack a mutual correction loop, which limits their adaptation to diverse adversarial patterns under few-shot supervision. 

Test-Time Defence. Test-time defences operate during inference and adapt per-instance without retraining. Test-Time Counterattack (TTC) [ 38 ] exploits the anomalous stability of adversarial examples under secondary noise: it identifies the attack via noise injection and optimises a counter-perturbation that maximises feature deviation from the adversarial input. Robust Test-Time Prompt Tuning (R-TPT) [ 32 ] attenuates perturbations through multi-view consistency and refines predictions via point-wise entropy minimisation. Self-calibrated Consistency (SCC) [ 22 ] extends this line by addressing semantic drift and view sensitivity through semantic and spatial consistency constraints. These methods all operate on the visual side and rely on consistency as the supervisory signal; the textual prototypes that determine cross-modal alignment [30] remain unchanged. 33 Method

4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setups Datasets. Following standard benchmarks for CLIP adversarial robustness, we evaluate on a suite of 11 datasets covering diverse visual domains: ImageNet-1k [ 9] and Caltech101 [ 10 ] for generic object recognition; OxfordPets [ 29 ], Flowers102 [ 28 ], Food101 [ 3], StanfordCars [ 18 ], and FGVC-Aircraft [ 25 ] for fine-grained classification; SUN397 [ 37 ] for scene recognition; UCF101 [ 34 ] for action recognition; and DTD [ 7 ] together with EuroSAT [ 13 ] for specialised domains (textures and satellite imagery). 

Baselines. We compare against representative methods spanning the three defence paradigms. For 

test-time defences we evaluate Anti-Adv (image‚Äìtext consistency maximisation) [ 1 ], TTC (counter-perturbation optimisation) [ 38 ], and R-TPT (multi-view entropy minimisation) [ 32 ], alongside standard adaptation methods (TPT [ 33 ], C-TPT [ 40 ], MTA [ 41 ]). For training-based defences 

we adopt a strict cross-dataset transfer protocol to assess generalisation: models are trained on a large-scale source dataset (ImageNet) and evaluated directly on unseen downstream domains. Within this protocol we compare two sub-categories: (i) Adversarial Fine-Tuning (AFT): TeCoA (semantic guidance) [ 27 ], FARE (unsupervised) [ 31 ], PMG-AFT [ 35 ], and a clean fine-tuned baseline (CLIP-FT); and (ii) Adversarial Prompt Tuning (APT) in the few-shot regime: AdvVP (visual prompts only) [ 27 ], AdvVLP (independent dual prompts) [ 48 ], AdvMaPLe (unidirectional deep prompting) [48], and FAP (interaction-optimised prompting) [48]. 

Implementation Details. We use CLIP with Vision Transformer (ViT) backbones (ViT-B/16 and ViT-B/32) as the visual encoders. The semantic anchor is initialised from the standard manual template "a photo of a [class]" on every dataset, and both the visual and textual learnable prompts use a context length of 4 tokens (sensitivity in Appendix B.6). Following the schedule in Appendix A.2.5, models are trained for 5 epochs under the cross-dataset transfer benchmark and 10 epochs in the few-shot setting; adversarial perturbations are generated by a 2-step PGD attack at 

œµ=1 /255 and Œ±=1 /255 in ‚Ñì‚àû. We report Top-1 accuracy on both clean and adversarial examples. At evaluation we use a suite of white-box and adaptive attacks: PGD-‚Ñì‚àû [24 ], the ensemble framework AutoAttack [ 8], the margin-based Carlini‚ÄìWagner attack (CW-‚Ñì‚àû) [ 4], and the adaptive Expectation-over-Transformation PGD (EOT-PGD) [ 2 ]; their roles, budgets and results are presented alongside the corresponding analyses in Section 4. The defence mechanism is visualised in Appendix D, and all experiments are run on NVIDIA H100 GPUs.

Adaptive EOT-PGD. Because the multi-view aggregation in Eq. (6) introduces stochasticity in the forward pass, an attacker may underestimate the true gradient by sampling a single random draw‚Äîa failure mode known as gradient obfuscation [2]. EOT-PGD addresses this by drawing eot independent augmentation samples at each PGD step and averaging their per-sample gradients, giving an unbiased Monte-Carlo estimator of the expected loss gradient under the augmentation distribution; larger 

eot tightens the estimate. At eot=4 on the unaltered stochastic pipeline, CLBP retains 73 .14% on Caltech101 and 28 .72% on DTD, +27 .34% and +15 .95% above R-TPT respectively (Appendix B.5); the robustness gain is not an artefact of gradient obfuscation.

5 Conclusion 

We presented Closed-Loop Bidirectional Prompting (CLBP), an adversarial defence for VLMs that recovers cross-modal alignment by alternating textual denoising and visual refinement under a fixed semantic anchor. The composed loop is locally contractive on the reachable set induced by the anchor, with empirical Lipschitz constant q ‚àº 10 ‚àí4, so a single bidirectional update is already close to the loop‚Äôs fixed point. Across 11 classification datasets, CLBP improves few-shot robustness, base-to-new generalisation, and cross-dataset transfer over the AFT, APT, and test-time defence baselines we evaluate. 

6 Limitations 

We outline two aspects of CLBP whose extension we see as natural directions for future work. 

Architectural scope. Our analysis and empirical study focus on CLIP-style dual-tower VLMs, where the visual and textual branches are processed by separate encoders and combined through a cosine similarity. The bidirectional formulation is conceptually compatible with deeper-fusion architectures (e.g., BLIP-style models with intra-encoder cross-attention layers), but extending the closed-loop construction to such models may call for a different parametrisation of the T2V and V2T maps; we leave this extension to future work. 

Analytical tightness. The convergence result in Theorem 3.1 relies on a factorised Lipschitz bound 

q ‚â§ LG LHx that is convenient but not tight in our setting: the empirical Lipschitz constant we measure (q ‚àº 10 ‚àí4) is several orders of magnitude below the product of the per-branch constants. A tighter bound that better captures the contractive behaviour we observe, together with extensions to threat models beyond ‚Ñì‚àû digital attacks‚Äîsuch as patch-based or physical-world threats‚Äîwould further strengthen the theoretical picture. 9References

D.1 Semantic Rectification and Margin Recovery 

We measure two diagnostics: (i) the logit margin , the difference between the logit of the correct class and the largest competitor, and (ii) the cosine similarities between the image feature and each text prototype. Choice of V for main experiments. The main-paper results are reported at V =32 . This choice is governed by two considerations. Second, the analysis above shows that CLBP‚Äôs robust accuracy on DTD is saturated for V ‚â• 8: the spread across V ‚àà { 8, 16 , 32 } is within 1.1% , and the seed-control and shared-pool experiments above place this spread within test-time sampling noise. A consequence of this saturation is that the main-table numbers reported at V =32 are not driven by the larger view budget; the same numbers, up to seed-level fluctuation, would be obtained at V =8 at 0.092 s/img instead of 0.227 s/img. We therefore present V =32 as the conservative protocol setting for the main results, and V =8 as a deployment-friendly alternative when inference cost is the binding constraint.

I Broader Impact 

CLBP addresses a security concern that grows in importance as Vision Language Models are deployed in safety-relevant settings. By improving robustness without retraining the underlying encoders, our method offers a parameter-efficient route to mitigating adversarial vulnerabilities that may be especially useful for resource-constrained or already-deployed systems. We acknowledge two considerations. Dual-use nature. Defensive techniques in adversarial machine learning are dual-use: the same observation that motivates our defence‚Äîthat cross-modal alignment can be disrupted bidirectionally‚Äîcould in principle inform stronger attacks. We mitigate this by evaluating only against well-established benchmarks (PGD, AutoAttack, CW, EOT-PGD) and by releasing our code openly so that the community can audit and improve both defences and threat models. Inherited biases. Like any test-time defence built on a frozen backbone, CLBP relies on the quality of the pre-trained text encoder; biases or stereotypes present in the underlying VLM are not corrected by our method and may persist in downstream outputs. We therefore recommend that practitioners pair adversarial defences such as CLBP with bias and fairness audits appropriate to their deployment context. To support transparent evaluation and reproducibility, our implementation, training scripts, and pre-trained adapters are released under a permissive open-source license, and the experimental protocols are documented in Section 4 and Appendices A‚ÄìB.5. 24
