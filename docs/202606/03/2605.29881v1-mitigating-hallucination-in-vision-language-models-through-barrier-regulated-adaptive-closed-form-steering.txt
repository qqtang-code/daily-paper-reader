# Mitigating Hallucination in Vision-Language Models through Barrier-Regulated Adaptive Closed-form Steering 

Soumyadeep Jana *, Pulkit Mittal * and Sanasam Ranbir Singh 

Indian Institute of Technology Guwahati, India 

{sjana, m.pulkit, ranbir}@iitg.ac.in 

Abstract 

Large vision-language models (LVLMs) often hallucinate objects that are not present in the input image, largely because visual ground-ing weakens as decoding progresses. Exist-ing inference-time mitigation methods modify logits or hidden states throughout generation, but they suffer from three key limitations: they lack an explicit grounding objective, intervene even when the model is already well-grounded, and use fixed correction strengths that do not adapt to the severity of grounding failure. We propose BRACS (Barrier-Regulated Adaptive 

Closed-form Steering), a training-free steering framework that addresses these issues through barrier-regulated adaptive closed-form steering. BRACS monitors the model’s own attention to measure visual grounding and applies correc-tions to the hidden states only when grounding deteriorates. The corrective update is computed analytically in closed form, requiring no train-ing of auxiliary networks or model retraining. Experiments on LLaVA-1.5-7B and Qwen-VL-Chat show that BRACS consistently outper-forms prior methods on hallucination bench-marks, reducing CHAIR s by 9.4 points and im-proving POPE F1 by 2.7 points, while match-ing or improving performance on four general multimodal benchmarks. BRACS also remains efficient, operating at 80% of greedy decoding throughput and achieving 1.3× higher speed on average than the baselines. 

1 Introduction 

Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) (Liu et al., 2023, 2024a; Bai et al., 2023) describe im-ages with language, yet they frequently generate descriptions of non-existent content. This phe-nomenon of generating objects, attributes, or re-lations absent from the input image is termed hallu-cination and it undermines the reliability of LVLMs on any downstream task that requires faithful visual grounding. 

> *Equal contribution.

Prior works (Leng et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b) attribute this hallucination to two main factors. (i) The LLM backbone exhibits a strong language prior bias, where the model tends to favor high-probability textual continuations learned during pretraining, even when they are weakly supported or completely unsupported by the visual input. (ii) Attention to image tokens decreases across layers and decoding steps, weakening visual grounding as generation becomes longer. To address this, many recent training-free methods apply corrections dur-ing inference. Contrastive decoding (Leng et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025) targets (i) by subtract-ing logits obtained from corrupted visual inputs or image-free forward passes. Activation steering (Su et al., 2025) shifts hidden states along a learned linear direction of non-hallucinating state to pre-vent language-prior bias. Attention manipulation 

(Liu et al., 2024b; Sarkar et al., 2025) targets (ii) by reweighting attention towards visual tokens or suppressing hallucination-prone heads. Despite their effectiveness, existing methods share three common limitations. (L1) Lack of an explicit grounding objective: Most methods modify logits, attention maps, or hidden states us-ing heuristic adjustments, without clearly defin-ing what constitutes sufficient visual grounding or whether the applied steering is minimal. (L2) No criterion for when to steer: Steering is made at every step, even when the model does not need help. (L3) No criterion for how much to steer: 

The magnitude of the steering is usually fixed or pre-defined, and does not adapt to the current level of grounding failure during generation. 

Our Approach: We introduce BRACS (Barrier-

Regulated Adaptive Closed-form Steering), a steer-ing framework that steers the model’s hidden states during decoding to address all three limitations. 

Addressing L1: Instead of using heuristic adjust-ments, we define visual grounding through an ex-plicit barrier score hl(xt), the pre-softmax image-1

We call a decoding step grounded when hl(xt) ≥ τ , with τ a hyper-parameter that sets the minimum required level of visual grounding. On grounded steps, BRACS leaves the hidden state untouched; otherwise (when 

hl(xt) < τ ) it computes a correction and steers the hidden state xt. Addressing L3: When grounding falls below the threshold hl(xt) < τ , BRACS com-putes the minimum hidden-state steering θ⋆ needed to bring h back to τ . Since the grounding barrier is linear in the hidden state xt, the corrective steering is obtained exactly in closed form, without training, or the use of any auxiliary networks. The correc-tion strength is adaptive, applying larger corrective steering only when the model drifts farther from the image. The edit is applied to xt before the Q/K/V pro-jections, so the corrected query, key, and value all enter the KV cache. Every later decoding step attends to a cache that is consistent with the correction. On LLaVA-1.5-7B and Qwen-VL-Chat, BRACS leads every hallucination benchmark (CHAIR s −9.4, POPE F1 +2 .7), matches or im-proves four general-purpose benchmarks, and re-tains 80% of greedy throughput and is 1.3× faster on average than the baselines. 

Contributions: (1) We identify three key limita-tions of existing training-free methods for LVLMs: lack of grounding objectives, always-on steer-ing, and fixed steering strengths. (2) We pro-pose BRACS, a training-free steering framework that explicitly defines grounding, intervenes only when needed, and computes adaptive minimum hidden-state corrections in closed form without training. (3) Across POPE, CHAIR, MMHal, and four general-purpose benchmarks, BRACS outper-forms baselines on hallucination while matching or improving general reasoning. 

2 Related Work 

Training-based methods reduce hallucination through additional supervision and retraining. Sun et al. (2023) extend RLHF to LVLMs for improv-ing factual consistency, while FGAIF (Jing and Du, 2025) uses fine-grained AI-generated feedback to detect hallucinations in responses. LACING (Zhao et al., 2025) mitigates language bias using dual-attention with soft image guidance. Despite their effectiveness, these approaches require costly re-training and substantial computational resources. More recently, training-free methods have emerged as lightweight alternatives for mitigating hallucination during inference. OPERA (Huang et al., 2024) uses beam-search penalties to sup-press over-trusting generations. Several works employ contrastive decoding: VCD (Leng et al., 2024) contrasts outputs from original and perturbed images, PAI (Liu et al., 2024b) enhances image attention and refines logits using image-free pre-dictions, DAMRO (Gong et al., 2024) suppresses high-attention outlier tokens, and HALC (Chen et al., 2024) contrasts multiple visual contexts with visual matching scores. Other approaches inter-vene directly in the latent space: ICT (Chen et al., 2025) applies targeted activation shifts to selected attention heads, SPIN (Sarkar et al., 2025) sup-presses non-visual attention heads, VTI (Liu et al., 2025) steers generation using pre-computed inter-vention vectors, and ASD (Su et al., 2025) corrects hidden states and logits through contrastive steer-ing. In contrast to these methods, BRACS intro-duces an explicit grounding objective with selec-tive, adaptive closed-form steering, avoiding con-tinuous steering, auxiliary networks, and multiple forward passes.

4 Method 

BRACS incorporates the three design considera-tions D1-D3 from §3 as a single closed-form up-date applied to the hidden state stream of a frozen LVLM. BRACS (i) reads a scalar grounding bar-rier hl(xt) directly from the model’s own attention mechanism, (ii) compute its gradient ∇xt hl ana-lytically without backpropagation, and (iii) apply the minimum-norm hidden-state steering that lifts 

hl(xt) above a threshold τ whenever the step is flagged as poorly grounded. The correction is ap-plied to xt rather than the query qt, so the induced changes are cached into K and V and propagate consistently through all subsequent attention oper-ations.

5 Experimental Settings 

5.1 Benchmarks Visual Hallucination: CHAIR (Rohrbach et al., 2018) measures object hallucination in open-ended image captioning via two metrics: CHAIR s, the fraction of captions that mention at least one hallu-cinated object, and CHAIR i, the fraction of halluci-nated object instances over all mentioned instances. Following prior work (Leng et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b), we evaluate on 500 randomly sampled im-ages from the MS-COCO val2014 split with the prompt “Describe this image in detail.” and decode up to 140 tokens using greedy method, nucleus sampling and beam search. POPE (Li et al., 2023) evaluates hallucination via binary questions about object presence across three sampling strategies-

random , popular , and adversarial - drawn from MS-COCO ( ∼8,910 questions total). We report per-split Accuracy and F1. MMHal-Bench (Sun et al., 2023) contains 96 image-question pairs scored by GPT-4o ( gpt-4o ) on a 0-6 hallucination scale. We report the average score and the fraction of responses flagged as hallucinated. 

General Multimodal Understanding: To verify that BRACS preserves general multimodal capabil-ities, we evaluate on MME (Fu et al., 2023) (per-ception + cognition), MMBench (Liu et al., 2024c) (3K multiple-choice questions across 20 reasoning and perception tasks), MMMU (Yue et al., 2024) (11.5K knowledge-intensive questions spanning 30 subjects), and LLaVA-Bench (in-the-wild) (Liu et al., 2023) (60 open-ended questions over 24 im-ages, GPT-4o-scored). 

5.2 Implementation Details Backbones and Baselines: We evaluate on 

LLaVA-1.5-7B (Liu et al., 2024a) and Qwen-VL-Chat (Bai et al., 2023) to confirm that BRACS transfers across architectures (different vision en-coder, different attention sink pattern). BRACS is applied at inference only, backbone weights are never modified. We compare against four represen-tative inference-time methods. VCD (Leng et al., 2024), VDD-None (Zhang et al., 2025), PAI (Liu et al., 2024b) and SPIN (Sarkar et al., 2025). Each baseline is run with its published hyperparameters. Details of baselines are in Appendix B.

6.2 General Multimodal Understanding and MMHal 

A hallucination-reduction method is only useful if it does not trade off broader multimodal capabil-ity. Table 3 evaluates BRACS on four capability-oriented benchmarks - MME, MMBench, MMMU, and LLaVA-Bench, against the same baselines as POPE and CHAIR. Baseline methods that improve hallucination robustness often degrade general mul-timodal reasoning performance. On LLaVA-1.5-7B, baselines reduce MME score, suggesting that continuous corrections interfere with reasoning steps unrelated to grounding. In contrast, BRACS is the only method to simultaneously improve MME (+14), achieve the best MMBench score (72.83), tie for the best MMMU score (34.56), and obtain the highest MMHal score (2.72). A simi-lar trend appears on Qwen-VL-Chat. While VCD, VDD-None, PAI and SPIN improve some bench-marks, they decrease MMHal. BRACS, in compar-ison, maintains competitive general benchmark per-formance while achieving an increase in MMHal (3.72). These results suggest that selective, adap-tive intervention preserves reasoning ability more effectively than continuous steering approaches.

7.2 Choice of α

The norm correction θ∗ already gives the minimum correction needed to clear the barrier (Eq. 4), while 

α controls its strength. On LLaVA (Table 4(a)), Adv F1 peaks at α=1 .0. Smaller values ( 0.3, 0.5)reduce F1 by 0.75 -0.88 , while larger values ( 1.25 ,

1.5) reduce it by 0.53 -1.33 . On Qwen (Table 5(a), Appendix C), the same trend appears, with Adv F1 dropping to 81 .81 at α=1 .5. This indicates that increasing the steering strength oversteers the model and introduces potential semantic drift. 

7.3 Threshold τ sensitivity 

The threshold τ controls how often BRACS steers: very small values rarely trigger corrections, while very large values make correction always active. On LLaVA (Table 4(b)), Adv F1 peaks at τ =−5,dropping by 1.4 F1 at τ =−6 and by 0.8 at τ =−4.On Qwen (Table 5(b), Appendix C), the same trend appears around τ =−6, with Adv F1 decreasing by 

0.48 at τ =−8 and 1.10 at τ =−4.

8.2 Computational Complexity 

BRACS is applied to L′ = |L steer | steered layers. It adds per decoding step one matrix–vector product per steered layer to evaluate hl(xt) and its closed-form gradient (§4.2), an O(L′ H d m d) overhead plus an O(L′d) scalar solve for θ∗. On LLaVA-1.5-7B ( |L steer |=16 ) this adds ∼25% overhead per de-coding step ( 0.80 × end-to-end throughput; §8.3). 

8.3 Decoding Throughput 

Figure 4 reports decoding cost on LLaVA-1.5-7B (single A100-80GB, batch size 1, greedy decoding, 

50 new tokens per caption averaged over 30 cap-tions). BRACS runs at 22 .1 tok/s, which is 0.80 ×

the greedy throughput and about 1.3× faster on average than the baselines. This speedup comes from the closed-form correction (§4.2). BRACS adds only a light per-step computation, whereas the baselines (VCD, VDD-None and PAI) run a sec-ond forward pass per decoding step, which roughly halves throughput when run sequentially.

Limitations 

BRACS’s barrier hl(xt) enforces visual ground-ing by encouraging attention toward image tokens, but it does not explicitly control which specific im-age regions or tokens the model should attend to. Fine-grained hallucinations that depend on where the model looks, spatial relations, counting, OCR, may require a barrier with spatial structure, which we leave to future work (see Appendix F for an 8example of a spatial-relation failure). We evalu-ate on LLaVA-1.5-7B and Qwen-VL-Chat. Ex-tension to stronger backbones (LLaVA-NeXT, In-ternVL, Qwen2-VL) is straightforward in principle but may require re-selecting Lsteer if the distribu-tion of image-attention sinks differs substantially.
