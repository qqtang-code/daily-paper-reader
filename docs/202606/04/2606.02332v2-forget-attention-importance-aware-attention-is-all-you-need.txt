# Abstract 

Combining attention’s global retrieval with the sequential importance signal of state space models (SSMs) is the open challenge of hybrid language modeling. Transformers see everywhere but cannot prioritize; SSMs know what matters but cannot revisit. Existing hybrids—Jamba (block level) and Hymba (head level)— place the two in separate compartments, so neither informs the other during the attention computation itself. We propose SISA (SSM-Informed Softmax Attention), which adds an SSM-derived importance term directly inside the attention score 

and realizes the full operation as a single SDPA call on augmented query/key vectors —no recurrent state, no custom kernel. At 152M / 5B tokens, SISA reaches LAMBADA-greedy 17.3% (vs. Transformer 13.9 and Mamba-3 15.5) and attains NIAH 100% from step 1K, 7× faster than Transformer’s retrieval convergence ;at 369M, Mamba-3 leads LAMBADA while SISA preserves perfect NIAH and stock-SDPA execution. SISA thus defines a third design axis for SSM–attention hybrids— score-level fusion —beyond the block-level and head-level paradigms that have dominated the field. 

# 1 Introduction 

Foundation models for language are dominated by two architectural lineages with complementary strengths and complementary failures. Transformers [ 1] let every position attend to every other, but the only criterion for relevance is query–key content similarity—the sequential dynamics in which a token appears are ignored. State space models (SSMs), notably the Mamba family [ 2 – 4], process tokens in order and selectively update a recurrent state, but once information has decayed it cannot be recovered. We diagnose this as a single missing capability that neither family possesses on its own: an attention mechanism that knows which tokens matter from sequential structure while remaining able to revisit any of them by content. The Transformer “can look anywhere but does not know where to look”; the SSM “knows what is important but cannot revisit what is past.” Existing hybrid architectures attempt to combine the two—but the way they do so has remained essentially unchanged since the first generation. Jamba [5] interleaves SSM and Transformer layers (block level); Hymba [ 7] runs attention and SSM heads in parallel within a layer (head level). In both designs the two mechanisms remain compartmentalized: each produces its output independently and never references the other during the attention computation itself. A separate line—FoX [ 13 ] and the position biases ALiBi/T5/DAPE—also adds to the score, but only as a scalar function of position or decay (Section 2.2); we instead fuse a vector-valued SSM signal that encodes both decay and data-dependent rotation. We argue that the missing principle is fusion at the level of the attention score , where every weight is jointly determined by content similarity and SSM-derived importance.

2.2 Attention Score Biases 

ALiBi [ 10 ] adds a fixed distance penalty to the logits, T5 [ 11 ] adds a learned position bias, and DAPE [ 12 ] makes the bias content-dependent. All three are pure positional priors : they encode “how far apart are two tokens,” not “what happened in the sequence between them.” None can be informed by the input’s sequential dynamics—precisely the signal that an SSM is built to produce. 

The closest prior work is FoX [ 13 ] (Forgetting Transformer), which adds a cumulative forget-gate bias to the score: A = softmax (QK ⊤ + D). Here D is a per-pair scalar (decay only). SISA’s bias is a 

ds-dimensional inner product that encodes both decay and data-dependent rotation. FoX uses a scalar forget-gate bias and avoids materializing the full bias matrix via a modified FlashAttention-style implementation; SISA differs by realizing the bias purely through augmented Q/K, so any unmodified FlashAttention/SDPA kernel works without customization. # 6 Analysis 

6.1 The SSM Bias as an Importance Lens The SSM channels are not memory—they are an importance lens for attention. They store no information and are recomputed from the input at every layer; their job is purely to tell attention which earlier tokens matter when weighting key–value pairs. SISA has no recurrent state, no convolution, and no sequential processing. The name “SSM” reflects only the mathematical lineage—the actual implementation is linear projections, a cumulative sum, and SDPA. Structurally, SISA is a pure Transformer with an additive score bias .• Decay egi−c · e−(gj −c): data-dependent distance weighting between positions i and j. Unlike ALiBi’s fixed slope, it varies with the input. • Rotation R(Φ i)⊤R(Φ j ): distinguishes positions whose decay is similar but whose role in the sequence differs. Together, ¯C⊤ 

> i

¯Bj scores how strongly the sequence flow has marked token j as important to position i—a sequential prior that pure content attention cannot produce. 

6.2 Importance Signal Diluted by Softmax 

Why is SISA ds = 16 ’s edge dramatic on LAMBADA ( +3 .4 pp) but only modest on HellaSwag (+1 .5 pp)? Softmax dilutes the additive bias more on tasks where many tokens are roughly equally relevant. Softmax forces attention weights to sum to one, so when the content score 

q⊤k/√dh is already strong, the additive SSM bias gets relatively diluted by normalization. The bias matters most on tasks like LAMBADA where “which token is the right answer” is decisive, and least where many tokens are roughly equally relevant. Sigmoid attention [ 14 ] would in principle remove this dilution; we offer this as a hypothesis to be verified in SISA-2. 

# 7 Conclusion 

We introduced score-level fusion —a third integration point beyond block- and head-level—realized as SISA. To our knowledge, this vector-valued data-dependent score bias is novel among SSM-attention hybrids (Appendix I). 

Results. (1) At 152M, SISA gives the strongest LAMBADA-greedy ( +24% vs. Transformer, +12% 

vs. Mamba-3). (2) NIAH 100% from step 1K, 7× faster than Transformer; neither Mamba variant reaches 100%. (3) Single SDPA, FlashAttention-compatible, +25% throughput vs. Mamba-3. (4) At 369M, Mamba-3 leads LAMBADA while SISA preserves NIAH and stock SDPA—complementary strengths. (5) ds ablation across three scales quantifies the SSM/FFN trade-off. Limitations : 369M max, 5B tokens, Chinchilla-undertrained at 369M, softmax dilution (sigmoid → SISA-2), RoPE limit 2,048, 39% throughput overhead (see Apps. S–T). Score-level fusion identifies the attention score itself as a natural interface between Transformers and SSMs, opening a third design axis for hybrid sequence models.

# I Survey of SSM–Attention Hybrids 

To make explicit how SISA’s score-level fusion differs from prior hybrid paradigms, we summarize the major SSM-Transformer hybrids surveyed in this work, classified by fusion level: • Block level: alternating SSM and attention layers (e.g., 1:7 ratio or sandwich structures). The two mechanisms are split at the layer level , and only one type runs per layer. • Head level: attention heads and SSM heads in parallel within a layer; their outputs are concatenated or weighted-summed, but the two do not cross-reference inside the head computation .• Score level (this work): the two mechanisms are fused additively inside the attention score computation, via a single SDPA call. • Other: gated, cross-attention, and other variants not captured by the three above. 

Observation. Existing hybrids either (a) split the mechanisms across layers/heads, or (b) add only a 

scalar bias to the score. SISA adds a ds-dimensional inner product to the score, which encodes both decay ( egi−gj ) and data-dependent rotation ( R(Φ j − Φi)). We did not find this form of fusion in our survey.

(369M). At larger scale, shrinking the FFN hurts more , which explains why a fixed ds becomes automatically suboptimal. Three observations: 

(1) ds = 16 is a scale-invariant cost. FFN reduction is exactly 5.3% at 50M/152M and 7.4% at 369M—almost flat. ds = 16 is the natural “constant cost” choice, with bounded FFN loss at any scale. 

(2) ds = 32 degrades with scale. It is 10.5% at 50M/152M but rises to 14.7% at 369M, suggesting that a fixed ds behaves differently across regimes. The reason is that SSM parameters ( ∝ L · d · h · ds)grow faster than FFN ( ∝ L · d · dff ): from 50M to 369M, h doubles, L quadruples, d doubles, so SSM grows 16 × while FFN grows only 11 .5×.

(3) ds = 64 is extreme at 369M. It cuts FFN by 29.2%, making the FFN share (41.6%) only 2.4×

the SSM share (17.2%). At 50M (FFN cut 20.9%), ds = 64 is best on LAMBADA (14.40%) and WinoGrande (52.4%)—a regime where SSM gains exceed FFN losses. At 369M, FFN reduction matters more, so ds = 64 records the worst LAMBADA (13.10%, Section Q). 16 Figure 6: ds ablation. Best is ds = 64 at 50M, ds = 16 at 152M, and (among the retrain candidates) 

ds = 128 at 369M—the optimum is non-monotonic in scale. These three ratio regimes manifest as distinct ablation behaviors in Section Q. 

Why is ds = 16 optimal at 152M but ds = 128 at 369M? FFN share alone does not explain it: 152M (55.9%) and 369M (58.8%) are similar yet have opposite optima. Two additional factors matter. 

(i) Data-to-parameter regime. 152M ( 33 × tokens/param) is near Chinchilla-optimal ( ∼ 20 ×): the model uses its FFN capacity fully, so any FFN loss is paid in full and small ds wins on the cost–benefit margin. 369M ( 13 .5×) is data-constrained: part of the FFN capacity is unused, the marginal cost of FFN reduction is smaller, and richer SSM bias from larger ds provides more useful signal. 

(ii) Per-head SSM expressivity. The bias capacity is set by the rank of the per-head ds-dimensional inner product. At 152M (12 heads), a small ds = 16 already provides enough “importance signal,” and FFN preservation should dominate. At 369M (16 heads, 24 layers), the bias signal accumulates over a deeper stack and a richer per-head channel space helps; ds = 128 supplies it. In short, the optimal ds tracks “data/parameter ratio × per-head expressivity demand” , and these two factors create the observed U-shape: small ds at 152M, larger ds at the extremes (50M and 369M; Section Q).

# R Broader Impact 

SISA is a foundational study proposing an architectural improvement to language models. The general risks of language models (biased generation, misinformation, etc.) apply equally here; this work neither aggravates nor mitigates them. SISA’s training-efficiency improvement (matching final accuracy with about 5× fewer tokens) may have a small positive effect by reducing training cost and energy consumption.
