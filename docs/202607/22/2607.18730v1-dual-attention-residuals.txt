# Dual Attention Residuals

Abstract 

Recent work extends Transformer residual pathways along two complementary axes: historical retrieval selects information from earlier depths, whereas multi-stream methods maintain multiple residual trajectories. These capabilities have largely been studied in isolation, and assigning an independent re-triever to each stream still prevents one trajectory from influ-encing depth selection in another. We propose Dual Attention Residuals (DAR), which brings multi-stream interaction into historical retrieval through reciprocal cross-stream address-ing. For each target stream, DAR computes depth weights from normalized states in the opposite stream and applies them to values from the target stream’s own history. The re-trieved states are combined for an unchanged Transformer branch and updated through constrained gated writes; a block-form variant operates on block-level histories to control over-head. Across dense models from 0.1B to 1B parameters and a 7B sparse-MoE model, DAR consistently improves valida-tion loss over standard residual Transformers and Attention Residuals. Routing ablations show that the gain cannot be explained by an additional stream or value projection alone. Representation and intervention analyses further show that re-ciprocal cross-stream selection preserves depth-wise diversity and avoids the redundancy or functional imbalance observed in alternative two-stream designs. 

## Introduction 

Residual connections form the main pathway for transport-ing representations across Transformer depth (He et al. 2016; Vaswani et al. 2017). In a standard Transformer, successive computational branches are accumulated into a single resid-ual stream and propagated sequentially through the network. Recent work extends this pathway along two complementary directions. Historical retrieval methods expand access across depth: Transparent Attention and DenseFormer expose ear-lier representations to later computation (Bapna et al. 2018; Pagliardini et al. 2024), while Attention Residuals makes this access selective through learned depth-wise retrieval (Kimi Team et al. 2026). Multi-stream residual methods instead expand the pathway into multiple learned trajectories, be-ginning with Hyper-Connections (Zhu et al. 2025a) and ex-tending to Frac-Connections, MUDDFormer, and mHC (Zhu

Histori-cal retrieval introduces freedom over which depths to ac-cess, whereas multi-stream residuals introduce freedom over which residual trajectories to maintain and combine. These two degrees of freedom have been studied largely in isolation, leaving open whether they can interact productively within historical retrieval. A straightforward combination is to equip each residual stream with an independent historical retriever. This, how-ever, produces only parallel instances of single-stream re-trieval: each trajectory determines which depths to access and supplies the content returned from those depths, without allowing the other trajectory to influence its selection. We call this within-trajectory binding of the depth-scoring rep-resentation and returned content address-content coupling .To bring multi-stream flexibility into depth-wise retrieval, the trajectories must instead interact during selection. This makes trajectory diversity part of the retrieval rule: historical content in one stream can receive a high depth weight because the other stream supplies a matching address, even when the content representation would not itself strongly match the target query. The trajectories therefore contribute not only different stored content but also complementary signals for selecting it. To make multi-stream interaction part of depth-wise re-trieval, we introduce Dual Attention Residuals (DAR) with reciprocal cross-stream addressing. For each target stream, DAR computes depth weights from normalized states in the opposite stream and applies them to values from the tar-get stream’s history. The resulting stream-specific states are mixed as input to an unchanged Transformer branch. DAR then updates both residual trajectories through gated branch writes, with constrained mixing transporting the existing par-tial state within each block. For efficient scaling, Block DAR retrieves from completed block histories at a coarser depth granularity. Our contributions are summarized as follows: 

• We show that independently evolving residual trajecto-ries can serve not only as parallel information pathways, but also as complementary signals for selecting historical depths. 

• We propose DAR, which realizes this interaction through reciprocal cross-stream keys and self-stream values while leaving the Transformer branches unchanged. arXiv:2607.18730v1 [cs.CL] 21 Jul 2026 • We validate DAR through scaling experiments on dense models from 0.1B to 1B parameters and a 7B sparse-MoE model, routing ablations, and representation anal-yses, demonstrating consistent improvements over stan-dard residual connections and Attention Residuals. 

## Related Work 

Residual Transport and Multi-Stream Topologies 

Standard residual connections transport a single represen-tation through network depth (He et al. 2016; Vaswani et al. 2017). Transformer normalization improves optimiza-tion while preserving this single residual trajectory (Ba, Kiros, and Hinton 2016; Xiong et al. 2020; Zhang and Sen-nrich 2019). Hyper-Connections instead expand the residual pathway into multiple streams and learn how Transformer branches read from and write to them (Zhu et al. 2025a). Frac-Connections and MUDDFormer further enrich cross-layer connectivity, while mHC constrains multi-stream mix-ing to preserve stable transport at scale (Zhu et al. 2025b; Xiao et al. 2025; Xie et al. 2026). These methods show how multiple residual streams can transport and mix information within a layer. DAR extends this idea across depth by retain-ing both trajectories in the historical state bank and applying depth-wise retrieval over them. 

Depth-Wise Historical Retrieval 

Another line of work makes representations from earlier depths directly accessible to later computation. Transpar-ent attention and DenseFormer aggregate intermediate layer states, while Attention Residuals (AttnRes) uses learned pseudo-queries to perform softmax retrieval over previ-ous residual states (Bapna et al. 2018; Pagliardini et al. 2024; Kimi Team et al. 2026). DeepCrossAttention also learns input-dependent combinations of earlier layer out-puts through depth-wise cross-attention (Heddes et al. 2025). Delta Attention Residuals replaces cumulative hidden-state candidates with individual branch outputs (Luo, Cai, and Hu 2026). These methods vary the candidate states and aggrega-tion rules. In single-stream historical retrieval, however, the representations that determine depth weights and the values aggregated under them still come from the same residual trajectory. DAR instead uses the opposite trajectory to deter-mine each value’s depth weight while keeping that value in its original stream. 

## Methods 

DAR changes only the residual pathway around each Trans-former branch. As shown in Figure 1, DAR retrieves two states from depth, combines them for an unchanged attention or feed-forward branch, and updates a two-stream partial block through gated writes and constrained mixing.

Experimental Setup 

Models and data. We evaluate three smaller dense mod-els with 0.1B, 0.3B, and 0.5B non-vocabulary parameters, a main 1B dense model, and a sparse-MoE model. The smaller models are trained on FineWeb-Edu-10B (Penedo et al. 2024) with a token budget equal to 20 × their parameter count and evaluated on 10M held-out tokens. The 1B model is trained on 50B FineWeb-Edu-100B tokens. The sparse model has 7.22B total parameters and activates 1.40B per token, including vocabulary parameters; it is trained for 60K steps, corresponding to 31.46B FineWeb-Edu-100B tokens. All runs use a context length of 4096, with global batch sizes of 256 for dense models and 128 for sparse MoE. 

Training and implementation. All methods within each setting share the same data, backbone, and training con-figuration. The main mHC baseline uses four streams; to facilitate analysis, we additionally train a two-stream mHC under the same 0.5B configuration, denoted mHC-2. Mod-els are trained on a single node with eight H800 GPUs us-ing AdamW (Loshchilov and Hutter 2019). Dense experi-ments use PyTorch with DeepSpeed (Rasley et al. 2020) and 

torch.compile (Ansel et al. 2024), whereas sparse-MoE experiments use Megatron-Core (Shoeybi et al. 2019) and fused AttnRes/DAR operators. For the parameter-matched 0.5B ablation, we set the FFN width of AttnRes + SepV to 4992. We use Block DAR in the main dense and sparse-MoE experiments and evaluate both Block and Full DAR in the smaller models. Complete configurations and parameter counts are provided in Appendix Tables A.1–A.6.

Training Efficiency 

For the 7B sparse-MoE efficiency comparison, Attention Residuals and DAR use a matched Triton prefix-kernel fam-ily (Tillet, Kung, and Cox 2019) following Attention Resid-uals (Kimi Team et al. 2026). The kernels pack normalized keys and unprojected values, then compute the completed-history state and its log-partition. Following the two-phase computation of Attention Residuals, the current partial state is incorporated through an exact online-softmax merge. DAR evaluates both streams jointly and uses fused residual-read stages to predict αℓ and βℓ, together with ρb,i for i > 1,and to form the branch input and optional transported partial state. The Transformer branch remains unchanged; a post-attention epilogue fuses branch-output handling with the gated stream write. Appendix A.1 details the forward and backward boundaries. Relative to the residual baseline, Attention Residuals and DAR reduce throughput by 9.16% and 17.54%, while in-creasing per-GPU peak memory by 7.7 and 10.7 GiB, re-spectively. Under the matched fused implementations, DAR adds approximately 0.363M total and active parameters over Attention Residuals, reduces throughput by 9.23%, and uses 3.1 GiB (4.57%) more peak memory per GPU. Most of the memory increase already appears with single-stream histor-ical retrieval; DAR’s cross-stream routing adds a smaller but measurable cost.

Analysis 

Cross-stream representation geometry. We first assess whether the two streams learn distinct representations across depth. Using four FineWeb-Edu-10B validation sequences of length 4096, we collect post-MLP stream states over the fi-nal 128 tokens and compute cross-stream, cross-depth linear CKA (Kornblith et al. 2019). A high CKA value indicates that two depths organize the sampled tokens similarly, with-out requiring their hidden coordinates to align directly. DAR maintains low cross-stream similarity across most unmatched depths, with alignment confined to a narrow region near corresponding late layers. Unlike the broad high-CKA regions observed in DAR-SelfKV and mHC-2, this localized pattern indicates that DAR preserves distinct depth-wise trajectories without widespread cross-depth re-dundancy. For DAR-SelfKV, the early alignment expands into a broad block across the middle layers. DAR-FixedKV produces repeated vertical bands: rather than aligning at cor-responding depths, a few stream-1 states align with stream-0 states across a wide range of layers. This many-to-one pat-tern suggests that fixed key–value roles make multiple depths reuse similar representations, reducing the depth-wise diver-sity available for historical retrieval. For mHC-2, many un-matched late-layer pairs have high CKA, forming a broad cross-depth region. This pattern is consistent with a lower effective depth, as representations from distinct late layers become less distinguishable. These CKA patterns charac-terize representation geometry but do not establish whether particular depths are frequently retrieved or whether either stream contributes to the model’s prediction.

In DAR-FixedKV, retaining the designated value stream 1 recovers the output, whereas retaining the key stream 0 alone often yields near-zero or negative rescue. This asymmetry is consistent with its routing rule: stream 1 carries the historical values available to subsequent retrieval, while stream 0 supplies only the keys that determine their weights. Retaining the value stream therefore preserves retrievable content; retaining the key stream alone preserves a selection signal whose corresponding values have been removed. This one-sided recoverability shows that DAR-FixedKV under-uses the two-stream capacity: one trajectory is confined to addressing and the other to retrievable content, rather than allowing both to contribute reciprocally as in DAR. Its higher validation loss than DAR indicates that this fixed division of roles is less effective than reciprocal routing. For mHC-2, both streams have positive rescue gains at late layers. Taken alone, this shows that each carries useful predictive infor-mation; combined with the broad high-CKA region across unmatched late depths, however, it indicates that much of this information is duplicated rather than specialized. Unlike DAR’s localized alignment and reciprocal use of both tra-jectories, mHC-2 spends its additional trajectory repeating similar late-depth computation, consistent with a lower ef-fective depth. Moreover, stream 1 has negative rescue gain at several middle layers, showing that this redundancy does not produce consistently useful cooperation across depth.

## Conclusion 

Dual Attention Residuals formulates historical residual re-trieval as a joint choice over depth and stream roles. It uses cross-stream keys to compute historical depth weights and applies them to self-stream values, without changing the Transformer branches. Across dense and sparse models, DAR improves validation loss. Ablations and analyses rule out an additional stream or value projection as the source of the gain and show that both trajectories remain useful without the redundancy or imbalance of alternative designs.
