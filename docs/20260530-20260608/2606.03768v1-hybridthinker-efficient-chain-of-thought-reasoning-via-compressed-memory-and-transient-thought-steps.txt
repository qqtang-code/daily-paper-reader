Abstract
Extended chain-of-thought (CoT) traces im-
prove LLM reasoning but incur substantial
computational and memory costs. While ex-
isting CoT compression methods mitigate this
by condensing thought steps into compact rep-
resentations via memory tokens and retaining
only these representations at inference time, the
loss of fine-grained information makes subse-
quent steps more error-prone. To alleviate this,
we propose HybridThinker, where in addi-
tion to preserved these representations, thought
steps are also temporarily retained to provide
fine-grained details.
However, we observe
that naively keeping thought steps accessible
to subsequent steps during training lets the
model bypass memory tokens by retrieving in-
formation directly from these steps, leaving the
model’s ability to compress and retrieve infor-
mation through memory tokens insufficiently
trained. We therefore introduce a hybrid train-
ing scheme, in which only some thought steps
are directly accessible through attention to sub-
sequent steps, while the other thought steps are
masked, forcing the model to use memory to-
kens for compression and retrieval. Across 4
reasoning benchmarks, HybridThinker matches
the uncompressed baseline, advancing the state
of the art in CoT compression by 5.8 points on
average accuracy with similar inference time.
Ablation studies confirm that both temporary
thought-step retention and the hybrid training
scheme contribute to these gains.

Introduction
Recent advances in large language model reason-
ing have increasingly relied on extended chain of
thoughts (CoTs), which have been shown to be
effective across arithmetic, common sense, and
knowledge-intensive tasks (Wei et al., 2023; Ko-
jima et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2025;
*Equal contribution.
†Corresponding author.
Yang et al., 2024). However, as illustrated in Fig-
ure 1(a), these extended CoT traces introduce sub-
stantial inference overhead, as the self-attention
computation scales quadratically and the KV cache
grows linearly with the sequence length, hindering
the broader deployment of reasoning models.
To alleviate this, two main approaches have been
proposed: KV cache pruning, and CoT compres-
sion. KV cache pruning reduces memory (Zhang
et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2025) usage by selec-
tively retaining important portions of the KV cache
while discarding less critical ones during inference,
but discarded information is irrecoverable without
full regeneration. CoT compression (Zhang et al.,
2025a; Kontonis et al., 2026; Liu et al., 2026b;
Zhu et al., 2026) condenses lengthy thought steps
into compact representations, after which the origi-
nal thought steps are discarded immediately as we
illustrate in Figure 1(b). Unfortunately, this prema-
ture discarding renders the immediately following
step more error-prone: key details such as exact
numerical values, strict constraints, and interme-
diate conclusions may be obscured or lost in the
compact representations (Zhang et al., 2025b; Shen
et al., 2026). In multi-step reasoning scenarios,
such single-step errors propagate along the chain
of thought in a cascading fashion.
To enable efficient reasoning in LLMs, we pro-
pose HybridThinker, a CoT compression method
that mitigates the loss of fine-grained local informa-
tion caused by the immediate discarding of thought
steps in existing CoT compression methods. Sim-
ilar to existing CoT compression approaches, Hy-
bridThinker compresses lengthy thought steps into
compact representations via memory tokens and re-
tains those representations after each thought step
is completed. However, the thought steps are not
immediately discarded in HybridThinker; instead,
they are temporarily retained and remain accessi-
ble to several subsequent steps. We illustrate the
HybridThinker inference in Figure 1(c).

To train HybridThinker, we observe that naively
applying the same attention pattern during training
as at inference is suboptimal (see Section 4.3 for de-
tails) due to shortcut learning: when both memory
tokens and thought steps are visible to subsequent
steps, the model over-relies on the more readable
plain-text thoughts, leaving its ability to compress
and retrieve information through memory tokens
undertrained. We therefore propose Hybrid Atten-
tion for training, combining two complementary
mechanisms: Bottleneck Attention, which hides
each thought step from subsequent steps, forcing
the model to access prior reasoning exclusively
through memory tokens; and Shortcut Attention,
which keeps each thought step visible to the im-
mediately following few steps before discarding it,
adapting the model to the inference-time setting.
Experiments demonstrate that HybridThinker
achieves an average accuracy improvement of 5.8
points over existing CoT compression methods,
matching the performance of the uncompressed
baseline. Ablation studies further confirm the ef-
fectiveness of Hybrid Attention as a training strat-
egy, showing that it outperforms training with pure
Bottleneck Attention or pure Shortcut Attention
alone. To summarize our contributions: (1) We
propose HybridThinker, in which thought steps are
temporarily retained and kept visible to several sub-
sequent steps alongside compact representations
during inference, alleviating the loss of fine-grained
information. (2) We propose Hybrid Attention for
training, which resolves the shortcut learning prob-
lem that arises in naive HybridThinker training.
(3) We demonstrate that HybridThinker achieves
substantial performance improvements over exist-
ing CoT compression methods, establishing a new
state-of-the-art performance in CoT compression.

Related Work
We organize related work into two categories: KV
cache pruning, which reduces memory by directly
discarding less important KV cache entries, and
compression techniques, which also discards the
original KV cache but first compresses the dis-
carded information into compact representations.
Compression techniques can be further divided into
prompt compression, a well-established research
area, and CoT compression, an emerging direction.
KV Cache Pruning.
KV cache pruning reduces
memory footprint by discarding less important KV
cache entries, with methods differing primarily in
2


how importance is estimated. H2O (Zhang et al.,
2023) uses accumulated attention weights as the
importance criterion. StreamingLLM (Xiao et al.,
2024) treats the first token and the most recent
tokens as important due to the attention sink phe-
nomenon. SepLLM (Chen et al., 2025) extends
StreamingLLM by additionally retaining delimiter
tokens, which implicitly compress contextual in-
formation. However, these methods face inherent
limitations: low cache budgets have been shown
to paradoxically lengthen CoT outputs (Liu et al.,
2025), and discarded cache becomes inaccessible.
Compression Techniques.
Existing prompt com-
pression methods (Chang et al., 2024; Li et al.,
2024a; Jiang et al., 2023; Mu et al., 2024; Ge et al.,
2024; Li et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024; Tang
et al., 2025, 2026a,b,c; Zhao et al., 2025, 2026a,b;
Liu et al., 2026a) focus on compressing model in-
puts, which are fully accessible ahead of time. CoT
reasoning, however, is generated incrementally and
cannot be accessed in its entirety beforehand, ne-
cessitating step-wise compression and rendering
most prompt compression techniques directly in-
applicable. To fill this gap, several dedicated CoT
compression methods have been proposed. Light-
Thinker (Zhang et al., 2025a) compresses each
completed thought step into the KV cache of a
small set of memory tokens and immediately dis-
cards the thought step’s KV cache, retaining only
the memory tokens. MEMENTO (Kontonis et al.,
2026) instead compresses thought steps into shorter
text sequences. MemoSight (Liu et al., 2026b) im-
proves upon LightThinker’s compression mecha-
nism and further incorporates multi-token predic-
tion. However, all of these CoT compression meth-
ods discard thought steps immediately upon com-
pression, losing fine-grained local information.

Method
In this section, we introduce HybridThinker. We
first define the key notation in Section 3.1. Then,
in Section 3.2, we describe the inference procedure
of HybridThinker from a KV cache management
perspective. In Section 3.3, we present the train-
ing scheme of HybridThinker, which addresses the
shortcut learning problem from directly applying
the inference-time attention pattern during training.
3.1
Notation
We introduce the key notation used in Section 3.2
and Section 3.3. We use t = 1, 2, . . . , k to index
thought steps. w is the temporary retention dura-
tion of a thought step. For example, w = 4 in
Figure 1(c) as S3 is accessible to S3, S4, S5, S6.
Input and Output.
The model receives a request
Q that requires CoT reasoning and generates a CoT
Y = S1, S2, . . . , Sk consisting of k thought steps.
The answer is contained in Y . Each thought step
St is a token sequence terminated by a delimiter.1
Memory Tokens.
HybridThinker maintains a
fixed sequence of L learnable special tokens M =
(m1, . . . , mL), called memory tokens. After gener-
ating St, the model performs a forward pass over
M to produce the KV cache ˜
Mt, which serves as
a compressed summary of St. A fresh copy of M
is used at each thought step to capture the thought
step semantics; we write Mt when we specifically
refer to the memory tokens inserted after St.
KV Cache Container.
We use Ct to denote the
KV cache container that the model attends to when
generating step St. We introduce three types of
KV cache notation used throughout: (1) ˜Q: the KV
cache of the question Q, computed once during pre-
fill and retained throughout. (2) ˜
Mt: the KV cache
of memory tokens Mt produced by compressing St.
(3) ˜St: the KV cache of the raw thought step St that
is retained temporarily through a few subsequent
thought steps in HybridThinker.

3.3
HybridThinker Training
We fine-tune an LLM with CoT data to obtain a
model capable of the inference procedure above.
Following other CoT compression methods, our
approach requires only two modifications over stan-
dard LLM training: reconstructing the training data
and adjusting the attention mask.
Data Reconstruction.
Each training instance is
a pair (Q, Y ). Following Zhang et al. (2025a), we
segment Y into k thought steps S1, . . . , Sk using
“\n\n” as the delimiter, then insert a memory token
sequence Mt after each intermediate step St (t <
k) to form the training sequence:
X = Q ⊕S1 ⊕M1 ⊕S2 ⊕M2 ⊕· · · ⊕Sk
= (x1, x2, . . . , x|X|),
(4)
where ⊕denotes concatenation and xu is the u-th
token of X. All M1, . . . , Mk−1 share the same
token content but occupy different positions.
Attention Mask Construction.
The most nat-
ural way to construct the training attention mask
is to replicate the inference-time attention pattern
directly. At inference time, information in each
thought step St reaches subsequent steps via two
pathways: (i) an compression pathway, in which
St passes coarse-grained information through the
memory tokens Mt; and (ii) a direct pathway, in
which St is directly attended to by the subsequent
w −1 steps. Directly replicating this pattern during
training yields what we call Shortcut Attention:

4
Experiments
4.1
Experimental Setup
Baselines.
We evaluate HybridThinker on two
backbone models, Qwen2.5-7B (Hui et al., 2024)
and Llama3.1-8B (Grattafiori et al., 2024), against
six representative baselines organized into three
groups by methodology. (1) CoT methods: the
standard instruction model prompted with CoT, and
the distilled reasoning model Distill-R1 (Guo et al.,
2025). (2) Trained reasoning models: Vanilla and
LightThinker (Zhang et al., 2025a), both initialized
from Distill-R1 and further instruction fine-tuned
on Bespoke-Stratos-17k under the same training
configuration as HybridThinker. (3) Training-free
acceleration techniques: H2O (Zhang et al., 2023)
and SepLLM (Chen et al., 2025) are two KV cache
pruning strategies applied directly on top of Vanilla.
Full configuration details for all baselines are pro-
vided in Appendix A.1.
Evaluation Metrics and Datasets.
We evalu-
ate HybridThinker on four reasoning benchmarks:
GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), MMLU (Hendrycks
et al., 2021), GPQA (Rein et al., 2023), and
BBH (Suzgun et al., 2022). These benchmarks
span mathematical reasoning, knowledge-intensive
question answering that demands extended reason-
ing chains, and multi-step complex reasoning, al-
lowing us to test the performance of compressed
reasoning under different reasoning demands. We
report accuracy (Acc) for task performance, total in-
ference time (Time), and peak token usage (Peak).
Together, these three metrics capture whether a
compression method can reduce deployment over-
head while preserving accuracy.
Implementation Details.
All trainable methods
are trained for 5 epochs with a batch size of 64
and a maximum training sequence length of 4096
tokens. For HybridThinker, the number of memory
tokens L = 9 (same as LightThinker), the tempo-
rary retention duration w = 4, and the number of
Shortcut Attention steps |I| = 2. The maximum
output length during evaluation is set to 10240 to-
kens. Further training details are provided in Ap-
pendix A.2.
4.2
Main Results
Table 1 shows our main results. On Qwen2.5-7B,
HybridThinker matches the accuracy of the uncom-
pressed baseline Vanilla (68.78%) while reducing
peak token usage by 61.7% and inference time by
20.3%. On Llama3.1-8B, HybridThinker (66.98%)
also approaches Vanilla (68.99%). Compared with
LightThinker, which discards thought steps imme-
diately after compression, HybridThinker achieves
a clear accuracy advantage across both models and
all four benchmarks, outperforming it by 5.8 points
on Qwen2.5-7B and 2.7 points on Llama3.1-8B
on average, at the modest cost of retaining only
about 100–150 more peak tokens, which trans-
lates to around 10% more peak token usage. No-
tably, this accuracy gain does not come at the ex-
pense of inference speed: HybridThinker gener-
ates shorter CoT sequences than LightThinker (see
Section 5.2), which offsets the overhead of the ad-
ditional retained tokens and yields comparable or
even slightly faster inference. HybridThinker also
outperforms KV cache pruning methods including
H2O and SepLLM with generally significant less
total inference time. These results demonstrate that
temporarily retaining thought steps to supply fine-
grained local information to several subsequent
steps effectively improves accuracy over existing
CoT compression, establishing HybridThinker as a
strong CoT compression approach.

show our results in Table 2.

5
Analysis
5.1
Hyperparameter Analysis
Effect of Memory Token Count (L).
As shown
in Figure 3(a), the number of memory tokens deter-
mines the compression capacity for each thought
step. Experiments show that average accuracy in-
creases from 60.86% to 68.78% as L grows from
1 to 9, reaching parity with the uncompressed base-
line Vanilla at L = 9, which indicates that 9 mem-
ory tokens is sufficient to carry the information
of one step. We remark that HybridThinker sur-
passes LightThinker accuracy with less peak token
usage at L = 3. Further increasing L leads to accu-
racy degradation while peak token usage continues
to rise, suggesting that additional memory tokens
introduce redundant representations and context
overhead rather than useful information.
Effect of Temporary Retention Duration (w).
As shown in Figure 3(b), starting from no thought
step retention (w = 1), a short retention duration
(w = 2) improves average accuracy but is still sub-
optimal to keep fine-grained information, yielding
only 64.45% accuracy. Extending the duration to
the default setting (w = 4) preserves sufficient fine-
grained information and raises accuracy to 68.78%.
Further prolonging the retention duration makes
such information arguably increasingly redundant,
even noisy, causing accuracy to drop to 67.14% and
65.55%, while peak token usage keeps growing.
Effect of the Number of Shortcut Attention
Steps (|I|).
As shown in Figure 3(c), |I| controls
how many thought steps per training instance fol-
low the Shortcut Attention pattern. When |I| = 0
(pure Bottleneck Attention training), average accu-
racy drops to 57.67%, reflecting the mismatch with
7


the shortcut inference pattern. Increasing |I| from
2 to 4 results in a slight decrease in average accu-
racy (68.78% vs. 68.18%), suggesting even a few
shortcut steps suffice for the model to adapt to the
Shortcut Attention pattern at inference time. Train-
ing with only Shortcut Attention (|I| = full) leads
to worse average accuracy of 66.87%, and also in-
curs significantly higher peak token usage. This is
because the memory tokens are under-trained for
compression and retrieval, causing the model to re-
peatedly re-read the question instead of continuing
reasoning, as detailed in Section 5.3.

6
Conclusion
We propose HybridThinker, which akin to CoT
compression condenses thought steps into memory
tokens but temporarily retains thought steps to sup-
ply fine-grained information together with memory
tokens for inference. We discover that while such
inference process is effective generally, training the
model that aligns training and inference is subopti-
mal due to shortcut learning. To address this, we
propose a hybrid training scheme mixing shortcut
and bottleneck learning in attention. Experiments
on 4 benchmarks show a 5.8 point average accu-
racy gain over existing CoT compression methods,
matching the uncompressed baseline while reduc-
ing peak token usage by 61.7% and inference time
by 20.3%. Compared to existing CoT compression
methods, HybridThinker achieves higher average
accuracy with similar inference time and slightly
more (∼10%) peak memory usage.
8


Limitations
Temporarily retaining thought steps slightly in-
creases peak KV cache usage compared to methods
that discard steps immediately. As shown in Fig-
ure 3(a), this can be effectively mitigated by reduc-
ing the number of memory tokens L: with L = 3,
HybridThinker already achieves lower peak usage
than LightThinker while maintaining a 2.5 point
accuracy advantage. Our approach also assumes
explicit step delimiters; extending it to support free-
form reasoning without clear boundaries is left for
future work.

A
Experimental Details
A.1
Baseline Details
During evaluation, we employ greedy decoding
with a maximum output length of 10,240 tokens
for all models. We compare HybridThinker against
the following baselines:
• CoT:
A
baseline
that
applies
few-shot
Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting to the
Qwen2.5-7B (Hui et al., 2024) and Llama-3.1-
8B (Grattafiori et al., 2024) models without
additional training.
• Distill-R1 (Guo et al., 2025):
A reasoning
model distilled from DeepSeek-R1’s response
data, built upon the Qwen and Llama architec-
tures.
• Vanilla: A standard full-parameter instruction-
tuned model. Operating without any compres-
sion or acceleration mechanisms, it serves as the
empirical upper bound for reasoning accuracy.
• H2O (Zhang et al., 2023): A training-free KV
cache eviction strategy that retains "Heavy Hit-
ter" tokens and a local window. We apply H2O
to the Vanilla model using a sliding window of
1024 and a heavy-hitter budget of 512 tokens.
• SepLLM (Chen et al., 2025): A training-free
framework that preserves KV caches for initial
tokens, separators, and a local window. We con-
figure SepLLM with an initial cache size of 384,
a separator budget of 64, and a local window of
256, maintaining a total cache capacity of 1024.
• LightThinker (Zhang et al., 2025a): A post-
training method that compresses each reasoning
step into a fixed number of memory tokens. Upon
reaching a step boundary, the model generates
memory tokens to summarize the context, after
which the original reasoning tokens are evicted
from the KV cache. The training data and most
hyperparameter settings follow those of Hybrid-
Thinker, with the exception that LightThinker
uses L = 9, w = 1, and |I| = 0, while all re-
maining hyperparameters are kept identical to
HybridThinker.
A.2
Training Details
The Vanilla baseline,
LightThinker and Hy-
bridThinker are initialized from DeepSeek-R1-
Distill (Guo et al., 2025) and trained on the
Bespoke-Stratos-17k (BS17K) dataset for 5 epochs.
Experiments are conducted on 8 H200 GPUs using
DeepSpeed ZeRO-3 offload. We use a micro-batch
size of 2 and 4 gradient accumulation steps, yield-
ing a global batch size of 64. We employ a cosine
learning rate schedule with a 0.05 warmup ratio.
The peak learning rate for Vanilla is set to 1e-5,
while for the other models, it is set to 2e-5. All
the CoT token length (including memory tokens)
has been set to 4096. For HybridThinker, we use 9
memory tokens, a retention horizon of w = 4, and
a shortcut set size of |I| = 2.
A.3
Evaluation Details
During inference, we evaluate all models using
greedy decoding with a repetition penalty of 1.1.
Prompt configurations for Table 1 are as follows:
Vanilla, H2O, SepLLM, LightThinker, and Hy-
bridThinker share the same system prompt (Fig-
ure 8) and task prompts (Figure 9). Distill-R1
uses these task prompts but omits the system
prompt. The CoT baseline uses a few-shot sys-
tem prompt (Figure 6) alongside task-specific
prompts for different benchmarks (Figure 7). For
MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and GPQA (Rein
et al., 2023), multiple-choice options are random-
ized to prevent positional bias.
B
Case Study
B.1
HybridThinker: Clear and Faithful
Reasoning
As shown in Case B.4, HybridThinker reasons in
a clear, linear fashion.
After framing the task
as a cost comparison (Steps 1–2), it computes
ticket cost at $4 per ride and Dasha’s total at $36
(Steps 3–6), then directly concludes $36 - $30
= $6 with a brief cross-verification (Steps 7–9).
The solution phase faithfully mirrors this deriva-
tion (Steps 11–13). Brief self-corrections appear
but are quickly resolved, leaving no redundant re-
derivation. This coherent reasoning is enabled by
HybridThinker’s temporary retention of thought
steps, which supplies subsequent steps with the
fine-grained local details needed for accurate re-
trieval and computation.
B.2
LightThinker: Retrieval Error from
Compressed Memory
As shown in Case B.5, LightThinker correctly iden-
tifies the per-ride cost as $4 during the thinking
stage (Steps 3–5), resolves the ambiguity of ride
splitting (Steps 8–10), and confidently concludes
11


$36 - $30 = $6 with repeated verification (Steps 11–
15). However, when transitioning to the solution
stage, the model erroneously retrieves the per-ride
cost as $8 from its compressed memory (Step 17),
computing 9 × $8 = $72 and ultimately outputting
$42 (Step 20), directly contradicting its own prior
reasoning. This is consistent with the known limita-
tion of existing CoT compression methods: imme-
diately discarding thought steps after compression
causes fine-grained details to be obscured or lost
in the compact representations, making subsequent
steps more error-prone.
B.3
HybridThinker (shortcut): Redundant
Reasoning Loop
As shown in Case B.6, this model arrives at the
correct answer but at significant token cost. After
correctly computing the $6 saving in Steps 1–5, the
model immediately derails into an extended loop
(Steps 6–25) over the irrelevant question of how
David and Dasha split the 9 rides—repeatedly re-
reading the problem, self-contradicting, and failing
to escape despite having already reached the cor-
rect conclusion. Throughout this loop, the token
"wait" appears dozens of times in rapid succession
as a reflexive filler that restarts rather than advances
reasoning (highlighted in the original trace). The
model only breaks free in Steps 26–30 by essen-
tially ignoring the self-imposed ambiguity and re-
confirming $36 - $30 = $6, followed by a clean
solution phase (Steps 32–35). This behavior is con-
sistent with the shortcut learning problem described
in our paper: when thought steps remain directly
accessible during training, the model over-relies on
plain-text thoughts rather than learning to compress
and retrieve information through memory tokens,
leaving the memory token mechanism undertrained
and ultimately degrading reasoning coherence.
